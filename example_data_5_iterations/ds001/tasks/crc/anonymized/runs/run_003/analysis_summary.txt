Analysis Summary: ds001_crc (n = 50,000 patients)
Outcome analyzed: pfs_months (mean 4.31, SD 2.29, range 0.00-16.25)

OVERVIEW

I conducted ten iterations of propose-test-refine analysis on this 124-feature CRC cohort. Across them I (1) screened all 80 binary, 7 ordinal, and 34 continuous features for univariate association with pfs_months, (2) built and refined multivariable models, (3) probed for demographic main effects and effect modification, (4) systematically tested interactions among the strongest binary signals, and (5) characterized the resulting treatment-by-biomarker pattern. The cohort's PFS variation is highly structured: a 12-term linear model explains R-squared = 0.91, with the remaining ~110 features adding essentially nothing (full kitchen-sink R-squared = 0.86 for the 11 main-effect features alone).


STRONGEST INDIVIDUAL PREDICTORS OF pfs_months

feature_078 (continuous, range 30-90, mean 65). The single most powerful predictor: Pearson r = +0.766, OLS slope +0.176 months per unit (p essentially 0). Mean PFS rises from 2.08 (lowest quartile) to 6.57 (highest). The slope is uniform across race strata (race x feature_078 F-test p = 0.71). Whatever this feature represents in clinical terms, it is by far the dominant axis of prognosis in this cohort.

feature_051 (binary, prevalence 55%). Largest binary effect: PFS 5.06 (=0) vs 3.70 (=1), difference -1.35 months (p < 1e-300). The effect is a near-constant additive shift -- it does not interact with feature_038 (-1.37 mo with f038=0 vs -1.30 mo with f038=1) and is uniform across race (-1.32 to -1.52 across the five categories). feature_051 behaves as a strong prognostic factor with no predictive component for the treatment-like signal.

feature_057 (3-level ordinal: 0/1/2 with prevalences 35/50/15%). Monotonic dose-response: 5.27 -> 4.05 -> 2.91, each level worsening PFS by ~1.18 months. Effect is uniform across race strata. The pattern is consistent with a clinical staging or performance-status-like variable.

feature_092 (continuous, range 1.5-5.5, mean 3.8). OLS slope = +0.476 mo/unit, p < 1e-300. Decile means rise monotonically from 3.91 to 4.67. Narrow range, monotonic positive effect -- consistent with a continuous favorable laboratory marker.

feature_099 (continuous, range 0-25). OLS slope = -0.077 mo/unit, p = 2e-167. Quartile means: 4.57, 4.36, 4.18, 3.87 -- monotonic negative.

feature_009 (continuous, long-tail, range 0.02-777, median 4.4). r = -0.038 raw, r = -0.062 on log1p scale (p = 5e-44). Log transformation tightens the linear fit, consistent with a tumor-marker-style distribution. Quintile means decline 4.50 -> 4.14.

feature_006 (continuous, range 48-830, mean 224). Small but multivariable-significant (-0.0005 mo/unit, t = -13.7).


THE TREATMENT-BY-BIOMARKER PATTERN (THE CENTRAL FINDING)

The most clinically interesting pattern in this dataset is the interaction between feature_038 (binary, prevalence 20%) and the three binary features feature_013 (prev 42%), feature_043 (prev 35%), and feature_109 (prev 5%).

Marginally, feature_038 looks like a positive prognostic factor: PFS 4.12 (=0) vs 5.09 (=1), difference +0.97 mo. But this average obscures a striking pattern of effect modification. All three of feature_013, feature_043, feature_109 individually abolish feature_038's benefit:
  feature_038 x feature_013 interaction coef = -1.629 mo (p = 8e-226).
    f038=1/f013=0: 5.77; f038=1/f013=1: 4.14 -- benefit of +1.66 collapses to +0.03.
  feature_038 x feature_043 interaction = -1.454 (p = 4e-168).
  feature_038 x feature_109 interaction = -1.183 (p = 6e-23).

When all three are simultaneously absent (define any_resistance = f013 OR f043 OR f109; prevalence 65%), the picture sharpens:
  any_resistance = 0 (n = 17,372, 35% of cohort): feature_038 effect = +2.758 mo (p < 1e-300).
  any_resistance = 1 (n = 32,628): feature_038 effect = +0.030 mo (p = 0.29).

In the doubly-negative subgroup (f013 = 0 AND f043 = 0; n = 18,853): f038 effect = +2.520 mo, p < 1e-300. In any cell where at least one marker is positive, the treatment effect is statistically null (effect estimates 0.04-0.07 mo, all p > 0.15).

In the final 12-term OLS model -- which adds the feature_038 x (1 - any_resistance) interaction term to the main-effect block -- the main effects of feature_038 and of all three resistance markers collapse to zero (each p > 0.7), and the interaction coefficient is +2.74 mo (t = 178, p < 1e-300). This is the canonical signature of a predictive (rather than prognostic) biomarker pattern: feature_013, feature_043, and feature_109 do not change PFS in untreated patients; they only matter as effect modifiers that abolish the benefit of feature_038.

Importantly, within the biomarker-eligible subgroup, the feature_038 benefit is constant across other axes:
  by feature_057 (PS-like) levels 0/1/2: +2.66 / +2.77 / +2.74 mo (interaction p = 0.39).
  by feature_078: interaction coef +0.003 mo per unit, p = 0.29.

So the treatment benefit is approximately a homogeneous +2.7 mo additive shift in the eligible subgroup, regardless of age-like, performance-status-like, or other prognostic features.


DEMOGRAPHIC AND DISPARITY ANALYSIS (NULL FINDINGS)

I ran nine demographic-related tests across iterations 4 and 8. Every one is null:

1. PFS by feature_064 (race: white, hispanic, black, asian, other): one-way ANOVA F = 0.872, p = 0.480. Group means span only 4.27-4.41.
2. PFS by feature_018 (insurance: medicare, private, medicaid, uninsured): F = 1.475, p = 0.219. Group means span 4.25-4.33.
3. After adjustment for top biological predictors, race coefficients all |t| < 0.7, insurance coefficients all |t| < 1.3.
4. Race x feature_051 (largest binary effect) joint F-test: F = 0.43, p = 0.79. Stratum-specific f051 effects -1.32 to -1.52, indistinguishable.
5. Race x feature_078 joint F-test: F = 0.54, p = 0.71. The dominant +0.176 mo/unit slope is identical across race.
6. Treatment receipt (feature_038) by race: 19.2-21.1%, chi-square p = 0.55.
7. Treatment receipt by insurance: 19.0-20.2%, chi-square p = 0.59.
8. Among biomarker-eligible patients, treatment receipt still does not differ by race (p = 0.60) or insurance (p = 0.83).
9. Resistance-marker prevalences are essentially identical across races (any_resistance: 62.8-65.8%; feature_013: 41-43%; feature_043: 34-36%; feature_109: 4.3-5.2%).
10. Treatment efficacy in the eligible subgroup, by race: white +2.63, hispanic +3.21, asian +2.88, black +2.79, other +3.07; by insurance: medicare +2.80, private +2.60, medicaid +3.09, uninsured +2.75. Stratum estimates overlap and the average benefit is +2.7-3.2 mo everywhere.

In short: no PFS gap by race or insurance; no disparity in treatment access; no disparity in resistance-marker prevalence; no disparity in treatment efficacy. This is a notable null and worth flagging explicitly because it cannot be assumed of an EHR-derived oncology cohort.


HYPOTHESES SUPPORTED VS REFUTED

Supported:
- All six main-effect hypotheses in iteration 2 (h2_f078_strong, h2_f051_neg, h2_f038_pos, h2_f057_dose, h2_f092_pos, h2_f099_neg).
- All independence/explanatory-power hypotheses in iteration 3.
- All four interaction hypotheses in iteration 5 (h5_int_38_13, h5_int_38_43, h5_int_38_109, h5_predictive_pattern).
- The doubly-negative refinement (h6_double_neg) and the prognostic-only hypothesis for feature_051 (h6_f51_prognostic).
- The full interaction model in iteration 7 (h7_full_int_model).
- The log-scale hypothesis for feature_009 (h9_f009_log).
- The 12-term final model (h10_final_model, R-squared = 0.91).
- The aggregate null-disparity claim (h10_no_demographic_signal).

Refuted:
- All four demographic main-effect/interaction hypotheses in iteration 4.
- All four disparity-of-access hypotheses in iteration 8.
- The treatment x performance-status (h7_treat_x_PS) and treatment x age (h7_treat_x_age) effect-modification hypotheses -- the +2.7 mo treatment benefit is uniform across these axes.
- The hypothesis of additional low-prevalence treatment-like signals (h6_no_lowprev_treatment).
- The hypothesis of meaningful residual binary signal after adjustment (h9_residual_binary).


OVERALL CONCLUSIONS

1. The PFS distribution in this 50,000-patient CRC cohort is dominated by a small number of features. A 12-term OLS model captures R-squared = 0.91; the remaining ~110 features collectively add < 0.005 to R-squared.

2. Among these dominant features, the most clinically meaningful pattern is a treatment-by-biomarker interaction. feature_038 (prevalence 20%) confers a +2.74 mo PFS benefit, but only in the ~35% of patients who are negative for all three of feature_013, feature_043, and feature_109. In the marker-positive 65%, the treatment effect is statistically zero. This is the canonical signature of a predictive (not prognostic) biomarker structure -- the resistance markers exist only as effect modifiers.

3. Other features behave as classical prognostic variables: feature_078 (very strong positive, +0.18 mo/unit), feature_057 (-1.17 mo per ordinal level), feature_051 (-1.36 mo), feature_092 (+0.48 mo/unit), feature_099 (-0.08 mo/unit), feature_009 (log-scale negative), feature_006 (small negative). None of these modify the feature_038 treatment effect; they shift baseline prognosis additively in eligible and ineligible patients alike.

4. Across all nine demographic tests (race and insurance as main effects, as effect modifiers of biological predictors, as predictors of treatment receipt, as predictors of marker prevalence, and as effect modifiers of treatment efficacy), this cohort shows no meaningful disparities. Mean PFS, marker prevalence, treatment receipt, and treatment efficacy are statistically indistinguishable across race and insurance categories.
