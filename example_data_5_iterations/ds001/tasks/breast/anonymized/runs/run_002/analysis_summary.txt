DATASET ds001_breast — ANALYSIS SUMMARY
=========================================

Cohort: 50,000 patients with breast cancer drawn from a vendor-aggregated EHR
dataset, de-identified to opaque feature labels. Outcome: pfs_months
(progression-free survival in months; continuous, mean 4.69, SD 2.50, range 0
–16.66, no censoring indicator provided). Predictor set: 127 features (82
binary, 7 multi-level ordinal/integer, 36 continuous, 2 categorical strings:
feature_011 = race, feature_089 = insurance). Analyses below were run over 10
iterations of propose-test-refine.


ITERATION 1 — UNIVARIATE BINARY SCREEN
--------------------------------------
A Welch t-test for every binary feature against pfs_months identified seven
features with very strong main effects (|mean diff| ≥ 0.36 mo, p ≤ 1e-58):

  feature_056   present in 30%   mean diff −1.54 mo   p ≈ 0
  feature_042   present in 35%   mean diff +1.10 mo   p ≈ 0
  feature_048   present in 10%   mean diff −1.05 mo   p = 1.5e-178
  feature_015   present in 35%   mean diff −0.56 mo   p = 6.1e-144
  feature_111   present in 70%   mean diff +0.57 mo   p = 3.7e-143
  feature_040   present in 18%   mean diff −0.46 mo   p = 6.1e-70
  feature_039   present in 64%   mean diff +0.36 mo   p = 5.8e-59

Several other binaries (feature_034, feature_077, feature_086, feature_055,
feature_005, feature_031) reached nominal significance with very small effects
(|diff| < 0.15 mo); these were treated as candidates for confounding.


ITERATION 2 — CONTINUOUS / ORDINAL SCREEN
-----------------------------------------
- feature_080 (range 30–90, mean 65, plausibly age) showed by far the
  strongest single association with pfs_months: Pearson r = +0.702 (p ≈ 0).
  Mean PFS rises monotonically from 1.70 mo in the lowest decile of
  feature_080 to 7.84 mo in the highest. The relationship is essentially
  linear (linear R² = 0.492; quadratic adds only +0.001).
- feature_063 (3-level ordinal: 0/1/2 with counts 17 592 / 24 971 / 7 437)
  showed a clean monotonic decline in PFS: 5.64 → 4.44 → 3.29 mo
  (ANOVA F ≈ 2860, p ≈ 0). Behaves like a stage-/grade-/ECOG-style ordinal.
- Smaller but highly significant continuous correlations: feature_067
  r = −0.116 (p = 4e-148), feature_019 r = +0.100 (p = 9e-112), feature_101
  r = −0.091 (p = 3e-93).
- The other multi-level ordinals (feature_021, _024, _127, _001, _073,
  _016) had no detectable association with PFS (ANOVA p ≥ 0.27 for all).


ITERATION 3 — RACE & INSURANCE
------------------------------
Pre-specified disparity hypotheses were tested and refuted:

  feature_011 (race, 5 categories): means 4.679 (white) / 4.707 (hispanic) /
    4.717 (black) / 4.735 (asian) / 4.762 (other). One-way ANOVA F = 0.85,
    p = 0.49. Pairwise Welch t-tests vs white: |diff| ≤ 0.082 mo, all p > 0.23.

  feature_089 (insurance, 4 categories): means 4.685 (private) / 4.692
    (medicare) / 4.706 (medicaid) / 4.733 (uninsured). ANOVA F = 0.31,
    p = 0.82.

There is no marginal racial or insurance disparity in pfs_months in this
cohort.


ITERATION 4 — MULTIVARIABLE BACKBONE
------------------------------------
An OLS regression of pfs_months on the 12 strongest predictors (feature_080,
C(feature_063), feature_056, feature_042, feature_048, feature_015,
feature_111, feature_040, feature_039, feature_067, feature_019, feature_101)
yielded R² = 0.7919 (adjusted R² 0.7919) on 50 000 observations. All
coefficients except feature_039 retained large t-statistics (|t| ≥ 35); the
unadjusted +0.36 mo association of feature_039 dropped to +0.014 (p = 0.32),
consistent with confounding by correlated predictors. The small signals
identified in iteration 1 (feature_034, _086, _055, _005, _031, _077) were
not retained — their marginal associations were confounded.

Effect of feature_080 was unchanged after adjusting for feature_063 (slope
0.176 → 0.176), confirming additive independent contributions from these two
strongest prognostic factors.


ITERATION 5 — PAIRWISE INTERACTIONS
-----------------------------------
Tested every plausible pairwise interaction among the top main effects.
Two stood out as massive:

  feature_111 × feature_042   coef = +1.559   p < 1e-300   ΔR² = +0.019
  feature_015 × feature_042   coef = −1.665   p < 1e-300   ΔR² = +0.023

Smaller but significant:

  feature_063 × feature_080   p = 1.6e-18     ΔR² = +0.0003
    (feature_080 slope shallower at higher feature_063 levels)
  feature_056 × feature_080   p = 2.0e-21     ΔR² = +0.0004
    (gap between feature_056 strata widens at high feature_080)

The dominant interaction structure pointed to a treatment-by-biomarker
mechanism centred on feature_042.


ITERATION 6 — DEMOGRAPHIC HETEROGENEITY
---------------------------------------
Within-race and within-insurance Welch t-tests for feature_042 yielded
mean differences of:

  white     +1.107 mo   p = 2e-264
  hispanic  +1.115 mo   p = 6e-67
  black     +1.060 mo   p = 3e-45
  asian     +1.062 mo   p = 3e-23
  other     +1.007 mo   p = 4e-8

  medicare  +1.110 mo
  private   +1.091 mo
  medicaid  +1.081 mo
  uninsured +1.097 mo

Differences across strata are within ~0.1 mo. No racial or insurance-related
treatment-effect heterogeneity. Treatment-access checks (prevalence of
feature_042 = 1 by race / insurance) showed 33.7–35.2% across all strata —
no exposure disparity. Prevalence of the favorable biomarker pattern
(feature_111 = 1 AND feature_015 = 0) was 43.4–46.4% across races — no
biomarker-distribution disparity.


ITERATION 7 — TREATMENT × BIOMARKER × RESISTANCE (3-WAY)
---------------------------------------------------------
Stratifying by the four cells of (feature_111, feature_015) revealed a
striking pattern of effect concentration:

  (f111=0, f015=0): feature_042 mean diff = −0.05 mo (p = 0.28)   — null
  (f111=0, f015=1): feature_042 mean diff = +0.04 mo (p = 0.52)   — null
  (f111=1, f015=0): feature_042 mean diff = +2.38 mo (p ≈ 0)      — large +
  (f111=1, f015=1): feature_042 mean diff = +0.03 mo (p = 0.39)   — null

The saturated 2×2×2 model produced:
  feature_042                              −0.048 (n.s.)
  feature_042 × feature_111                +2.424 (p < 1e-300)
  feature_042 × feature_111 × feature_015  −2.428 (p = 1.6e-128)

i.e., the f042:f111 synergy is exactly cancelled when feature_015 = 1. The
F-test for the three-way term gave F = 585.1, p = 1.6e-128.

Within the n = 22 788 responder subgroup (feature_111 = 1, feature_015 = 0),
feature_042 retained an adjusted +2.39 mo effect after controlling for
feature_080, feature_063, feature_056, feature_048, feature_067, and
feature_019 (R² = 0.722). This is the cleanest signal in the dataset.

Functional reading: feature_042 behaves like a TREATMENT with a predictive
biomarker profile (positive marker feature_111 AND absent resistance marker
feature_015). In all other patients it is essentially inert.


ITERATION 8 — EFFECT-MODIFIER REFINEMENTS
-----------------------------------------
Within the responder subgroup, the feature_042 mean difference was:
  feature_080 low tertile   +2.43 mo
  feature_080 mid tertile   +2.26 mo
  feature_080 high tertile  +2.40 mo
The treatment effect is uniform across age. The relationship of feature_080
to PFS is approximately linear (cubic adds <0.001 to R²). The interaction
between feature_063 and feature_056 is essentially additive (interaction
coefficients +0.09 / +0.12, p = 0.05–0.08).


ITERATION 9 — DOSE-RESPONSE & BEST-PROGNOSIS PROFILE
----------------------------------------------------
The feature_063 ordinal preserves rank ordering 0 > 1 > 2 within both
strata of feature_056:
  feature_056 = 0:   6.12  /  4.89  /  3.74
  feature_056 = 1:   4.52  /  3.39  /  2.26
Effects are additive on the original scale.

Combining the favorable factors yields the model-predicted "best prognosis"
profile: feature_063 = 0, feature_056 = 0, feature_048 = 0, feature_111 = 1,
feature_015 = 0, feature_042 = 1, high feature_080. A patient at feature_080
= 80 with this binary profile has model-predicted pfs_months ≈ 9–10 mo
versus the cohort mean of 4.69 mo.


ITERATION 10 — SYNTHESIS
------------------------
Confirmed:
  • Strong main effects (in adjusted model): feature_080, feature_063,
    feature_056, feature_042, feature_048, feature_015, feature_111,
    feature_040, feature_067, feature_019, feature_101.
  • One major three-way interaction: feature_042 helps only when
    feature_111 = 1 AND feature_015 = 0.
  • Several modest two-way interactions involving feature_080
    (feature_063 × feature_080, feature_056 × feature_080).
  • Multivariable model with main effects + the treatment-by-biomarker
    interaction reaches R² ≈ 0.81.

Refuted:
  • Race-based disparity in PFS or treatment effect (feature_011).
  • Insurance-based disparity in PFS or treatment effect (feature_089).
  • Racial differences in feature_042 exposure or biomarker prevalence.
  • Independent contribution of feature_039 (confounded).
  • Independent contribution of the small marginal binary signals
    (feature_034, _086, _055, _005, _031, _077).
  • Substantive nonlinearity in the feature_080–PFS relationship.
  • Multiplicative (rather than additive) feature_063 × feature_056
    interaction.

Overall conclusion. The dataset's PFS landscape is dominated by (a) a strong
linear effect of feature_080 (which behaves like patient age in this
synthetic cohort, but is positively rather than negatively associated with
PFS), (b) a clean monotone ordinal effect of feature_063, (c) several
binary prognostic markers that are largely additive, and (d) a single
predictive treatment–biomarker structure: feature_042 confers ~+2.4 mo PFS
benefit only in patients carrying feature_111 = 1 and lacking
feature_015 = 1, with no benefit in any other subgroup. Race and insurance
contribute neither to baseline outcome nor to treatment-effect heterogeneity
in this cohort, so any disparity narrative would have to draw on factors
not captured here.
