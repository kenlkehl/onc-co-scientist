Analysis Summary — ds001_aml (n = 50,000; objective_response prevalence 16.9%)
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
The dataset contains 124 de-identified patient features and a binary
objective_response outcome (8,452 responders out of 50,000 patients, 16.9%).
Features are a mix of binary indicators (81), three categorical variables
(race feature_005; insurance feature_087; a 3-level ordinal feature_057),
five 5-level ordinals (feature_096, feature_064, feature_045, feature_042,
feature_125), and 36 continuous variables. We ran 10 iterations of propose →
test → refine, focusing on identifying main predictors, treatment-like
effects, prognostic biomarkers, dose-response gradients, interactions, and
demographic disparities.

ITERATION 1 — Univariate screen
-------------------------------
Strong main-effect signals emerged across modality types:
  * feature_057 (3-level ordinal): a clear monotonic gradient — response
    21.3%, 15.5%, 11.2% across levels 0, 1, 2 (chi^2 p ≈ 5e-98). This is by
    far the largest single signal in the dataset and behaves like a
    risk/stage stratifier.
  * feature_035 (binary, 7.2% prevalent): response 22.1% vs 16.5% (z-test
    p ≈ 1e-17, +5.6 pp absolute). Plausibly a treatment indicator.
  * feature_011 (continuous, 0–23.6, mean 3.85, ~28% zero): mean 3.38 in
    responders vs 3.95 in non-responders (Welch t-test p ≈ 7e-39).
  * feature_006 (continuous, 20–100, mean 60): mean 58.2 vs 60.3 (p ≈ 1e-19).
  * feature_005 (race): chi^2 p ≈ 0.010, range 14.7% (other) to 17.3% (white).
  * feature_087 (insurance): chi^2 p = 0.54 — no association.

ITERATION 2 — Multivariable logistic regression (147-column design)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Mutual adjustment in a logistic model with all features (one-hot encoding
for race, insurance, and the multi-level ordinals) confirmed the main
predictors as independent and identified additional weaker contributors:
  * feature_057: level 1 OR e^-0.39 ≈ 0.68; level 2 OR ≈ 0.46 (p ~1e-51 / 1e-77).
  * feature_035: coef +0.36 (OR ~1.43; p ≈ 3e-17).
  * feature_011 (per SD): coef -0.157 (p ≈ 7e-35).
  * feature_006 (per SD): coef -0.112 (p ≈ 3e-20).
  * feature_099 (per SD): coef +0.059 (p ≈ 1e-6) — small positive.
  * feature_063 (per SD): coef -0.066 (p ≈ 2e-6) — small negative.
  * feature_092 (per SD): coef -0.058 (p ≈ 1e-5) — small negative.
  * feature_093 (binary, 8% prevalent): coef +0.13 (p = 0.002) — second
    candidate "treatment" effect.
Pseudo-R^2 of the consolidated model (12 predictors) was 0.019 — the data
are noisy and most variation in objective_response is unexplained, but a
small set of effects are highly stable.

ITERATION 3 — feature_035 effect across feature_057 strata
----------------------------------------------------------
feature_035 raised response in every feature_057 stratum (absolute
differences +6.15, +5.35, +4.43 pp from level 0→2). The multiplicative
interaction was non-significant (interaction coefs +0.028, +0.053; p ≈ 0.71).
Conclusion: the benefit of feature_035 is qualitatively consistent across
prognostic strata, with absolute differences narrowing in the worst-prognosis
stratum (a typical pattern of constant log-odds effect when baseline risk
is lower).

ITERATION 4 — feature_035 modification by continuous biomarkers
---------------------------------------------------------------
Tested feature_035 × feature_011 and feature_035 × feature_006 interactions:
  * feature_035 × feature_011: interaction coef -0.054 (p = 0.225). Tertile
    diffs: low +6.12 pp, mid +6.96 pp, high +3.54 pp. Descriptive
    attenuation of benefit at high feature_011 but not formally significant.
  * feature_035 × feature_006: coef +0.036 (p = 0.39); no modification.
  * feature_011 × feature_006: coef +0.021 (p = 0.093); marginal but not
    significant — the two biomarkers behave as independent prognostic axes.

ITERATION 5 — Demographic and age disparities
---------------------------------------------
Adjusted models (with feature_057, feature_035, feature_011, feature_006)
showed:
  * Race (feature_005): no significant adjusted differences in
    objective_response. Coefficients for black -0.029 (p=0.64), hispanic
    +0.041 (p=0.49), other -0.116 (p=0.26), white +0.078 (p=0.13).
  * Insurance (feature_087): all coefficients near zero, all p > 0.5.
  * Age (feature_078): no association at all — response rates by quartile
    16.7%, 17.4%, 16.6%, 16.9%; adjusted coef +0.004 per SD (p=0.75).
The small unadjusted race effect (chi^2 p = 0.010) is fully explained by
other features in the dataset.

ITERATION 6 — Consolidated model and ordinal trends
---------------------------------------------------
A 12-term consolidated logistic model retained all key predictors with
stable coefficients (pseudo-R^2 = 0.019). Two more weak signals confirmed:
  * feature_125 (5-level ordinal): linear trend coef -0.023 per level
    (p = 0.038); response rates 17.4, 16.4, 16.7, 16.8, 15.7%.
  * feature_014 (binary, 40% prevalent): coef +0.059 (p = 0.016) — small
    positive.
  * feature_121 (binary, 8% prevalent): coef -0.088 (p = 0.049) — small
    negative.

ITERATION 7 — Low-prevalence binary screen + remaining ordinals
---------------------------------------------------------------
Among 51 binary features with prevalence 0.5–20%, after adjustment for
feature_057, feature_035, feature_093, feature_011, and feature_006, none
beyond feature_121 reached p < 0.05; feature_111 was borderline
(p = 0.051). No additional strong "treatment" candidates exist in this
prevalence range.
For the remaining multi-level ordinals (feature_096, feature_064,
feature_045, feature_042) chi^2 tests against response were all p > 0.09 —
they carry no detectable signal.

ITERATION 8 — Joint and modifier analyses for feature_093
---------------------------------------------------------
The two "treatment-like" binaries combine approximately additively:
joint response rates were 16.4% (neither), 17.9% (feature_093 only),
21.8% (feature_035 only), 25.8% (both). Interaction coef +0.110 on the
log-odds scale, p = 0.452. Patients with both flags have the highest
observed response.
feature_093 × feature_057 interaction: descriptive stratum effects +1.8,
+2.6, -0.7 pp; interaction with the highest-risk stratum coef -0.179
(p = 0.235). Suggestive attenuation of feature_093 benefit in the
highest-risk stratum but not statistically significant.

ITERATION 9 — Effect modification of feature_035 by demographics
----------------------------------------------------------------
Formal LR tests for treatment-effect heterogeneity:
  * race × feature_035: LR = 2.06 on 4 df, p = 0.726. Stratum-specific
    absolute differences: white +6.5 pp, asian +4.0 pp, black +4.1 pp,
    hispanic +4.1 pp, other +5.4 pp.
  * insurance × feature_035: LR = 6.46 on 3 df, p = 0.091. Differences:
    medicaid +7.2 pp, medicare +7.4 pp, private +3.5 pp, uninsured +5.8 pp.
    Suggestive but not significant — the smaller benefit observed in
    privately insured patients could be chance.

ITERATION 10 — Marginal effect quantification and prognostic independence
-------------------------------------------------------------------------
Marginal standardization (g-computation) on the adjusted model:
  * feature_035: adjusted marginal probability rises from 16.5% (off) to
    21.9% (on); average risk difference +5.4 pp.
  * feature_057: adjusted marginal probability falls from 21.2% (level 0)
    to 11.2% (level 2); average risk difference -10.0 pp.
The continuous biomarkers feature_011 and feature_006 have means that are
essentially identical across feature_057 strata (feature_011: 3.80, 3.90,
3.84; feature_006: 59.85, 60.07, 59.83), confirming that they are
independent prognostic axes rather than surrogates for the feature_057
stratifier.

OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
-------------------
Hypotheses supported by the data:
  * feature_057 is the dominant prognostic stratifier — large monotonic
    gradient in response, ~10 pp swing between extreme levels.
  * feature_035 behaves as a robust positive intervention — adjusted
    +5 pp benefit; effect direction preserved in every subgroup tested
    (every feature_057 stratum, every race, every insurance category, and
    across feature_011/feature_006 distributions).
  * feature_093 is a weaker but real second positive factor (+1.7 pp
    absolute, p = 0.002 adjusted), additive with feature_035.
  * feature_011 and feature_006 are independent continuous prognostic
    biomarkers that lower response monotonically.
  * Three smaller adjusted signals confirmed: feature_099 (+), feature_063
    (-), feature_092 (-), plus weak feature_014 (+), feature_121 (-),
    feature_125 (- linear trend).

Hypotheses refuted by the data:
  * Race (feature_005) is NOT a meaningful disparity once prognostic
    factors are adjusted for; nor does it modify the feature_035 effect.
  * Insurance (feature_087) shows no main effect on response and no
    significant treatment-effect modification.
  * Age (feature_078) has essentially no relationship to objective_response.
  * No qualitative interaction of feature_035 with feature_057, feature_011,
    or feature_006 — treatment effect is broadly applicable.

Caveats: pseudo-R^2 of even the best model is only ~0.02, so the dataset
contains substantial unexplained variation in objective_response. The very
large n (50,000) means small effects show high statistical significance;
clinical interpretation should weigh effect sizes alongside p-values.
