Analysis summary - ds001_aml (50,000 AML patients; outcome objective_response, marginal rate 16.9%)

Approach
--------
Across 10 iterations I proposed natural-language hypotheses about features, treatments, and subgroup interactions, then tested each with chi-square or logistic regression on the full 50,000-patient cohort. Effect estimates are reported on the natural scale of each test (risk difference, log-odds, or coefficient per unit of a continuous predictor) and significance is judged at p<0.05 (or Bonferroni for multi-testing screens).

Iteration 1 - AML mutation main effects
Of the eight AML disease-biology features tested, only IDH1 mutation showed a clear main effect: response 22.1% in IDH1+ vs 16.5% in wild-type, +5.6 pp, p=3.5e-18. NPM1 was directionally favorable (+0.6 pp) but not significant (p=0.11). TP53, complex karyotype, FLT3-ITD, FLT3-TKD, IDH2, secondary AML, and "unfit_for_intensive" all showed essentially zero unadjusted main effect on response - a striking finding given that several of these (TP53, complex karyotype) are textbook poor-prognosis markers in real-world AML.

Iteration 2 - Treatment main effects (univariable)
Among six AML treatments, only venetoclax+azacitidine reached p<0.05 (log-OR +0.053, RD +0.74 pp, p=0.031). Midostaurin, ivosidenib, and 7+3 were null. Gilteritinib (RD -0.71 pp, p=0.13) and enasidenib (RD -0.86 pp, p=0.17) were directionally NEGATIVE on the population marginal scale, contrary to expectation.

Iteration 3 - FLT3-inhibitor x FLT3-mutation interactions
None of the four interaction tests was significant; three of four were directionally OPPOSITE the canonical biomarker-matched pattern. In FLT3-ITD+/midostaurin+ patients (n=1468), response was 16.1% vs 17.4% in FLT3-ITD+ patients not on midostaurin. In FLT3-ITD+/gilteritinib+ patients (n=1491), response was 16.9% vs 17.2% off-treatment. In this dataset the FLT3 inhibitors do NOT show enriched activity in FLT3-mutated AML.

Iteration 4 - IDH-inhibitor x IDH-mutation interactions
The most striking negative result of the entire analysis: ivosidenib x IDH1 interaction log-OR = -0.36, p=0.027, with the wrong sign relative to the FDA-label expectation. In IDH1-mutant patients, ivosidenib-treated patients responded at 17.4% vs 22.5% for those not on ivosidenib (i.e., the IDH1 main effect is essentially erased, and arguably reversed, by ivosidenib). Enasidenib x IDH2 interaction was numerically negative (log-OR -0.12, p=0.45). The two off-target combinations (ivosidenib x IDH2, enasidenib x IDH1) were null as expected.

Iteration 5 - Treatment x fitness interactions
Ven/aza x unfit_for_intensive interaction was positive and significant (log-OR +0.10, p=0.036): RR difference for ven/aza was +1.6 pp in unfit patients vs +0.2 pp in fit patients. This is the cleanest "right-direction" pattern in the dataset. The 7+3 x unfit interaction was directionally negative (intensive chemo doing worse in unfit patients) but not significant (p=0.50). Stratifying ven/aza by age band, the absolute benefit was largest in the 60-74 stratum (+1.2 pp), not in the >=75 stratum (+0.2 pp), so the "elderly benefit" sub-hypothesis was not supported.

Iteration 6 - Performance status, age, and adjusted mutation effects
ECOG performance status emerged as the dominant clinical predictor: log-OR -0.38 per unit (OR 0.68), p<1e-95. Age, in contrast, had essentially no independent effect (coefficient +0.0004 per year, p=0.73) once ECOG was included - all of the apparent "older = worse" signal in AML is captured by performance status, not chronological age. After adjustment, NPM1 retained its positive direction (+0.043) but lost statistical significance (p=0.10). TP53 and complex karyotype remained null even after adjustment.

Iteration 7 - Sociodemographic disparities
Black patients had a lower response rate than white patients: 15.7% vs 17.3%, log-OR -0.11, p=0.004 (absolute disparity ~1.5 pp). Hispanic and Asian patients trended lower but did not reach significance. Insurance type (medicare/medicaid/uninsured vs private) and rural residence showed no association with response - any care-access proxies that one might worry about did NOT track with this outcome, but self-identified Black race did.

Iteration 8 - Laboratory and inflammatory markers
The clearest single signals after ECOG were:
  - weight_loss_pct_6mo: log-OR -0.040 per percentage point, p=7e-35 (strongest single lab predictor; stratified RRs 18.6% / 17.9% / 15.1% / 11.9% across <=0 / 0-5 / 5-10 / >10 % weight loss)
  - blast_pct_marrow: log-OR -0.0057 per percent, p=1.2e-19
  - albumin_g_dl: log-OR +0.115 per g/dL, p=1.4e-6
  - crp_mg_l: log-OR -0.0070 per mg/L, p=2.8e-6
  - wbc_k_per_ul: log-OR -0.0022 per K/uL, p=1.9e-5
LDH, NLR, hemoglobin, and platelet count were null. The pattern suggests a coherent "frailty/inflammation/disease-burden" axis (low albumin, high CRP, weight loss, high marrow blasts) is the strongest prognostic structure in the cohort.

Iteration 9 - Pharmacogenomic SNPs
Of 24 candidate SNPs, only two reached nominal p<0.05 (rs1050828 +0.118 per allele, p=0.005; rs2032582 -0.048, p=0.049), and ZERO survived a Bonferroni correction (threshold p<0.0021). The SNP panel as a whole shows no convincing signal beyond chance.

Iteration 10 - Multivariable synthesis
A single logistic regression on response that included ECOG, age, key labs, AML mutations, all six treatments, and race produced an internally consistent picture:
  ECOG -0.383 (p<1e-95)            <- dominant clinical predictor
  weight_loss -0.040 (p<1e-33)
  blasts -0.0057 (p<1e-18)
  IDH1 mutation +0.362 (p<1e-16)   <- only mutation with independent positive effect
  albumin +0.117 (p<1e-5)
  CRP -0.0072 (p<1e-5)
  ven/aza +0.058 (p=0.017)         <- only treatment with independent positive effect
  Black race -0.107 (p=0.006)      <- disparity persists after full adjustment
  Age, NPM1, TP53, complex karyotype, FLT3-ITD: all null after adjustment
  Other five treatments: all null after adjustment
ECOG-stratified ven/aza analysis: largest absolute benefit in ECOG 0 (+1.4 pp), modest in ECOG 1-2 (+0.3-0.7 pp).

Overall conclusions
1. The strongest predictors of objective response are clinical/laboratory frailty/burden markers (ECOG, weight loss, marrow blasts, albumin, CRP), not molecular markers - except IDH1 mutation, which has a consistent ~5 pp main effect on response.
2. Among treatments, only venetoclax+azacitidine is independently associated with higher response, and the effect is small (~1 pp absolute) but reproducible across univariable, fitness-stratified, and multivariable models. Its activity is enriched in patients labeled unfit_for_intensive (interaction p=0.036), which matches its real-world clinical positioning.
3. Several canonical biomarker-matched treatment effects were NOT recovered in this dataset:
   - FLT3 inhibitors (midostaurin, gilteritinib) showed no benefit in FLT3-ITD or FLT3-TKD patients.
   - Ivosidenib in IDH1-mutant AML showed a SIGNIFICANTLY NEGATIVE interaction (p=0.027), the opposite of the FDA-label expectation - ivosidenib appears to abolish the favorable IDH1 main effect.
   - Enasidenib in IDH2-mutant AML was directionally negative and non-significant.
   These results either reflect a treatment-assignment process in the underlying EHR data that confounds biomarker-matched effects, or genuine null/inverse effects in this synthetic-feeling cohort. They warrant scrutiny.
4. A racial disparity exists: Black patients have ~1.5 pp lower response than white patients, and the gap is essentially unchanged after adjusting for ECOG, labs, mutations, and treatment - implying it is not mediated by the measured prognostic or treatment variables in this dataset. Insurance and rurality are not associated with response.
5. The 24-SNP pharmacogenomic panel contributes nothing of significance; this matches the prior expectation for this kind of curated marker list against a single response endpoint at this sample size.
