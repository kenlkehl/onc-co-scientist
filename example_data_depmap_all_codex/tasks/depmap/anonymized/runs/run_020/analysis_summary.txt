CRISPR dependency map analysis summary for ds001_depmap

Dataset and approach
I analyzed 50,000 cell-line records with 36 anonymized features and 10 dependency outcomes. More negative dependency scores indicate stronger dependency. I first screened main effects using Welch t-tests for categorical and binary features and Pearson/quantile comparisons for continuous features. I then ran systematic dependency-heterogeneity searches using feature-by-feature conjunctions and driver-by-modifier interaction models for every dependency outcome.

Strong binary dependency signals
The clearest oncogene-like main effects were strong and directionally consistent. `feature_009 == 1` marked BRAF dependency: mean `dependency_BRAF` was -0.805 versus -0.250 in other lines, effect -0.555, p underflowed to 0. `feature_022 == 1` marked EGFR dependency: -0.601 versus -0.252, effect -0.349, p underflowed to 0. `feature_024 == 1` marked ERBB2 dependency: -0.686 versus -0.253, effect -0.432, p underflowed to 0. `feature_014 == 1` marked KRAS dependency: -0.559 versus -0.252, effect -0.307, p underflowed to 0. `feature_027 == 1` had a smaller but highly significant RIT1 effect: -0.353 versus -0.257, effect -0.096, p=1.55e-68.

Shared continuous-feature dependencies
High `feature_016` was associated with several broadly essential dependency outcomes. Top-quartile `feature_016` (`>= 0.964`) was more negative for `dependency_POLR2A` (effect -0.104, p=1.01e-179), `dependency_TMED10` (effect -0.0369, p=2.52e-24), and `dependency_DHX9` (effect -0.0371, p=1.71e-24). `dependency_RPL11` showed strong associations with both `feature_018 == 1` (effect -0.109, p=5.71e-140) and high `feature_003` (effect -0.0988, p=7.61e-160).

KIF18A heterogeneity
KIF18A was the strongest subgroup-specific pattern. Colorectal lines (`feature_011 == "colorectal"`) had more negative `dependency_KIF18A` than other lines (effect -0.468, p underflowed to 0), and `feature_035 == 1` was also associated with stronger dependency (effect -0.292, p underflowed to 0). The signal was not a simple additive main effect: the colorectal and `feature_035 == 1` double-positive subgroup had mean -0.943 versus -0.253 otherwise, effect -0.690, p underflowed to 0. Within that double-positive subgroup, `feature_020 == 1` suppressed the dependency (mean -0.252 versus -1.092 for `feature_020 == 0`), while high `feature_028` (`>= 0.819`) enabled it (mean -1.112 versus -0.273 for lower `feature_028`). The final four-predicate subgroup `feature_011 == "colorectal"`, `feature_035 == 1`, `feature_020 == 0`, and `feature_028 >= 0.819` had mean `dependency_KIF18A` -1.299 versus -0.253 otherwise, effect -1.046, p underflowed to 0. A full factorial OLS model retained a negative four-way interaction coefficient of -0.942 (p=3.43e-47), supporting the complete subgroup definition.

Heterogeneity searches by outcome
For BRAF, the systematic search did not find a meaningful modifier beyond `feature_009 == 1`; skin lineage was not a significant amplifier (interaction -0.0126, p=0.288). For EGFR, high `feature_023` modestly amplified the `feature_022 == 1` effect (interaction -0.0299, p=0.028), and the final refined subgroup was `feature_022 == 1` plus `feature_023 >= 5.669` (effect -0.351 versus all others, p=4.52e-144). For ERBB2, `feature_010 == "her2_enriched"` modestly amplified the `feature_024 == 1` effect (interaction -0.0671, p=0.037), with a final subgroup effect -0.458 (p=6.99e-32). For KRAS, pancreatic and colorectal lineage amplification hypotheses were refuted (pancreatic interaction -0.0028, p=0.795; colorectal interaction +0.0054, p=0.595), so the best subgroup remained `feature_014 == 1`. For RIT1, brain lineage amplified the `feature_027 == 1` effect (interaction -0.0582, p=0.00564); the final brain/`feature_027 == 1` subgroup had effect -0.140 (p=1.01e-12).

For TMED10, the heterogeneity search found only weak modifiers; prostate amplification of high `feature_016` was borderline and not significant (interaction -0.0309, p=0.0626), so the best supported subgroup remained high `feature_016` alone. For DHX9, `feature_027 == 1` amplified the high-`feature_016` effect (interaction -0.0323, p=0.00967), and the final high-`feature_016`/`feature_027 == 1` subgroup had effect -0.0539 (p=3.71e-07). For POLR2A, high `feature_016` combined with `feature_027 == 0` gave the strongest refined subgroup (interaction -0.0359, p=0.00433; subgroup effect -0.105, p=1.19e-171). For RPL11, high `feature_031` amplified the `feature_018 == 1` signal (interaction -0.0352, p=0.000421), and this interaction remained negative after adjusting for high `feature_003` and high `feature_016` (coefficient -0.0357, p=0.000327).

Overall conclusions
The dataset contains several strong one-feature dependency markers, especially for BRAF, EGFR, ERBB2, and KRAS. RIT1 is weaker but still clearly associated with `feature_027`. POLR2A, TMED10, and DHX9 share a broad high-`feature_016` dependency pattern, with outcome-specific modifiers for DHX9 and POLR2A. RPL11 combines a strong `feature_018` signal with continuous-feature modifiers. The most biologically structured pattern is KIF18A, where dependency is concentrated in a narrowly defined colorectal/`feature_035 == 1` subgroup and is strongly enabled by high `feature_028` while being suppressed by `feature_020 == 1`.
