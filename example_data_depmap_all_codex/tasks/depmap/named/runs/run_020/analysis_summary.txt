CRISPR dependency map analysis summary for ds001_depmap
Rows analyzed: 50000; dependency outcomes: dependency_BRAF, dependency_EGFR, dependency_ERBB2, dependency_KRAS, dependency_RIT1, dependency_KIF18A, dependency_TMED10, dependency_DHX9, dependency_POLR2A, dependency_RPL11.

Iteration 1 tested direct oncogene-addiction hypotheses. Matching alterations were the dominant effects: braf_v600e=1 shifted dependency_BRAF by -0.5552, egfr_amplification=1 shifted dependency_EGFR by -0.3486, erbb2_amplification=1 shifted dependency_ERBB2 by -0.4321, and kras_mutation=1 shifted dependency_KRAS by -0.3075; all were highly significant, with p-values under numerical precision or effectively zero.

Iteration 2 examined heterogeneity of those addiction signals. The BRAF V600E effect was not measurably stronger in skin lineage (interaction -0.0126, p=0.288), indicating the driver event itself explains most of the BRAF dependency. EGFR-amplified lines showed modest refinement: high MYC RNA made dependency_EGFR more negative by -0.0303 within amplified lines (p=0.0205), whereas luminal subtype was less negative by 0.0585 (p=0.0123). ERBB2-amplified HER2-enriched lines were more dependent than other ERBB2-amplified lines by -0.0689 (p=0.0243).

Iteration 3 checked KRAS and RIT1 refinement. The KRAS mutation effect was not pancreatic-specific (KRAS-by-pancreatic interaction -0.0028, p=0.795), despite pancreatic lines having a lower average KRAS dependency overall due to mutation enrichment. RIT1 dependency was linked to nf1_loss=1 (effect -0.0955, p=1.55e-68). Within NF1-loss lines, brain lineage intensified dependency_RIT1 by -0.0560 (p=0.00432), while EGFR amplification was a modest suppressor, shifting scores less negative by 0.0405 (p=0.0344).

Iterations 4 and 5 focused on the strongest heterogeneous signal, dependency_KIF18A. Colorectal lineage alone shifted KIF18A by -0.4680 (p=<1e-300). Within colorectal lines, APC mutation shifted scores by -0.6833 and high WNT activity shifted scores by -0.6698; both were highly significant. The complete dependency-enabling subgroup was lineage=colorectal, apc_mutation=1, wnt_activity_score in the top quartile, and smad4_loss=0. This subgroup had mean dependency_KIF18A -1.2990 versus -0.2530 in all other lines, an effect of -1.0460 (p=<1e-300). SMAD4 loss was a strong suppressor inside colorectal/APC-mutant lines: smad4_loss=1 made KIF18A scores less negative by 0.8406 relative to SMAD4-retained colorectal/APC-mutant lines (p=<1e-300).

Iteration 6 tested MYC/ribosome-related dependencies. myc_amplification=1 shifted dependency_RPL11 by -0.1091 (p=5.71e-140). Among MYC-amplified lines, high growth further shifted RPL11 by -0.0471 (p=2.67e-08), and high copy_number_8q24 shifted it by -0.1018 (p=1.34e-06).

Iteration 7 found a weaker MYC/growth DHX9 axis. Top-quartile rnaseq_MYC_log2_tpm shifted dependency_DHX9 by -0.0286 (p=2.05e-15). The combined high-MYC/high-growth subgroup had a stronger contrast versus all other lines, effect -0.0595 (p=2.48e-21), and within high-MYC lines growth added -0.0464 (p=4.50e-11).

Iteration 8 showed that growth rate was the main broad dependency covariate. Top-quartile growth shifted dependency_POLR2A by -0.1043 (p=1.01e-179), the largest general growth-associated effect. It also shifted dependency_TMED10 by -0.0369 (p=2.52e-24). Within high-growth lines, basal subtype was less TMED10-dependent by 0.0413 (p=0.00716), suggesting a suppressive subtype context for that otherwise weak outcome.

Iteration 9 performed the required systematic dependency-heterogeneity search for every dependency outcome using single predicates and pairwise conjunctions over lineage, subtype, culture, batch, binary genomic features, and top/bottom quartiles of continuous features, with a minimum subgroup size of 300. The interpretable best-supported subgroups were: BRAF with braf_v600e=1; EGFR with egfr_amplification=1; ERBB2 with erbb2_amplification=1; KRAS with kras_mutation=1; RIT1 with nf1_loss=1 plus brain lineage; KIF18A with colorectal/APC-mutant/WNT-high/SMAD4-retained status; TMED10 with high growth, with a smaller high-growth prostate subgroup; DHX9 with high MYC RNA plus high growth; POLR2A with high growth; and RPL11 with MYC amplification plus high growth.

Iteration 10 checked robustness of the four canonical oncogene effects in adjusted OLS models including lineage, culture_type, screen_batch, cas9_activity_score, and growth_rate_doublings_per_day. The adjusted coefficients remained large and negative: BRAF -0.5545, EGFR -0.3489, ERBB2 -0.4325, and KRAS -0.3095; all retained p-values below numerical precision or far below 0.05. This supports the interpretation that the primary oncogene dependencies are not artifacts of screen batch, culture, growth, or lineage composition.

Overall conclusions: the strongest biological effects were alteration-matched oncogene dependencies for BRAF, EGFR, ERBB2, and KRAS; an NF1-loss/RIT1 dependency with brain-lineage intensification and EGFR-amplification suppression; a highly specific KIF18A dependency requiring colorectal lineage, APC mutation, high WNT activity, and retained SMAD4; MYC/growth-associated RPL11 and DHX9 dependencies; and a broad high-growth dependency signal for POLR2A and, more weakly, TMED10. Refuted or weakened hypotheses included skin-specific amplification of the BRAF V600E effect and pancreatic-specific amplification of the KRAS mutation effect.
