CRISPR Dependency Map Analysis Summary for ds001_depmap

I analyzed 50,000 cell-line records with 10 CRISPR dependency outcomes. Dependency scores are centered so more negative values mean stronger dependency. The analyses used Welch subgroup contrasts, adjusted OLS models with lineage/culture/batch/screen-quality covariates, selected interaction models, and a systematic heterogeneity search over binary features, categorical levels, and quartile-thresholded continuous features for every dependency outcome.

Iteration 1 tested canonical oncogene-addiction main effects. The expected driver dependencies were strongly supported. `braf_v600e` = 1 predicted more negative `dependency_BRAF` with an adjusted coefficient of -0.5545 (p < 1e-300). `egfr_amplification` = 1 predicted more negative `dependency_EGFR` (beta -0.3489, p < 1e-300), `erbb2_amplification` = 1 predicted more negative `dependency_ERBB2` (beta -0.4326, p < 1e-300), `kras_mutation` = 1 predicted more negative `dependency_KRAS` (beta -0.3095, p < 1e-300), and `nf1_loss` = 1 predicted more negative `dependency_RIT1` (beta -0.0954, p=7.87e-69).

Iteration 2 asked whether the canonical driver dependencies were lineage-restricted. Most were not. Among BRAF V600E-mutant lines, skin lineage was not significantly more BRAF-dependent than non-skin mutant lines (effect -0.0103, p=0.295). Among KRAS-mutant lines, pancreatic/colorectal lineages were not more KRAS-dependent than other mutant lineages (effect +0.0085, p=0.191). EGFR-amplified high-growth lines were modestly more EGFR-dependent (effect -0.0322, p=0.0126), and ERBB2-amplified HER2-enriched lines were modestly more ERBB2-dependent (effect -0.0689, p=0.0243), but the main amplification events were the dominant signals.

Iterations 3 and 4 identified the strongest multifeature dependency: `dependency_KIF18A`. Colorectal lineage alone had a large negative contrast (effect -0.4680, p < 1e-300), APC mutation was also strong (effect -0.2921, p < 1e-300), and the combined colorectal/APC-mutant group was much stronger (effect -0.6899, p < 1e-300). Within colorectal APC-mutant lines, WNT-high status (`wnt_activity_score` >= 0.819) separated dependent from nondependent lines (effect -0.8392, p < 1e-300). The complete best-supported subgroup was `lineage` = colorectal, `apc_mutation` = 1, `wnt_activity_score` >= 0.819, and `smad4_loss` = 0: mean `dependency_KIF18A` -1.2990 vs -0.2530 in other lines, effect -1.0460, adjusted beta -1.0380, p < 1e-300. SMAD4 loss suppressed this dependency: within colorectal/APC/WNT-high lines, `smad4_loss` = 1 had scores 1.0433 units less negative than SMAD4-intact lines (p < 1e-300), and the colorectal interaction model gave an APC:WNT coefficient of -0.9933 plus an APC:WNT:SMAD4-loss coefficient of +0.9958.

Iterations 5 and 6 explored MYC, copy-number, and growth-associated dependencies. `dependency_RPL11` was tied to MYC/8q24 context: `copy_number_8q24` >= 0.428 plus `myc_amplification` = 1 had effect -0.1123 (p=7.53e-144) and adjusted beta -0.1074 (p=1.93e-133). High growth plus MYC amplification produced an even larger raw RPL11 contrast (effect -0.1303, p=1.10e-68). `dependency_DHX9` showed a smaller MYC/growth pattern: high growth plus high `rnaseq_MYC_log2_tpm` had effect -0.0595 (p=2.48e-21), adjusted beta -0.0339 (p=4.02e-07). `dependency_POLR2A` was dominated by growth rate: high-growth lines had effect -0.1043 (p=1.01e-179). A more concentrated high-growth/9p21-low/IFN-high subgroup had effect -0.1239 and retained a smaller adjusted beta of -0.0467 (p=6.96e-04).

Iteration 7 focused on `dependency_TMED10`. The evidence supported broad screen/growth effects rather than a molecularly defined dependency. High-growth lines had more negative TMED10 scores (effect -0.0369, p=2.52e-24), and adjusted continuous models showed negative coefficients for growth rate (beta -0.0203 per SD, p=1.84e-38) and Cas9 activity (beta -0.0124 per SD, p=1.86e-15). However, the high-growth/high-Cas9 subgroup did not have an independent residual effect after adjusting for growth and Cas9 main effects (beta +0.0033, p=0.646).

Iteration 8 ran the required systematic heterogeneity screen for every dependency outcome. The dominant subgroups were `braf_v600e` = 1 for BRAF, `egfr_amplification` = 1 for EGFR, `erbb2_amplification` = 1 for ERBB2, `kras_mutation` = 1 for KRAS, `nf1_loss` = 1 plus low MYC expression for RIT1, colorectal/APC/WNT-high/SMAD4-intact for KIF18A, high growth for TMED10, high growth plus high MYC expression for DHX9, high growth with optional 9p21-low/IFN-high concentration for POLR2A, and MYC/8q24 context for RPL11.

Iteration 9 validated the final selected subgroups in adjusted models. The driver dependencies and KIF18A/RPL11 subgroups remained extremely stable after covariate adjustment. RIT1 was sharpened by `nf1_loss` plus low MYC expression (adjusted beta -0.1130, p=4.19e-27). DHX9 and POLR2A retained weaker but significant adjusted subgroup coefficients. TMED10 remained best interpreted as a growth/Cas9-associated screen effect rather than a discrete residual subgroup.

Overall conclusions: the dataset contains clear synthetic DepMap-like oncogene dependencies for BRAF, EGFR, ERBB2, KRAS, and RIT1; a very strong multivariable KIF18A dependency specific to colorectal APC-mutant WNT-high SMAD4-intact lines; and broader growth/MYC-axis dependencies for DHX9, POLR2A, TMED10, and RPL11. The most biologically specific multifeature rule is the KIF18A subgroup, where SMAD4 loss almost completely suppresses the APC/WNT-associated dependency.
