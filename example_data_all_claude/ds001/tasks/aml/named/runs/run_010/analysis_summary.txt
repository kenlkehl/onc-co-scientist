ds001_aml — Analysis Summary
============================

Cohort. 50,000 adult AML patients (mean age 68 yr, 45% female). Overall objective_response = 18.8%.
The dataset includes baseline demographics, performance status, AML disease features (secondary AML, fitness for intensive therapy, cytogenetics, mutations), labs, and six treatment indicators (midostaurin, gilteritinib, ivosidenib, enasidenib, venetoclax + azacitidine, 7+3). Outcome: binary objective_response.

The analysis proceeded over 25 iterations across three phases: (1) main effects of every feature and every treatment, (2) targeted treatment-by-biomarker interactions, and (3) systematic subgroup discovery for each treatment with usable variation, refined down to the smallest subgroup that captures the heterogeneity.

----------------------------------------------------------------
PHASE 1 — Main effects
----------------------------------------------------------------

Treatment marginal effects on response (chi-square, unadjusted):
- treatment_venetoclax_azacitidine:    24.0% on vs 18.6% off  (Δ=+5.4 pp, p≈7e-51) — STRONG
- treatment_7plus3:                   18.9% vs 18.6%          (Δ=+0.3 pp, p=0.46) — null
- treatment_midostaurin:              18.6% vs 18.8%          (Δ=−0.2 pp, p=0.67) — null
- treatment_gilteritinib:             18.1% vs 18.9%          (Δ=−0.7 pp, p=0.14) — null
- treatment_ivosidenib:               18.8% vs 18.7%          (Δ=+0.1 pp, p=0.93) — null
- treatment_enasidenib:               18.6% vs 18.8%          (Δ=−0.2 pp, p=0.79) — null

Biomarker / demographic main effects, adjusted (multivariable logistic with all 36 features):
Significantly INCREASE objective_response: npm1_mutation (β=+0.44, OR≈1.55, p≈2e-71), unfit_for_intensive (β=+0.37, p≈2e-57), albumin_g_dl (β=+0.12, p=1.4e-7), treatment_venetoclax_azacitidine (β=+0.36, p=8e-54), alkaline_phosphatase_u_l (β=+0.0005, p=0.02).
Significantly DECREASE objective_response: ecog_ps (β=−0.36, p=9e-93), complex_karyotype (β=−0.17, p=6e-7), tp53_mutation (β=−0.19, p=4e-6), wbc_k_per_ul (β=−0.0018, p=1e-4), blast_pct_marrow (β=−0.005, p=1e-17), weight_loss_pct_6mo (β=−0.037, p=3e-31), crp_mg_l (β=−0.006, p=6e-6).
NOT independently associated (after adjustment): age_years, sex_female, secondary_aml, flt3_itd, flt3_tkd, idh1_mutation, idh2_mutation, ldh_u_l, nlr, hemoglobin_g_dl, AST/ALT, total_bilirubin, creatinine, BUN, sodium, potassium, calcium, treatment_midostaurin, treatment_gilteritinib, treatment_ivosidenib, treatment_enasidenib, treatment_7plus3.

Conclusions from Phase 1: Of six treatments only venetoclax + azacitidine has any marginal or adjusted main effect; the four targeted therapies and 7+3 are all null on a population basis. NPM1 is a strongly favorable disease marker; TP53 mutation, complex karyotype, high ECOG, low albumin, high blast%, high WBC, weight loss, and high CRP are all unfavorable.

----------------------------------------------------------------
PHASE 2 — Targeted-therapy × biomarker interactions
----------------------------------------------------------------

A natural prior expectation in AML is that midostaurin and gilteritinib should benefit FLT3-mutant patients, ivosidenib should benefit IDH1-mutant patients, and enasidenib should benefit IDH2-mutant patients. We tested each interaction directly, with within-subgroup stratified rates and with logistic-regression interaction terms (both unadjusted and adjusted for age, sex, ECOG, secondary_aml, unfit, complex_karyotype, tp53, albumin).

Result (unadjusted interaction beta on log-odds; within-subgroup absolute rate difference):
- midostaurin × flt3_itd:    β=−0.106, p=0.19; in flt3_itd=1 (n_on=1505, n_off=8504) Δ=−1.5 pp, p=0.18 (NOT supported).
- gilteritinib × flt3_itd:   β=+0.079, p=0.32; in flt3_itd=1 Δ=+0.2 pp, p=0.83 (NOT supported).
- ivosidenib × idh1_mutation: β=−0.30, p=0.077; in idh1=1 (n_on=300, n_off=3324) Δ=−4.0 pp, p=0.11 (trend in WRONG direction).
- enasidenib × idh2_mutation: β=+0.04, p=0.79; in idh2=1 Δ=+0.4 pp, p=0.89 (NOT supported).
- gilteritinib × flt3_tkd:    β=−0.21, p=0.16; Δ=−3.5 pp, p=0.10 (NS).

Across all four canonical pairings, the targeted-therapy benefit predicted by clinical biology is absent in this cohort. The single nominally-significant interaction across the full 60-test screen for these therapies is enasidenib × sex_female (β=+0.31, p=3e-4), which has no biological prior and likely reflects multiple-testing noise.

By contrast, the within-subgroup rate differences for ven/aza and 7+3 stratified by their natural modifiers were striking:
- ven/aza in unfit_for_intensive=1: 33.0% on vs 18.5% off (Δ=+14.5 pp, p≈9e-128).
- ven/aza in unfit=0 (fit):         15.0% on vs 15.7% off (Δ=−0.8 pp, p=0.08, NS).
- ven/aza × unfit interaction (adjusted): β=+0.95, p≈10^-86.
- 7+3 in unfit=0:                   19.7% on vs 19.4% off (Δ=+0.4 pp, NS).
- 7+3 in unfit=1:                   17.6% on vs 17.5% off (Δ≈0, NS).
- 7+3 × unfit interaction:          NS.

Phase-2 takeaway: there are TWO biomarker-defined heterogeneities worth refining (ven/aza × unfit, 7+3 × something) and ZERO heterogeneities for the four targeted therapies.

----------------------------------------------------------------
PHASE 3 — Systematic subgroup discovery
----------------------------------------------------------------

Exhaustive screen. We adjusted-fit each of the 60 (treatment × binary modifier) interactions and ranked by p-value. The top hits (descending |β|):

  ven/aza × npm1_mutation        β=+1.07, p=2.6e-103   (positive — NPM1 amplifies ven/aza benefit)
  ven/aza × unfit_for_intensive  β=+0.90, p=5e-80      (positive — unfit amplifies ven/aza benefit)
  ven/aza × tp53_mutation        β=−0.43, p=1.4e-7     (negative — TP53 suppresses ven/aza benefit)
  ven/aza × complex_karyotype    β=−0.32, p=3.4e-6     (negative — complex karyotype suppresses)
  enasidenib × sex_female        β=+0.31, p=2.5e-4     (no biological prior; single-test only)
  7+3 × complex_karyotype        β=−0.22, p=9e-4       (negative — complex karyotype makes 7+3 worse)

A continuous-modifier screen (treatment × z-scored age, ECOG, albumin, WBC, LDH; 30 tests) found NO interaction at p<0.05. The signal is entirely binary-modifier driven.

3a. Ven/aza super-responder subgroup. We descended into the 8 cells of (unfit × npm1 × tp53):
  unfit=1, npm1=1, tp53=0  →  on 69.1% (n=2125) vs off 15.7% (n=3224)  →  Δ=+53.4 pp, p≈0
  unfit=1, npm1=1, tp53=1  →  on 18.8%        vs off 18.3%             →  Δ=+0.6 pp, NS
  unfit=1, npm1=0, tp53=0  →  on 16.9%        vs off 16.4%             →  Δ=+0.5 pp, NS
  unfit=1, npm1=0, tp53=1  →  on 17.1%        vs off 16.8%             →  Δ=+0.3 pp, NS
  unfit=0, npm1=1, tp53=0  →  on 15.7%        vs off 16.1%             →  Δ=−0.5 pp, NS
  …all four other unfit=0 cells: |Δ|≤0.02, NS.

Adding complex_karyotype as a fourth refining predicate within unfit=1, npm1=1, tp53=0:
  ck=0 → on 78.6% (n=1790) vs off 15.8% (n=2741) → Δ=+62.8 pp, p≈0
  ck=1 → on 18.2% (n=335) vs off 14.9% (n=483)   → Δ=+3.3 pp, p=0.21 (NS)

The treatment effect of ven/aza is therefore NOT distributed across the cohort but is essentially ENTIRELY concentrated in a single 4-predicate subgroup. Outside that subgroup, ven/aza has no detectable effect (or a tiny non-significant negative trend in some fit-patient cells, possibly an unmeasured-confounding artifact).

This was confirmed in a joint logistic regression with all four interactions and baseline covariates:
  ven/aza × unfit         β=+0.95 (p=2e-86)
  ven/aza × npm1          β=+1.11 (p=4e-109)
  ven/aza × tp53          β=−0.49 (p=5e-9)
  ven/aza × complex_karyo β=−0.35 (p=5e-7)
  ven/aza main effect      β=−0.38 (p=4e-22)  ← in patients lacking ALL four favorable predicates, ven/aza is harmful or null on the log-odds scale.

3b. 7+3 heterogeneity. Cell-level rates by (complex_karyotype × tp53):
  ck=0, tp53=0  → on 19.8% (n=17274) vs off 19.0% (n=21047) →  Δ=+0.8 pp, p=0.048
  ck=0, tp53=1  → on 16.7% vs 16.7%                          →  Δ≈0, NS
  ck=1, tp53=0  → on 15.6% (n=3078) vs off 18.0% (n=3616)   →  Δ=−2.3 pp, p=0.011
  ck=1, tp53=1  → on 14.8% vs 16.9%                          →  Δ=−2.1 pp, NS

Adjusted joint model: 7+3 × complex_karyotype β=−0.23 (p=0.0006); 7+3 × tp53 NS; 7+3 × unfit NS. Among the 10 binary modifiers, only complex_karyotype materially modifies the 7+3 effect, and the modification is negative — i.e. in complex karyotype patients 7+3 is statistically worse, while in non-complex-karyotype patients 7+3 is mildly better.

3c. Targeted-therapy subgroup search. For each of midostaurin, gilteritinib, ivosidenib, enasidenib we tested response benefit within the canonical target subgroup (FLT3-ITD, FLT3-TKD, IDH1, IDH2 respectively) and within progressively-refined subsets that strip away unfavorable modifiers (TP53, complex karyotype). No combination produced a benefit at p<0.05 in the expected direction with adequate sample size:
  midostaurin in flt3_itd=1 ∧ tp53=0 ∧ ck=0:    Δ=−2.0 pp, p=0.12
  gilteritinib in flt3_itd=1 ∧ tp53=0:           Δ=+0.9 pp, p=0.48
  ivosidenib in idh1=1 ∧ tp53=0:                 Δ=−4.6 pp, p=0.07 (wrong direction)
  enasidenib in idh2=1 ∧ tp53=0:                 Δ=+1.2 pp, p=0.61
The conclusion is that no targeted therapy produces a detectable benefit in any sample-adequate subgroup of this cohort.

----------------------------------------------------------------
SYNTHESIS — what is supported, what is refuted
----------------------------------------------------------------

Supported:
1. NPM1 mutation is a strong favorable prognostic marker (OR ≈ 1.55 after full adjustment; about +6.7 pp marginal response).
2. TP53 mutation, complex karyotype, ECOG performance status, low albumin, high blast %, high WBC, weight loss, and elevated CRP are unfavorable independent predictors of objective response.
3. Treatment_venetoclax_azacitidine increases objective_response in a tight subgroup defined by:
       unfit_for_intensive=1 AND npm1_mutation=1 AND tp53_mutation=0 AND complex_karyotype=0
   In that subgroup the response rate goes from 15.8% (off ven/aza, n=2741) to 78.6% (on ven/aza, n=1790), a +62.8 pp absolute increase. Outside that subgroup ven/aza has no detectable benefit, and all four predicates appear necessary (removing any one collapses the effect to near zero or non-significant).
4. Treatment_7plus3 is a marginal, weak booster of objective response in patients without complex karyotype (ck=0: Δ ≈ +0.8 pp, p=0.05) and is significantly worse than no-7+3 in complex karyotype patients (Δ ≈ −2.3 pp, p=0.01).

Refuted (clinical priors NOT supported in this dataset):
1. Midostaurin → benefit in FLT3-mutant patients: NOT supported in any FLT3 subgroup or any adjusted model.
2. Gilteritinib → benefit in FLT3-mutant patients: NOT supported.
3. Ivosidenib → benefit in IDH1-mutant patients: NOT supported (mild trend in the WRONG direction, NS).
4. Enasidenib → benefit in IDH2-mutant patients: NOT supported.
5. 7+3 has a uniform population benefit: NOT supported; the marginal effect is null and the effect is heterogeneous across complex karyotype.
6. Higher age is associated with worse response: NOT supported after adjusting for ECOG, fitness, and disease features (age β ≈ 0, p=0.67).

Final treatment-effect heterogeneity hypotheses (one per treatment with usable signal):
- treatment_venetoclax_azacitidine: positive effect on objective_response, restricted to patients with unfit_for_intensive=1 AND npm1_mutation=1 AND tp53_mutation=0 AND complex_karyotype=0 (Δ ≈ +0.63, p≈0). Outside this subgroup, the effect is null. The unfavorable predicates whose presence suppresses the treatment effect are tp53_mutation=1 and complex_karyotype=1; the favorable predicate npm1_mutation=1 and the patient-level predicate unfit_for_intensive=1 are both required.
- treatment_7plus3: small positive effect on objective_response in complex_karyotype=0 patients (Δ ≈ +0.008, p≈0.05); in complex_karyotype=1 patients the effect reverses (Δ ≈ −0.023, p=0.01). The unfavorable variable suppressing the effect is complex_karyotype=1.
- treatment_midostaurin / treatment_gilteritinib / treatment_ivosidenib / treatment_enasidenib: no subgroup with a positive treatment effect was identified at p<0.05 in the expected direction at any sample size threshold tested.

Limitations: The cohort is observational EHR data, so treatment assignment is not random and unmeasured confounding cannot be ruled out (the marginally negative ven/aza effect in fit patients and the marginally negative ivosidenib trend in IDH1 patients could partly reflect channeling). Multivariable adjustment substantially supports rather than overturns the principal findings, but the absent targeted-therapy benefit in expected target subgroups is the most clinically surprising result and warrants careful interpretation in light of its possible non-causal origin.
