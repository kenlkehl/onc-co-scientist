Analysis summary — ds001_aml (n=50,000; outcome objective_response, 18.75% positive)
==================================================================================

Overview
--------
This cohort contains 35 de-identified features (16 binary, 1 ordinal with values
0/1/2 = feature_004, and 18 continuous) and a binary objective_response outcome.
We worked iteratively, beginning with univariate screens, then a fully adjusted
multivariable logistic model, then targeted interaction analyses, then a
greedy subgroup search, and finally a treatment-effect-heterogeneity scan that
treated each candidate "treatment-like" binary as the index variable. The
dominant finding is a striking, well-defined responsive subgroup driven by a
3-way co-occurrence interaction with two specific spoiler markers; this was
robust to every sensitivity check we ran.

Iteration-by-iteration narrative
--------------------------------
Iter 1 — Univariate screening. Three binary features were strongly positively
associated with response (feature_014: +6.7pp, p=5.8e-70; feature_001: +5.6pp,
p=1.9e-55; feature_021: +5.4pp, p=6.6e-51); two were weakly negatively
associated (feature_026, feature_009, both small effects, p<1e-4). The ordinal
feature_004 showed a clean monotone gradient (0/1/2 → 23%/17%/13%; p=1.8e-90).
Among continuous features, feature_020 (β=-0.036, p=1.1e-30), feature_024
(p=2.4e-16), feature_029 (positive, p=2.9e-7), feature_027, and feature_022
were significant. The remaining ~20 features were null univariately.

Iter 2 — Multivariable logit (all 35 features, continuous standardized).
Adjusted effects largely mirrored univariate signals: positive — feature_014
(+0.44), feature_001 (+0.37), feature_021 (+0.36), feature_029 (+0.06),
feature_007 (small +); negative — feature_004 (-0.36), feature_020 (-0.14),
feature_024 (-0.10), feature_026 (-0.17), feature_009 (-0.19), feature_027
(-0.06), feature_022 (-0.05). All others non-significant after adjustment.
Pseudo-R^2 was modest (0.032) — i.e., main effects alone explained little of
the response variance, motivating the interaction work below.

Iter 3 — We tested whether the three top binaries (feature_014, feature_001,
feature_021) were correlated (they aren't — joint frequencies are essentially
the products of marginals) and stratified into all 8 cells of (001, 021, 014).
Seven of the 8 cells had response rates 15.7-17.2%; the single cell with all
three positive had response 63.3% (n=2401). This was an unexpected and
overwhelming pattern.

Iter 4 — Formal interaction test. A saturated 2x2x2 model gave: all three main
effects ≈ 0; all three 2-way interactions ≈ 0; and a 3-way interaction
coefficient of +2.13 (z=20.4, p≈0). LRT for the 3-way term χ² = 425.7 on 1 df.
Defining triple_pos = (feature_001=1 ∧ feature_021=1 ∧ feature_014=1) and
adjusting for all 35 baseline features, triple_pos coefficient = +2.30
(OR=10.0, z=40.6, p≈0). Notably the residual main effect of feature_014 was
mildly NEGATIVE (-0.07, p=0.016) outside the responsive subgroup — i.e., the
benefit is entirely concentrated in the triple-positive cell.

Iter 5 — Within-subgroup heterogeneity. We screened all binary features for
within-triple-positive modifier effects. Two features acted as near-complete
spoilers: feature_026=1 dropped response from 71.7% to 18.4% (p=7.6e-86) and
feature_009=1 dropped response from 69.1% to 18.8% (p=3.3e-59). No other
binary modifier was significant within triple_pos.

Iter 6 — Spoiler interactions and refined subgroup. The triple_pos × feature_026
interaction was -2.44 (z=-16.7), with no main effect of feature_026 outside
triple_pos (+0.02, p=0.59). The triple_pos × feature_009 interaction was -2.26
(z=-13.6), again with no main effect outside (-0.005, p=0.90). We defined
refined = (feature_001=1 ∧ feature_021=1 ∧ feature_014=1 ∧ feature_026=0 ∧
feature_009=0). Refined subgroup: n=1,790, response 78.6%; complement n=48,210,
response 16.5% (risk diff +62.1pp). Inspecting the full 32-cell joint table
over the five binaries, only this single cell is elevated; every other one of
the 31 cells has response between 8.6% and 19.6%.

Iter 7-8 — Treatment-effect-heterogeneity scans. As the protocol requires, we
treated each of the three candidate "treatments" (feature_014, feature_001,
feature_021) in turn as the index variable and screened all 35 features for
treatment-by-feature interactions (binary, ordinal feature_004, and
continuous). Each scan produced the same picture: the only large-magnitude
interactions were with the OTHER two members of the treatment-marker complex
(positive, |coef|≈+1.0) and with the two spoilers feature_026 and feature_009
(negative, |coef|≈0.3-0.6). A small but significant feature_014 × feature_004
interaction (+0.10, p=0.008) and a small feature_021 × feature_024 interaction
(+0.07, p=0.004) were the only other notable signals; no other feature showed
clinically meaningful TE heterogeneity.

Iter 9 — Greedy recursive subgroup search (manual depth-5 tree on Gini
reduction; min leaf = 200). The tree independently rediscovered the same
structure: two top-response leaves were both subsets of the refined subgroup,
split further by feature_004 (response 79.1% in feature_004=0 leaf, n=693;
67.8% in feature_004>0 leaf, n=1,332). This corroborates the refined subgroup
as the dominant signal in the data, not a hypothesis-driven artifact.

Iter 10 — Spoiler specificity. We tested whether feature_026 and feature_009
operate as spoilers in the "two-of-three" subgroup (any two of feature_001,
feature_021, feature_014 positive). They do not: response within two-of-three
is uniformly ~16-18% across all four (feature_026, feature_009) combinations.
The spoiler effect is specific to the triple-positive interaction.

Iter 11 — Within-refined heterogeneity. With n=1,790 in the refined subgroup
we asked which features further modify the high response rate. No binary
feature reached p<0.05 (smallest p≈0.12). The ordinal feature_004 was the
only feature with a clean modifier signal: rates 86.0% / 77.2% / 66.8% across
feature_004=0/1/2 (p≈3e-9). Among continuous features, only feature_020
showed a modest negative gradient within refined (β=-0.027, p=0.013); all
others were null.

Iter 12 — Refined × feature_004 interaction and final adjusted model. The
refined × feature_004 interaction was -0.17 (z=-1.96, p=0.05) — a borderline
attenuation, not abolition. Stratified within each level of feature_004, the
refined effect remains very large: risk differences of +65pp at feature_004=0,
+62pp at feature_004=1, and +56pp at feature_004=2. The full adjusted model
(refined + all 35 features, continuous standardized) gives refined coefficient
+3.06 (OR=21.4), still the dominant predictor in the model.

Hypotheses supported vs. refuted
--------------------------------
Supported:
  - Three-way symmetric interaction among feature_001, feature_021, feature_014
    (h3.2, h4.1, h4.2)
  - Spoiler effects of feature_026 and feature_009 within the triple-positive
    subgroup, with no main effect outside (h5.1, h6.1, h6.2)
  - Refined subgroup with response ~78% vs ~16% complement (h6.3, h13.1)
  - feature_004 monotone prognostic gradient that holds within the refined
    subgroup (h11.2, h12.1)
  - Independent prognostic effects of feature_020, feature_024, feature_029
    (h1.3, h13.2)
  - Greedy subgroup search rediscovers refined subgroup (h9.1)
  - Spoiler effect specific to triple-positive, absent in two-of-three (h10.1)

Refuted / null:
  - The three top binaries are NOT mutually correlated (h3.1 — refuted; their
    independent univariate signals were not driven by co-occurrence)
  - No additional binary modifier reaches significance within refined (h11.1
    held — i.e., the responsive subgroup is essentially uniform on other
    binaries)
  - Most continuous features show no meaningful within-refined modification
    (h11.3 supported only for feature_020; null for the others)

Final canonical treatment-effect subgroup
-----------------------------------------
We cannot tell from anonymized features alone which of feature_014,
feature_001, feature_021 is the actual administered "treatment" versus an
intrinsic biomarker; the 3-way interaction is fully symmetric in these three
variables. The most defensible reading is that the three together act as a
joint indicator complex — likely one administered intervention (feature_014 is
the most parsimonious candidate, since it has the smallest n and the largest
adjusted effect, and adjusted models give it a small NEGATIVE residual outside
the responsive subgroup — consistent with a treatment that is harmful or
neutral when given to non-biomarker-positive patients) co-occurring with two
permissive co-biomarkers (feature_001, feature_021).

Complete subgroup definition for high response:
    feature_001 = 1
    feature_021 = 1
    feature_014 = 1
    feature_026 = 0   (spoiler — response collapses if = 1)
    feature_009 = 0   (spoiler — response collapses if = 1)

Within this subgroup:  n = 1,790, response = 78.6%
Outside this subgroup: n = 48,210, response = 16.5%
Risk difference: +62.1 percentage points
Adjusted OR (full multivariable model, all 35 features): 21.4 (p ≈ 0)

The refined effect is large at every level of the dominant prognostic ordinal
feature_004 (+65pp at level 0, +62pp at level 1, +56pp at level 2), so the
subgroup is clinically meaningful across all baseline strata observed.

Other prognostic factors (independent of the refined indicator)
---------------------------------------------------------------
Negative: feature_004 (ordinal, OR ≈ 0.68 per unit, the strongest non-treatment
prognostic), feature_020, feature_024, feature_022, feature_027.
Positive: feature_029, feature_007 (small).
The remaining ~20 features carry no detectable signal after adjustment.

Caveats
-------
- All findings are observational; the 3-way interaction could in principle
  reflect a structured prescribing pattern (e.g., feature_014 only given to
  feature_001+ ∧ feature_021+ patients in some clinics) rather than a
  pharmacologic effect modifier. The fact that only ~12% of triple-eligible
  patients (2401/20020) actually have all three positive is suspicious of
  selection but the cell sizes are large enough that the interaction itself is
  unambiguously real in the data as given.
- Continuous features were checked at quartiles and via standardized linear
  log-odds slopes; we did not exhaustively model nonlinearities.
- Multiple testing was not formally controlled, but every key finding (3-way
  interaction p ≈ 0; both spoiler interactions p < 1e-40; refined subgroup
  risk diff 62pp; adjusted OR 21.4) survives any reasonable correction.
