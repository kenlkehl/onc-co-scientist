Oncology dataset analysis — ds001_aml
=====================================

Cohort: 50,000 adults treated for acute myeloid leukemia (AML), drawn from a
commercial EHR aggregator. Single binary outcome of interest: objective_response
(observed ORR ~16.9%). Each row is one patient and there are no missing values.

Across 25 iterations of propose-test-refine, three classes of hypothesis were
explored: (1) main effects of disease, host, and treatment features on
objective_response; (2) treatment-by-biomarker interactions, since AML therapy
is increasingly genotype-matched; and (3) sociodemographic disparities. The
following synthesis walks through what was supported, what was refuted, and
the multivariable picture.


1. Disease burden and host fitness drove most of the variance in response
-------------------------------------------------------------------------
The single strongest predictor was ECOG performance status. ORR fell
monotonically across ECOG strata: 21% (ECOG 0) -> 15% (ECOG 1) -> 11% (ECOG 2)
(Kendall tau -0.0905, p = 2.1e-99 in iteration 1, gradient preserved within
every age band examined in iteration 24).

Other clinical/laboratory features tracking disease burden or host
deterioration also showed robust, directional effects:

* weight_loss_pct_6mo: responders averaged 0.57 percentage points less
  weight loss (p = 7.2e-39, iteration 1).
* blast_pct_marrow: responders had 2.08 pp lower marrow blasts (p = 9.5e-20);
  patients with blast >=70% had ORR 14.9% vs 17.8% otherwise (iterations 2 & 21).
* wbc_k_per_ul: lower in responders by 1.33 K/uL (p = 1.3e-5); WBC > 50
  K/uL identified a 14.7%-ORR subgroup vs 17.1% otherwise.
* albumin_g_dl positive (p = 1.3e-6); crp_mg_l negative (p = 4.4e-7); a
  combined Glasgow-style inflammation index (low alb + high CRP) showed a
  monotonic stepwise drop in ORR: 17.7% -> 15.8% -> 13.4% across index 0/1/2
  (iteration 3).
* A composite poor-prognosis indicator (ECOG >=2 OR (low albumin AND high
  CRP)) identified a high-risk phenotype with ORR ~12-13% vs ~18% in patients
  with neither (iteration 22).

Notably, age, sex, and number of comorbidities were NOT independently predictive
in this dataset. ORR was flat across age bands (16.7-17.2% from <60 to 80+
years), sex (17.1% F vs 16.8% M, p = 0.33), and comorbidity count (no monotonic
gradient, Kendall tau -0.003, p = 0.43) (iterations 15 & 16). Only atrial
fibrillation among individual comorbidities reached significance (ORR 15.6% vs
17.0%, p = 0.025; multivariable-adjusted p = 0.05).


2. Mutation main effects: IDH1 stood out
----------------------------------------
Among molecular features:

* IDH1 mutation: STRONG positive main effect — IDH1+ ORR 22.1% vs 16.5%
  (+5.6 pp, p = 3.5e-18). This persisted in the multivariable model
  (log-odds +0.36, p < 1e-15).
* IDH2, NPM1, TP53, FLT3-ITD, complex karyotype all showed null or weak
  univariate effects on ORR. NPM1 trended favorable (+0.6 pp, p = 0.11)
  but did not reach significance. TP53 was effectively null overall in
  this cohort (p = 0.66) — surprising given its usual poor-prognosis
  signal, suggesting the response phenotype here is influenced by therapy
  selection or a less mutation-driven outcome definition.

3. Treatment main effects were small
------------------------------------
Treatment receipt by itself produced only modest signals:

* treatment_venetoclax_azacitidine: small but significant +0.7 pp on ORR
  (17.3% vs 16.6%, p = 0.032; multivariable log-odds +0.059, p = 0.016).
* treatment_7plus3, treatment_midostaurin, treatment_gilteritinib,
  treatment_ivosidenib, treatment_enasidenib: no significant main effect
  on ORR.

This pattern is consistent with the idea that targeted-therapy benefit is
not uniform across the population and is more naturally captured as
treatment-biomarker interactions.


4. Treatment-biomarker interactions: a mixed and instructive picture
--------------------------------------------------------------------
Hypothesis-driven interaction tests yielded the most clinically interesting
findings:

Strongly POSITIVE (hypothesis supported):
* venetoclax+azacitidine x IDH1 mutation — interaction log-odds +0.730,
  p = 7.7e-18. IDH1+ patients given ven+aza had ORR 29.8% vs 17.1% in
  IDH1+ patients NOT given ven+aza (a +12.6 pp absolute increase) while
  IDH1- patients showed no benefit. The benefit was preserved across
  fitness strata (32.7% vs 17.7% in unfit; 27.8% vs 16.7% in fit;
  iteration 19).
* venetoclax+azacitidine x NPM1 — +0.261, p = 6.6e-7 (NPM1+ ven+aza+
  19.3% vs NPM1+ ven+aza- 16.0%).
* venetoclax+azacitidine x unfit_for_intensive — +0.104, p = 0.036
  (consistent with the clinical role of ven+aza as an unfit-patient regimen).

Strongly NEGATIVE:
* treatment_7plus3 x complex_karyotype — interaction log-odds -0.213,
  p = 0.0016. Complex karyotype patients had ORR 15.5% on 7+3 vs 17.9%
  off 7+3, while non-complex patients fared slightly better on 7+3. This
  matches the known poor prognosis of complex karyotype with intensive
  chemotherapy.

NULL or paradoxical:
* treatment_midostaurin x FLT3-ITD: interaction -0.105, p = 0.22 (no
  positive matching benefit detected).
* treatment_gilteritinib x FLT3-ITD: +0.034, p = 0.69 (null).
* treatment_enasidenib x IDH2: -0.124, p = 0.45 (null/negative direction).
* treatment_ivosidenib x IDH1: -0.364, p = 0.027 — paradoxically NEGATIVE.
  Among IDH1+ patients, those receiving ivosidenib had ORR 17.5% vs 22.6%
  in IDH1+ patients not receiving ivosidenib. This almost certainly
  reflects channeling: in real-world data sicker / later-line IDH1+
  patients receive ivosidenib, while fitter newly-diagnosed IDH1+
  patients receive other regimens (often ven+aza, where benefit is
  retained per finding #4 above).
* treatment_venetoclax_azacitidine x TP53: -0.087, p = 0.28 (no
  significant interaction; TP53 did not modify ven+aza response in
  this cohort).

These results suggest that ven+aza (especially in IDH1+ patients) is the
dominant biomarker-matched treatment effect detectable in this cohort, and
that single-agent FLT3 and IDH inhibitor matching is not visible at the
ORR endpoint here — likely a combination of small targeted-therapy sample
sizes (n = 3,900-7,500), real-world treatment-selection bias, and possibly
modest overall ORR differences for these agents.


5. Sociodemographic disparities
-------------------------------
* race_ethnicity (iteration 12): chi-square p = 0.0096. ORR White 17.3%,
  Hispanic 16.7%, Asian 16.3%, Black 15.7%, Other 14.7%. After multivariable
  adjustment for age, sex, ECOG, blasts, WBC, albumin, CRP, weight loss,
  and IDH1, the disparities for Black (log-odds -0.110, p = 0.005) and
  Other (-0.195, p = 0.031) remained significant; Hispanic and Asian
  effects did not.
* These disparities were NOT mediated by differential treatment receipt:
  rates of ven+aza (39-42%), 7+3 (44-46%), midostaurin (14-15%),
  gilteritinib (14-16%), ivosidenib (~7.6-8%), and enasidenib (~7.3-8.4%)
  were essentially balanced across racial/ethnic groups (iteration 13).
  This points to either residual unmeasured biology (or unmeasured social
  determinants) or the imperfect adequacy of the captured covariate set
  to fully account for the disparity.
* insurance_type (iteration 14): no detectable effect (medicaid+uninsured
  16.81% vs private+medicare 16.92%, p = 0.81).
* rural_residence: no effect (16.91% rural vs 16.90% urban, p = 1.00).


6. Pharmacogenomic SNPs
-----------------------
Of 25 candidate SNPs tested univariately (iteration 23), only snp_rs1050828
(a G6PD variant) reached p < 0.05 (carriers ORR 18.5% vs 16.8%, p = 0.006,
+1.7 pp absolute). A few others trended (rs2032582 p = 0.05; rs1799853
p = 0.057; rs3813867 p = 0.064) but none survived as clearly above
multiple-testing noise. Overall, the candidate-SNP panel was a weak source
of predictive signal in this cohort.


7. Patient-reported symptoms
----------------------------
appetite_loss_grade was weakly associated with lower ORR (Kendall tau
-0.0097, p = 0.020). Fatigue and pain scores showed essentially flat
relationships with ORR (and trended slightly opposite to expectation;
both p > 0.3). The dominant performance-status signal is captured in
ECOG, not in patient-reported symptom grades, in this dataset.


Multivariable summary (iteration 20)
------------------------------------
After mutual adjustment, independent predictors of objective_response
(p < 0.05) were:

  ecog_ps                            -0.384  (p < 1e-50)
  blast_pct_marrow                   -0.006  (p < 1e-19)
  weight_loss_pct_6mo                -0.040  (p < 1e-30)
  wbc_k_per_ul                       -0.0022 (p = 1.1e-5)
  albumin_g_dl                       +0.118  (p = 9e-7)
  crp_mg_l                           -0.0072 (p < 1e-5)
  idh1_mutation                      +0.361  (p < 1e-15)
  treatment_venetoclax_azacitidine   +0.059  (p = 0.016)

Not independently significant after adjustment: age, sex, secondary AML,
unfit_for_intensive, complex karyotype, NPM1, FLT3-ITD, TP53, treatment_7plus3,
treatment_midostaurin, treatment_gilteritinib, treatment_ivosidenib,
treatment_enasidenib.


Bottom line
-----------
In this 50,000-patient AML cohort, the dominant signals on objective
response were classical clinical/biological prognostic features — ECOG,
weight loss, marrow blast %, WBC, albumin, CRP — together with a strong
favorable IDH1 mutation main effect and one treatment-biomarker pairing,
venetoclax+azacitidine in IDH1+ patients (with a smaller benefit in NPM1+
and unfit patients). Other targeted-therapy/biomarker matchings (FLT3
inhibitors x FLT3-ITD; IDH inhibitors x IDH1/IDH2) did not show the
expected positive interactions, with ivosidenib x IDH1 paradoxically
negative — most plausibly reflecting confounding by selection in
real-world treatment assignment. A statistically and clinically
meaningful disparity for Black and "Other" race patients persisted
after multivariable adjustment and was not explained by differences in
treatment receipt, suggesting unmeasured biological or social
determinants warrant further investigation.
