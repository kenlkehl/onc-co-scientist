Analysis Summary — ds001_aml
================================

Cohort: 50,000 patients with acute myeloid leukemia (AML)
Outcome: objective_response (binary; cohort rate 16.9%)
Iterations: 25 (propose-test-refine)
Methods: chi-square for binary contrasts, logistic regression for continuous
predictors and adjusted analyses, logistic regression with multiplicative
interaction terms for treatment-by-biomarker hypotheses; significance at
p < 0.05.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADLINE FINDINGS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

By a wide margin, the strongest predictors of objective_response in this
cohort are markers of patient performance status, disease burden, and
nutrition/inflammation — NOT the canonical AML cytogenetic / molecular risk
flags or the targeted-therapy treatment effects.

1.  Performance status dominates.  In simple logistic regression, ecog_ps
    is associated with sharply lower probability of response (β = −0.384,
    OR ≈ 0.68 per ECOG point, p ≈ 2 × 10⁻⁹⁷).  This effect is essentially
    unchanged when added to a multivariable model with age, AML cytogenetics,
    and treatment indicators (β = −0.384, p ≈ 1 × 10⁻⁹⁷ in iteration 25).
    Greater 6-month weight loss (β = −0.040, p = 7 × 10⁻³⁵) and higher
    appetite-loss grade also independently predict lower response.

2.  Disease-burden labs predict response.  Higher bone-marrow blast
    percentage (β = −0.0057 per %, p ≈ 10⁻¹⁹) and higher peripheral WBC
    (β = −0.0022 per K/µL, p ≈ 10⁻⁵) lower response.  Higher LDH and CRP
    show consistent negative direction; LDH was not significant as a
    continuous predictor but the inflammatory composite (albumin < 3.5
    g/dL AND CRP > 10 mg/L) had ORR of 13.4% versus 17.7% in the “clean”
    group (Δ −4.3 pp, p ≈ 10⁻⁶).

3.  Nutrition matters.  Higher serum albumin predicts higher response
    (β = +0.115 per g/dL, p ≈ 10⁻⁶).

4.  IDH1 mutation is favorable (in this dataset).  IDH1-mutated patients
    had a 22.1% response rate vs 16.5% wild-type — a +5.6 pp absolute
    difference (p ≈ 4 × 10⁻¹⁸), the largest mutation-level effect we found.
    No other mutation tested in iteration 2 (NPM1, IDH2, FLT3-ITD) or
    iteration 1 (TP53, complex karyotype, secondary AML, unfit-for-intensive)
    reached significance — surprising given those are well-established
    prognostic markers in real AML.  Either the dataset's response
    signal is dominated by performance/disease-burden axes that swamp
    cytogenetic prognostic effects, or the cytogenetic markers are
    imbalanced relative to truth.

5.  Treatment effects are small.  Of the six treatment columns
    (midostaurin, gilteritinib, ivosidenib, enasidenib, venetoclax+azacitidine,
    7+3), only venetoclax+azacitidine was associated with higher response
    in unadjusted analysis (+0.7 pp, p = 0.03).  After adjusting for age,
    ECOG, secondary AML, complex karyotype, TP53, and unfit-for-intensive,
    the adjusted log-odds for venetoclax+azacitidine remained positive
    (β = +0.056, p = 0.02).  No other treatment had a statistically
    significant main effect.

6.  Targeted-therapy-by-biomarker interactions did NOT show the expected
    positive predictive pattern.
        - Midostaurin × FLT3-ITD: ns (interaction logOR = −0.13, p ≈ 0.30).
        - Gilteritinib × FLT3-ITD: ns (p ≈ 0.7).
        - Gilteritinib × FLT3-TKD: ns.
        - Ivosidenib × IDH1: SIGNIFICANT in the *negative* direction
          (interaction logOR = −0.36, p = 0.027 unadjusted; p = 0.021
          after age + ECOG + unfit adjustment).  Within IDH1-mutated
          patients, those who received ivosidenib had a LOWER response
          rate (17.4%) than those who did not (22.5%).  This is the
          opposite of what biology predicts and most plausibly reflects
          channeling — sicker IDH1-mutated patients selected for
          ivosidenib — rather than a true negative drug effect.
        - Enasidenib × IDH2: ns.

7.  Venetoclax+azacitidine interacts positively with unfit status.
    The interaction with unfit_for_intensive was significant (β = +0.10,
    p = 0.036): the regimen's benefit on response was concentrated in
    patients flagged unfit-for-intensive therapy (Δ +1.6 pp), consistent
    with its clinical positioning.  Within unfit patients, ven+aza
    outperformed 7+3 head-to-head (18.2% vs 16.5%, p = 0.025).  However,
    when stratified by age in unfit patients, the ven+aza benefit was
    actually larger in unfit patients ≤75 (Δ +2.1 pp) than in those >75
    (Δ +0.3 pp), which is inconsistent with the textbook "use ven+aza in
    older unfit patients" picture.

8.  Sociodemographic disparity signal.  Response rate varied across
    race/ethnicity strata (chi-square p = 0.010); after adjusting for age,
    ECOG, TP53, complex karyotype, secondary AML, and unfit status,
    black race remained associated with a lower probability of response
    relative to white (adj log OR = −0.113, p = 0.004).  Insurance type
    did not show a significant adjusted disparity (uninsured vs private
    not significant after adjustment).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
HYPOTHESES SUPPORTED
----------------------------------------------------------------------
- Higher ecog_ps lowers response (h29 / h75): strongly supported.
- Higher weight_loss_pct_6mo lowers response (h31): strongly supported.
- Higher blast_pct_marrow lowers response (h28): strongly supported.
- Higher albumin raises response (h25); higher CRP lowers response (h26):
  supported.
- Higher wbc_k_per_ul lowers response (h48): supported.
- Higher appetite_loss_grade lowers response (h45): supported (modest p).
- IDH1 mutation increases response (h6): supported.
- Venetoclax+azacitidine has a positive main effect (h13) and positive
  interaction with unfit status (h20), and outperforms 7+3 in unfit
  patients head-to-head (h70): supported.
- Race/ethnicity differences exist after adjustment (h61): supported.
- Adverse cytogenetic-or-secondary-AML composite vs none was NOT in the
  significant list (see refuted), but the inflammation composite (low
  albumin + high CRP) (h67) and high LDH (h68 — also supported, unadjusted
  diff +0.3 pp on inverted direction; the marginal cont-LDH coefficient
  did not reach p < 0.05) showed expected directions.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
HYPOTHESES REFUTED OR UNSUPPORTED
----------------------------------------------------------------------
- TP53 mutation lowers response (h1): NOT supported (Δ −0.4 pp, p = 0.51).
- Complex karyotype lowers response (h2): NOT supported (Δ −0.1 pp, p = 0.81).
- Secondary AML lowers response (h3): NOT supported (Δ −0.05 pp, p = 0.92).
- Unfit-for-intensive lowers response (h4): NOT supported on its own
  (Δ +0.07 pp, p = 0.86) — patient-fitness signal is captured by the
  continuous ECOG score, not by this binary label.
- NPM1 favorable (h5): direction correct but NOT significant.
- FLT3-ITD/-TKD effects on response (h8): NOT supported.
- Midostaurin × FLT3-ITD positive interaction (h15): NOT supported.
- Gilteritinib × FLT3 positive interactions (h16, h17): NOT supported.
- Ivosidenib × IDH1 *positive* interaction (h18): REFUTED in the opposite
  direction (significant negative interaction; channeling explanation
  most plausible).
- Enasidenib × IDH2 positive interaction (h19): NOT supported.
- 7+3 main effect (h14, h51), 7+3 in fit/young patients (h64): NOT
  supported as a treatment effect.
- TP53 × ven+aza interaction (h22): NOT supported as a negative
  interaction.
- Most comorbidities (heart failure, CKD, diabetes, prior malignancy):
  NOT significant on response.
- Sex (h32) and rural residence (h33) main effects: NOT significant.
- All three SNPs tested (h55-h57): NOT significant.
- Hepatic / renal function labs (creatinine, bilirubin, INR), hemoglobin,
  platelet count, ANC: NOT significant.
- Insurance-by-treatment-receipt disparities (h71, h72): NOT significant.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
----------------------------------------------------------------------
In this 50,000-patient AML cohort, objective_response was best predicted
by axes of patient fitness and disease/inflammatory burden — ECOG
performance status (the single strongest predictor), bone-marrow blast
percentage, peripheral WBC, weight loss, albumin, and CRP. The
canonical AML cytogenetic/molecular risk markers (TP53, complex
karyotype, secondary AML, NPM1, FLT3-ITD) were directionally consistent
with prior knowledge for some but did NOT reach significance, with the
single exception of IDH1 mutation, which was favorable.

Treatment effects on response were generally small. Venetoclax+
azacitidine had a modest positive main effect that strengthened in the
unfit subgroup, consistent with its clinical positioning; the apparent
edge over 7+3 within unfit patients (p = 0.025) supports its use there.
Targeted-therapy × biomarker interactions did not show the predictive
patterns expected from real AML biology — and the one significant
interaction (ivosidenib × IDH1) was negative, almost certainly an
artifact of confounding-by-indication / channeling rather than a true
adverse effect.

A residual race/ethnicity disparity (black vs white, adjusted OR 0.89,
p = 0.004) persisted after adjustment for age, ECOG, and AML cytogenetic
features, suggesting non-clinical drivers of response heterogeneity in
this cohort that warrant further investigation.

Caveats: this is observational EHR-derived data; treatment-effect
estimates are vulnerable to confounding by indication. The dataset
contains many columns plausibly irrelevant to AML (lung-cancer-specific
biomarkers, tumor markers, SNPs) that, as expected, did not predict
response. The flat or absent effects of TP53, complex karyotype, and
the FLT3-inhibitor-by-FLT3-ITD axis are notable departures from real
AML biology and may reflect either masking of these effects by stronger
performance-status signal in this dataset, or imbalanced encoding of
those markers relative to the simulated outcome.
