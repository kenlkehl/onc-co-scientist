Oncology Dataset Analysis Summary — ds001_aml
==============================================

Cohort
------
50,000 patient records, 125 anonymized features (`feature_001` ... `feature_125`)
plus the binary outcome `objective_response`. Overall response rate: 16.9%
(8,452 / 50,000). Of the features, 80 are binary (0/1), 7 are ordinal small-k
(0-2 to 0-10), 36 are continuous, and 2 are categorical strings:
`feature_005` with values {white, hispanic, black, asian, other} (race/ethnicity-
like) and `feature_087` with values {medicare, private, medicaid, uninsured}
(insurance-type-like).

Across 25 iterations the analysis screened all features for univariate
association, fit a multivariable logistic-regression risk model, tested several
prespecified interactions, and probed whether any apparent disparities by
race/ethnicity or insurance survived adjustment for prognostic factors.

Iteration-by-iteration findings
-------------------------------

Iter 1 — feature_057 (ordinal 0/1/2, plausibly a performance-status-like
score). Strong monotonic decrease in response with increasing feature_057:
0 -> 21.3%, 1 -> 15.5%, 2 -> 11.2%. Welch t-test p = 2.6e-103. Hypothesis
strongly supported.

Iter 2 — feature_011 (continuous, range 0-23.6). Mean 3.38 in responders vs
3.95 in non-responders (p = 7.2e-39). feature_006 (continuous, range 20-100):
mean 58.2 vs 60.3 (p = 9.5e-20). Both predict lower response when higher.
Both hypotheses supported.

Iter 3 — Right-skewed continuous feature_063 (mean 6.1, max 300) and
feature_092 (mean 20.2, max 500). After log1p transformation, both predict
lower response (p = 6.4e-15 and p = 9.6e-13 respectively on the log scale,
matching raw t-test p-values of ~4e-7 and ~1e-5). Supported.

Iter 4 — feature_099 (continuous, narrow range 1.7-5.5). Mean 3.825 in
responders vs 3.796 in non-responders (p = 1.3e-6). Direction is opposite to
the other significant continuous predictors: higher feature_099 modestly
predicts higher response. Supported.

Iter 5 — feature_035 (binary, 7.2% prevalence). Response rate 22.1% when
feature_035 = 1 vs 16.5% when 0 (chi-square p = 3.5e-18; absolute difference
+5.6 percentage points). Strongest binary predictor; hypothesis supported.

Iter 6 — Other moderately-significant binary candidates. feature_093 = 1
predicts higher response (18.5% vs 16.8%, p = 0.006). feature_121 = 1 predicts
lower response (15.6% vs 17.0%, p = 0.025). feature_014 (p = 0.031) and
feature_030 (p = 0.064) show small effects in the predicted direction.
Supported for feature_093 and feature_121, weaker for feature_014/feature_030.

Iter 7 — Race/ethnicity (feature_005). Univariate response rates: white 17.3%,
hispanic 16.7%, asian 16.3%, black 15.7%, other 14.7% (chi-square p = 0.010).
The omnibus test is significant; the largest contrast is white vs other
(absolute difference +2.6 pp).

Iter 8 — Insurance type (feature_087). medicare 17.2%, medicaid 16.9%, private
16.7%, uninsured 16.6% (chi-square p = 0.54). No detectable association with
response.

Iter 9 — feature_078 (continuous, range 18-92, plausibly age) and feature_085
(binary, prevalence 55%, plausibly sex). Means in responders vs non-responders
68.0 vs 68.0 (p = 0.72). feature_085 = 1: 16.8% vs 17.0% (p = 0.51). Neither
shows a univariate association with response.

Iter 10 — Other ordinal features (feature_018 0-10, feature_096, feature_064,
feature_045, feature_042, feature_125 each 0-4). Spearman correlations are all
small (|rho| < 0.011); only feature_125 reaches p < 0.05 (rho = -0.010,
p = 0.020). The other ordinals do not show monotonic trends.

Iter 11 — Multivariable logistic regression of the seven top continuous/
ordinal predictors plus feature_035. All except feature_078 are independently
associated with response (all p < 1e-6 except feature_063 and feature_099
which are still p < 1e-6 and p ~ 9e-7). Adjusted logit coefficients (positive
sign = higher probability of response):
  feature_057    -0.383   (p = 1.3e-96)
  feature_011    -0.040   (p = 1.8e-34)
  feature_006    -0.0057  (p = 6.4e-20)
  log1p(f_063)   -0.074   (p = 7.3e-7)
  feature_099    +0.118   (p = 9.1e-7)
  log1p(f_092)   -0.081   (p = 2.2e-9)
  feature_078    +0.0003  (p = 0.76, NOT significant)
  feature_035    +0.360   (p = 1.7e-17)
Hypothesis supported.

Iter 12 — feature_005 (race/ethnicity) jointly tested via 4 dummies in a model
already adjusted for the eight prognostic predictors above. Likelihood-ratio
chi^2(4) = 13.18, p = 0.010. The race signal does NOT disappear after full
prognostic adjustment, but no single race coefficient is individually
significant (largest: race_white beta = +0.077, p = 0.139). The joint test
suggests modest residual heterogeneity by race that is not driven by any one
group versus the medicaid/asian reference.

Iter 13 — feature_087 (insurance) jointly tested in the same multivariable
framework. LR chi^2(3) = 2.39, p = 0.50. Insurance type has no detectable
adjusted association with response. Hypothesis refuted.

Iter 14 — feature_085 effect within race subgroups. Within-race response by
feature_085: white (17.5% vs 17.1%), hispanic (16.9% vs 16.5%), black (16.0%
vs 15.4%), asian (16.0% vs 16.8%), other (13.6% vs 16.0%). All within-race
chi-square tests p > 0.13. Consistent with the absent main effect.

Iter 15 — Interaction feature_035 x feature_057. Interaction beta = +0.027
(p = 0.67). The favourable feature_035 effect is approximately constant across
performance-status strata. Refuted.

Iter 16 — Interaction feature_011 x feature_057. Interaction beta = -0.002
(p = 0.71). No heterogeneity. Refuted.

Iter 17 — Interaction feature_006 x feature_057. Interaction beta = -0.0009
(p = 0.33). No heterogeneity. Refuted.

Iter 18 — Interaction feature_011 x feature_006. Interaction beta = +0.00033
(p = 0.093). A marginal positive trend (suggesting their negative effects
attenuate when both are extreme), but not significant at alpha = 0.05.
Marginally supported / inconclusive.

Iter 19 — feature_078 x feature_057 interaction. Interaction beta = +0.002
(p = 0.19). The (already null) age effect does not vary materially with
feature_057. Refuted.

Iter 20 — Hispanic-vs-rest x feature_057 interaction. Interaction beta =
+0.002 (p = 0.97). No race-by-prognostic interaction. Refuted.

Iter 21 — Composite linear-predictor risk score (from the iter-11 model)
stratifies the cohort into tertiles with response rates 11.8% / 16.5% / 22.4%.
Top vs bottom tertile difference +10.7 pp (z ~ 26, p = 2.6e-147). The
multivariable score has clear discriminative validity within the cohort.
Strongly supported.

Iter 22 — Sensitivity: race effect adjusted ONLY for feature_057. LR
chi^2(4) = 14.05, p = 0.0074. The univariate race signal (p = 0.0096) is
basically unchanged after adjusting for feature_057 alone. The univariate
race association is therefore NOT explained by feature_057 confounding.
Combined with iter 12, race appears to carry a small residual association
with response that survives standard prognostic adjustment but is not
attributable to any single prognostic covariate examined here.

Iter 23 — feature_125 ordinal effect. Logit beta = -0.030 per unit (p = 0.022).
A small but real monotonic decrease in response with increasing feature_125.
Supported.

Iter 24 — Cochran-Armitage-style trend on deciles of feature_006. Decile
response rates fall approximately monotonically from 19.4% (D1) to 14.3%
(D10); logit beta per decile = -0.038 (p = 2.5e-20). Strong dose-response.
Supported.

Iter 25 — Final fully adjusted model including the eight prognostic factors,
4 race dummies, and 3 insurance dummies. Overall LLR p < 1e-170, pseudo-R^2 =
0.019. Prognostic factors retain nearly identical estimates and significance;
no race or insurance category reaches individual p < 0.05 in the fully
adjusted model. The earlier joint LR test for race (iter 12) is borderline
(p = 0.010) but is driven by small, distributed differences rather than a
single contrast.

Overall conclusions
-------------------
Treatment-biomarker-outcome relationships in this cohort are dominated by a
compact set of prognostic features:

  Lower probability of objective response (negative direction):
    feature_057 (ordinal 0/1/2, dose-response across all three levels)
    feature_011, feature_006, feature_063 (log), feature_092 (log)
    feature_125 (small ordinal effect)
    feature_121 (binary)

  Higher probability of objective response (positive direction):
    feature_035 (binary, +5.6 pp absolute, +43% relative odds)
    feature_099 (continuous, narrow range)
    feature_093, feature_014 (binary, modest)

  Not associated with response (univariate or after adjustment):
    feature_078 (continuous; plausibly age)
    feature_085 (binary; plausibly sex)
    feature_087 (insurance)
    Most other binary and ordinal features

Equity / disparity probes:
- feature_087 (insurance): no association at any stage (univariate p = 0.54,
  adjusted p = 0.50). No evidence of insurance-related disparities in
  objective_response.
- feature_005 (race/ethnicity): a small omnibus association (univariate
  p = 0.010) that survives adjustment for both feature_057 alone (p = 0.007)
  and for the full prognostic-factor set (joint LR p = 0.010), though no
  single race contrast is individually significant after adjustment. The
  signal is small in absolute terms (largest contrast white vs other = +2.6
  percentage points) and does not appear concentrated in any single group;
  it is consistent with weak residual heterogeneity not captured by the
  measured prognostic features.

No prespecified interaction reached statistical significance: the prognostic
effects appear approximately additive on the logit scale across feature_057
strata and across race subgroups. A composite multivariable risk score
separates the cohort into tertiles with substantially different response
rates (11.8% vs 22.4%, p ~ 1e-147), confirming that the identified factors
together meaningfully discriminate response.

Hypotheses supported: feature_057 dose-response; feature_011, feature_006,
feature_063, feature_092 each predicting lower response; feature_099 and
feature_035 predicting higher response; feature_125 ordinal effect; trend
across feature_006 deciles; risk-score discrimination; no insurance effect;
small but persistent race signal after adjustment.

Hypotheses refuted: feature_078 effect on response; feature_085 effect on
response; all tested two-way interactions between top prognostic factors;
race-by-feature_057 interaction; insurance-by-prognostic effect; most
ordinal-feature monotonic trends.
