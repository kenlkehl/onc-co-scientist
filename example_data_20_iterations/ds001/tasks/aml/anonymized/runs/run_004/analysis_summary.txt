ds001_aml — Analysis Summary
============================

Cohort: 50,000 patients, 125 features (mix of binary, ordered categorical,
multi-level categorical, and continuous), single binary outcome
`objective_response`. Overall response rate = 16.9%. No missing values.

Across 25 iterations I screened all features for univariate association
with `objective_response`, then probed the strongest signals with
multivariable adjustment, interaction tests, subgroup analysis, and
nonlinearity checks. Below is a synthesis of what the data show.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. The dominant clinical signal: `feature_057`
------------------------------------------------------------------------
`feature_057` is a 3-level ordered factor (0/1/2) with strongly monotonic
response rates: 21.3% (n=17,592) → 15.5% (n=24,971) → 11.2% (n=7,437).
Chi-square chi2 = 447.9, p ≈ 5.5e-98 (iter 1). The level-2 vs level-0
absolute response difference is -10.1 percentage points. This feature
behaves like a clinical risk-stratification grouping (e.g. ELN/cytogenetic
risk-equivalent) and is by far the strongest predictor in the dataset; it
remains highly significant in every adjusted model that includes it
(iter 6 betas: T.1 = -0.38, T.2 = -0.77, both p<1e-50; iter 25 ranks it
first by |beta|).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. Continuous prognostic markers
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Five continuous features had robust univariate associations with response
(iter 2):

  feature_011 : higher → lower response.  beta = -0.040 per unit (p=7e-35).
  feature_006 : higher → lower response.  beta = -0.0057 (p=1e-19).
  feature_099 : higher → higher response. beta = +0.115 (p=1e-6).
  feature_092 : higher → lower response.  beta = -0.0022 (p=2e-5).
  feature_063 : higher → lower response.  beta = -0.0070 (p=3e-6).

`feature_078` (mean ~68, range 18-92, plausibly an age-like variable) is
NOT associated with response (iter 5; p=0.72), which is notable because
it is the most age-shaped variable in the dataset.

`feature_084` shows a small POSITIVE adjusted association (beta = +0.0005,
p=0.025; iter 18) — weak and likely not clinically meaningful.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. Binary signals
------------------------------------------------------------------------
`feature_035` (prevalence 7.2%) is associated with HIGHER response: 22.1%
vs 16.5%, absolute diff +5.6 pp, chi2 p=4e-18 (iter 1). It is the most
useful binary marker and remains independently significant after
adjustment (multivariable beta ~ +0.36, p=2e-17; iter 25).

`feature_093` has a smaller but real positive effect (+1.7 pp, p=6e-3).

Two further weak signals: `feature_121` (-1.4 pp, p=0.025) and
`feature_014` (+0.7 pp, p=0.031).

A Bonferroni-corrected screen of the remaining ~110 binary features
(iter 23) found no further associations surviving the corrected
threshold; the data appear to have a small number of dominant predictors
and a long tail of null variables.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. Collinearity, threshold effects, and shape
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- The three "burden-like" continuous markers (`feature_011`,
  `feature_092`, `feature_063`) are NOT strongly correlated with each
  other (all |r| < 0.01, iter 7), so they appear to capture distinct
  biology rather than one underlying construct. Each retains independent
  predictive value in a joint model (iter 6).

- `feature_011` has a clear zero/non-zero structure: 27.7% of patients
  have feature_011 = 0 (response 18.6%) versus feature_011 > 0
  (response 16.2%; diff +2.4 pp, p=1.5e-10, iter 13). However, among
  patients with feature_011 > 0 the negative slope is even steeper
  (beta = -0.045, p=3e-27), so the effect is NOT solely a zero/non-zero
  dichotomy.

- `feature_006` is approximately log-linear: adding a quadratic term
  did not improve fit (LR p=0.72, iter 12).

- Tested interactions of feature_011 with feature_057 (iter 8),
  feature_006 with feature_057 (iter 14), feature_011 with feature_099
  (iter 21), and feature_035 with feature_011 (iter 22) all returned
  non-significant interaction terms. The main effects appear additive on
  the log-odds scale across these dimensions.

- The benefit of feature_035 IS preserved across feature_057 strata
  (iter 9): absolute response advantage was +6.1 pp, +5.3 pp, and +4.7 pp
  in the three strata respectively, and the interaction term in the
  logit was not significant. This is a robust, broadly applicable signal.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. Sociodemographic features (race / ethnicity / insurance)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
`feature_005` (race/ethnicity, 5 levels): Unadjusted response rates were
white 17.3%, hispanic 16.7%, asian 16.3%, black 15.7%, other 14.7%
(chi2 p=0.01; iter 3). The white-vs-black contrast was the largest
disparity (-1.5 pp; p=0.004). Hispanic vs white was non-significant.

`feature_087` (insurance, 4 levels): No detectable association
(rates 16.6-17.2%; chi2 p=0.54; iter 4).

Adjustment for clinical covariates (iter 11) only modestly attenuated
the nonwhite-vs-white gap (unadjusted beta -0.078 → adjusted -0.075,
p=0.003). This is the OPPOSITE of what was hypothesized: I had expected
the apparent racial difference to be largely confounded by clinical
risk variables (feature_057, feature_006, feature_011, feature_099),
but adjustment moved the coefficient very little. So the modest
race-associated gap is NOT explained by these clinical covariates.

A race × insurance interaction was marginally significant
(LR chi2=22.9, df=12, p=0.028; iter 24), and a joint test of race +
insurance after clinical adjustment was also marginal (chi2=15.8,
df=7, p=0.027; iter 25). Within privately insured patients the spread
across race groups was actually larger (chi2 p=0.002, range 0.142-0.189
across groups; iter 10).

Caveat: with n=50,000 even very small effects achieve p<0.05; the
absolute response-rate differences across race groups are at most
about 2.6 percentage points, and across insurance categories under
0.6 pp. So while a residual demographic signal is detectable, it is
small in magnitude relative to the clinical predictors.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. Final multivariable model (iter 20 / iter 25)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Model: resp ~ feature_006 + feature_011 + feature_099 + feature_035 +
              C(feature_057) + C(feature_005) + C(feature_087)

Ranked by |beta|, the strongest independent predictors are:

  feature_057 == 2     beta = -0.765   p ≈ 1e-76    (worst risk stratum)
  feature_057 == 1     beta = -0.383   p ≈ 6e-51    (intermediate risk)
  feature_035 == 1     beta = +0.360   p ≈ 2e-17    (favorable marker)
  feature_099           beta = +0.118   p ≈ 9e-7     (positive predictor)
  feature_011           beta = -0.040   p ≈ 2e-34    (per-unit; -0.4 per 10 units)
  feature_006           beta = -0.0057  p ≈ 1e-19    (per-unit)
  feature_005 (race)   joint p ≈ 0.05  small magnitudes
  feature_087 (insur.) joint p ≈ 0.5   no signal

------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. Hypotheses supported vs refuted
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SUPPORTED:
- feature_057 is the strongest predictor of response, ordinally graded.
- feature_035 confers a clinically meaningful response advantage that
  is preserved across feature_057 strata.
- feature_011, feature_006, feature_099, feature_092, feature_063 each
  have a univariate association with response in the predicted direction.
- feature_078 has no association with response.
- feature_084 has at most a weak signal that does not survive adjustment
  in clinical magnitude (statistically marginal only).
- feature_006 is approximately linear on the log-odds scale.
- feature_011, feature_092, and feature_063 each retain independent
  signal in a joint model (i.e., they are not redundant).
- The benefit of feature_035 is broadly applicable (no significant
  interactions with feature_011 or with feature_057 strata).

PARTIALLY SUPPORTED OR REFUTED:
- The hypothesis that the apparent race-associated response gap would
  vanish after clinical adjustment was REFUTED: a small but statistically
  detectable nonwhite-vs-white gap persists (adjusted beta = -0.075,
  p=0.003).
- The hypothesis of NO race × insurance interaction was REFUTED at the
  conventional 0.05 threshold (LR p=0.028), but the magnitude is small.
- The hypothesis that feature_011, feature_092, and feature_063 share a
  common underlying construct was REFUTED — pairwise correlations are
  near zero.
- Tested interactions with feature_057 (for feature_011, feature_006,
  nonwhite race) were NOT significant: main effects appear additive
  across feature_057 strata.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. Overall conclusions
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The dataset is dominated by a small number of clinical predictors of
objective response. The strongest is `feature_057`, which behaves like
a 3-level risk grouping; the next tier comprises `feature_011`,
`feature_006`, `feature_099`, and the binary marker `feature_035`. These
effects are robust to multivariable adjustment, do not show evidence
of meaningful pairwise interaction, and feature_006 is approximately
log-linear. `feature_078` (the most age-shaped continuous variable in
the dataset) is null. Sociodemographic features (race/ethnicity,
insurance) show small residual associations after clinical adjustment
that reach statistical significance because of the large sample size,
but the absolute disparities are modest (≤ ~2.5 percentage points) and
much smaller than the clinical risk gradients. The data are most
parsimoniously explained by an additive multivariable logit on the
core clinical predictors.
