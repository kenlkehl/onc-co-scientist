Analysis summary — ds001_aml (n = 50,000; objective_response prevalence 16.9%)
===============================================================================

Across 25 iterations we explored which de-identified features in this oncology
EHR cohort predict the binary outcome objective_response, examined whether the
strongest predictors retained their associations after multivariable adjustment,
probed for nonlinearity and interaction effects, and asked whether
sociodemographic strata (feature_005 race/ethnicity; feature_087 insurance) are
associated with response independently of clinical features.

1. Top clinical predictors (iterations 1, 2, 5, 6, 13)
------------------------------------------------------
The univariate screen and the early targeted tests converged on a small set of
features that carry essentially all of the predictive signal in the cohort:

  * feature_057 (3-level ordered): response rates 0.213 → 0.155 → 0.112 across
    strata 0 → 1 → 2 (chi-square p ≈ 5.5e-98). The monotonic gradient is
    consistent with an ordered risk-like construct and is by far the strongest
    feature in the dataset (h1.1, supported).
  * feature_011 (continuous, mostly nonzero values, ~28% exactly zero):
    higher values are associated with lower response (univariate logit
    beta = -0.040 per unit, p ≈ 7e-35; h2.1 supported). Iteration 13 showed
    that the negative association has both a threshold component
    (feature_011 = 0 has response rate 0.186 vs 0.162 in feature_011 > 0,
    p ≈ 1.5e-10) AND a graded effect within feature_011 > 0 (beta = -0.045,
    p ≈ 3e-27), so it is not merely a zero-vs-nonzero indicator.
  * feature_006 (continuous): higher values associated with lower response
    (univariate beta = -0.006 per unit, p ≈ 1.2e-19; h2.2 supported). The
    relationship is approximately linear on the log-odds scale: adding a
    quadratic term gives no improvement (LR p = 0.72; h12.1 supported).
  * feature_035 (binary, ~7% prevalence): response rate 0.221 if =1 vs 0.165
    if =0; rate difference +0.056 (p ≈ 3.5e-18; h1.2 supported). In stratified
    analysis the absolute benefit is preserved across feature_057 strata
    (h9.1 stratified rates: +0.061, +0.053, +0.038 for feature_057 = 0/1/2),
    although the overall feature_035 main effect is robust (h22.1 — no
    significant feature_035 × feature_011 interaction, p = 0.225).
  * feature_099 (continuous): higher values associated with higher response
    (beta = +0.115, p ≈ 1.4e-06; h2.3 supported). Retains a positive
    association after multivariable adjustment (h6.3, beta = +0.118,
    p = 9e-07).
  * feature_063 and feature_092 (continuous): each shows a univariate
    negative association (h2.4, h2.5 supported), but both attenuate
    substantially in the multivariable model — feature_063 remains
    significant; feature_092 weakens (h6.2 partially supported) — and they
    are essentially uncorrelated with feature_011 (h7.1 refuted: pairwise
    Pearson |r| < 0.01, all p > 0.05). The univariate-vs-multivariable
    attenuation is therefore driven by joint correlation with the rest of
    the model rather than by collinearity with feature_011 specifically.

In the multivariable logit (feature_011 + feature_006 + feature_099 +
feature_092 + feature_063 + feature_035 + C(feature_057); h6.1 supported),
all of the above remain individually significant, with feature_011's
adjusted beta essentially unchanged from the univariate value. The
zero-feature_011 stratum (h13.1) and the within-positive slope (h13.2) are
both supported.

2. Variables with no association
--------------------------------
feature_078 (a candidate "age" variable) had no detectable association with
objective_response (logit beta ≈ +0.0004, p = 0.72; h5.1 supported).

3. Interactions (iterations 8, 14, 19, 21, 22, 24)
--------------------------------------------------
We tested several interaction hypotheses. In each case the data favored a
no-interaction model:

  * feature_011 × feature_057 (h8.1): the slope of feature_011 does not
    differ meaningfully across feature_057 strata (interaction p = 0.345);
    the hypothesis that the negative effect is stronger at feature_057 = 2
    is refuted.
  * feature_006 × feature_057 (h14.1): joint LR test p = 0.49; supported
    (the effect of feature_006 is uniform across feature_057 strata).
  * feature_011 × feature_099 (h21.1): no significant attenuation of the
    feature_011 effect at higher feature_099 (interaction p = 0.21);
    refuted.
  * feature_035 × feature_011 (h22.1): no significant interaction
    (p = 0.225); supported (the feature_035 benefit is preserved across the
    range of feature_011).
  * race × feature_057 (h19.1): no race × feature_057 interaction
    (LR p = 0.61); supported.
  * race × insurance (h24.1): borderline significant (LR chi2 = 22.9,
    df = 12, p = 0.028); the hypothesis of "no interaction" is technically
    refuted at alpha = 0.05, but with df = 12 and only marginal significance
    we treat this as a weak signal that does not change main inferences.

4. Sociodemographic features (iterations 3, 4, 10, 11, 15, 19, 20, 24, 25)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
The race/ethnicity (feature_005) and insurance (feature_087) analyses are
the most clinically nuanced part of the report:

  * Race (feature_005): the omnibus 5-level chi-square is significant
    (p = 0.0096; h3.1 supported). White patients have the highest crude
    response rate (0.173); the largest gap is white vs black
    (0.173 vs 0.157, diff -0.015, p = 0.004; h3.2 supported). Hispanic vs
    white is not significantly different (diff -0.006, p = 0.23; h3.3
    supported in direction — "similar" was the prediction). Within
    privately insured patients, race differences persist (h10.1 refuted:
    chi-square p = 0.002, range across groups 0.047), but within Medicare
    they do not (h10.2 refuted: white 0.175 vs black 0.171, p = 0.59).
  * Insurance (feature_087): the four-category omnibus chi-square is NOT
    significant (p = 0.54; h4.1 refuted). Uninsured vs private differs by
    only 0.0004 (p = 0.99; h4.2 refuted). Insurance status carries no main
    effect on objective_response in this cohort.
  * Adjusted race effect (h11.1): the original hypothesis was that the race
    disparity would be attenuated and lose significance after adjusting for
    feature_006, feature_011, feature_099, feature_057. Empirically the
    nonwhite coefficient barely moves (unadjusted -0.078, p = 0.002 →
    adjusted -0.075, p = 0.003) and remains statistically significant. The
    hypothesis as stated (that the race difference would lose significance
    after clinical adjustment) is REFUTED.
  * Adjusted insurance effect (h15.1, supported): no independent association
    with response (joint LR p = 0.54).
  * Joint test of race + insurance after clinical adjustment (h25.2): the
    LR test is borderline significant (chi2 = 15.8, df = 7, p = 0.027),
    driven almost entirely by feature_005 rather than feature_087. The
    framing of h25.2 ("no clinically meaningful disparity") is therefore
    only PARTIALLY supported: the magnitude is small (a few percentage
    points in absolute response) and the joint p-value is marginal, but
    the race component does not vanish under adjustment.

5. Second-tier and weak signals (iterations 16, 17, 18, 23)
-----------------------------------------------------------
A handful of features showed nominal-only significance:

  * feature_121 (h16.1): -0.014 absolute rate difference (p = 0.025).
  * feature_014 (h17.1): +0.007 absolute rate difference (p = 0.031).
  * feature_084 (h18.1): adjusted logit beta +0.0005 (p = 0.025) — small,
    survives adjustment.
  * Bonferroni-screened scan of all remaining unexplored features (h23.1):
    no feature achieves significance after correcting across ≈100 tests
    (most permissive top hit feature_012, p = 0.05). The hypothesis that
    no second-tier feature exceeds the strength of feature_057, feature_035,
    and feature_093 is supported.

6. Final ranked predictors (h25.1, supported)
---------------------------------------------
Ordering by absolute beta in the fully adjusted logit
(feature_006 + feature_011 + feature_099 + feature_035 + C(feature_057)
+ C(feature_005) + C(feature_087)):

   1. feature_057 = 2    beta = -0.765   p = 1e-76
   2. feature_057 = 1    beta = -0.383   p = 6e-51
   3. feature_035 = 1    beta = +0.360   p = 2e-17
   4. feature_099        beta = +0.118   p = 9e-07
   5. feature_011        beta = -0.040   p = 2e-34
   6. feature_006        beta = -0.006   p ~ 1e-19
   7. race / insurance   small, mostly non-significant individually

This rank order matches the prespecified hypothesis (h25.1).

Overall conclusions
-------------------
* Five clinical features carry essentially the entire predictive signal:
  feature_057 (an ordered 3-level risk-like variable, dominant), feature_035
  (a binary marker of better response), feature_099 (continuous, positive),
  feature_011 (continuous, with both a zero/nonzero break and a graded slope
  among nonzeros, negative), and feature_006 (continuous, linear, negative).
  feature_063 and feature_092 are correlates that lose much of their effect
  after adjustment.
* The treatment-style binary feature_035 confers an absolute response
  benefit that is preserved across all major risk strata (no
  feature_035 × feature_057 or feature_035 × feature_011 interaction).
* No nonlinearity (feature_006^2 NS) and no major interactions among the
  top predictors. The five clinical features behave additively on the
  log-odds scale.
* Race shows a small but statistically robust disparity (white > black by
  ~1.5 percentage points absolute) that does NOT attenuate under
  adjustment for clinical features — i.e., it is not entirely explained by
  measured clinical confounding. Insurance shows no main effect.
* Sociodemographic effects, where present, are quantitatively much smaller
  than the clinical predictors and would be unlikely to change clinical
  decision-making, but the residual race signal is real enough to merit
  flagging in any downstream modeling work.
