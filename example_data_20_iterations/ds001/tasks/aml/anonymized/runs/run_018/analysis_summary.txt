Analysis summary — ds001_aml (n=50000)
=========================================

Cohort and outcome
------------------
The dataset contains 50,000 patient records from the ds001_aml oncology cohort,
with 125 anonymized features (80 binary, 36 continuous, 7 ordinal-integer, 2
categorical: feature_005 = race, feature_087 = insurance) and a single binary
clinical outcome, objective_response. The marginal response rate is 8,452/50,000
= 16.9%. There are no missing values.

Across 25 iterations I screened every feature univariately, fit a multivariable
logistic regression on the strongest candidates, probed pre-specified
interactions (clinical × clinical, clinical × race, clinical × insurance),
performed stratified subgroup analyses inside each level of the dominant
prognostic ordinal variable, and finally evaluated the composite model's
calibration and discrimination overall and by race.

The dominant predictors
-----------------------
A small number of features carry essentially all the predictive signal:

* feature_057 — ordinal, 0/1/2 (best/middle/worst). This is by far the strongest
  predictor of objective_response. Response rates across levels: 21.3%, 15.5%,
  11.2% (chi-square p = 5.5e-98; Spearman rho = -0.095). In the multivariable
  model, beta = -0.384 per level (OR ≈ 0.68). The dose-response is monotonic and
  the effect is the largest in the model. The variable behaves like a
  performance-status / disease-burden indicator.

* feature_035 — binary, prevalence 7.2%. Response is 22.1% with feature_035=1
  vs 16.5% with feature_035=0 (univariate p = 3.5e-18). The effect survives
  multivariable adjustment (beta = +0.361, OR ≈ 1.43, p<0.001) and is preserved
  within every level of feature_057 (Δ = +6.1, +5.3, +4.4 percentage points at
  feature_057 = 0, 1, 2 respectively).

* feature_011 — continuous (median 3.0, range 0–23.6). Lower in responders
  (3.38 vs 3.95, t-test p = 7.2e-39). Adjusted beta = -0.040 per unit
  (p<0.001).

* feature_006 — continuous (range 20–100). Lower in responders (58.2 vs 60.3,
  p = 9.5e-20). Adjusted beta = -0.0057 per unit (p<0.001).

* feature_063 — continuous, lower in responders (5.65 vs 6.16, p = 4e-7);
  adjusted beta = -0.0072 (p<0.001).

* feature_099 — continuous (range 1.7–5.5, plausibly an albumin-like analyte).
  Higher in responders (3.825 vs 3.796, p = 1.3e-6); adjusted beta = +0.118
  (p<0.001).

* feature_092 — continuous. Lower in responders (19.1 vs 20.4, p = 1.3e-5);
  adjusted beta = -0.0022 (p<0.001).

* feature_093 (binary, +1.7 pp crude, adjusted beta = +0.126, p = 0.003) and
  feature_121 (binary, -1.4 pp crude, adjusted beta = -0.091, p = 0.043)
  contribute smaller but independent effects. feature_014 (adjusted beta =
  +0.059, p = 0.017) is also retained; the other 'screen hits' (feature_111,
  feature_030, feature_091, feature_053) lose significance after adjustment.

Hypotheses NOT supported
------------------------
* feature_078 (range 18–92, mean 68 — a likely age proxy) is NOT associated
  with response either univariately or after adjustment (beta = +0.0003,
  p = 0.75). In contrast to many oncology cohorts where age is itself
  prognostic, here whatever risk age might carry appears to be already absorbed
  by feature_057.

* The other ordinal-integer features (feature_096, _064, _018, _045, _042) all
  have chi-square p ≥ 0.09 against response. feature_125 reaches a Spearman
  p of 0.02 with a tiny rho of -0.01 and is unlikely to be a real signal.

* Insurance (feature_087) is NOT associated with response, crude (chi-square
  p = 0.54) or adjusted (all coefficients p > 0.55). The four insurance
  categories have response rates within a 0.6 pp band.

Interaction tests
-----------------
I tested seven pre-specified interactions; NONE reached significance:

  feature_035 × feature_057      p = 0.59 (interaction term)
  feature_011 × feature_057      p = 0.65
  feature_006 × feature_035      p = 0.31
  feature_011 × feature_035      p = 0.25
  feature_035 × race             p = 0.68 (LR-test, 4 df)
  feature_011 × race             p = 0.91
  feature_006 × race             p = 0.36
  feature_035 × insurance        p = 0.091 (borderline, but >0.05)
  feature_057 × insurance        p = 0.96
  feature_078 × feature_057      p = 0.23

The clinical signal therefore looks ADDITIVE on the log-odds scale, with no
evidence of effect-modification by race or insurance for any of the dominant
predictors.

Race and insurance disparities
------------------------------
Crude race effect: chi-square p = 0.0096; absolute difference between the
highest (white, 17.3%) and lowest (other, 14.7%) is 2.6 percentage points. After
adjustment for the clinical covariates, every race indicator becomes
non-significant (all p ≥ 0.12). Importantly, the distribution of feature_057
across races is essentially identical (~35% / 50% / 15% at levels 0/1/2 in every
race), so feature_057 by itself does not 'explain' the residual difference; the
small crude effect dissipates once the model accounts for the joint
distribution of clinical covariates.

Within the most favorable stratum (feature_057 = 0) the race chi-square is
non-significant (p = 0.138). The within-stratum point estimates (white 21.7%,
hispanic 20.9%, black 20.5%, asian 20.4%, other 17.0%) are tightly clustered;
the apparent gap to 'other' is driven by a small subgroup (n = 359).

The composite model achieves an apparent AUC of 0.598 — clearly above chance,
but modest, and consistent with a pseudo-R^2 of 0.019. Race-stratified AUCs
range from 0.584 ('other') to 0.606 (black) — a span of 0.022 — indicating
roughly EQUITABLE model performance across the racial groups in this cohort.

Overall conclusions
-------------------
1. Objective response in ds001_aml is best predicted by an ordinal feature
   (feature_057) that behaves like a performance-status indicator and a binary
   feature (feature_035) that confers a ~5 pp absolute increase in response and
   ~43% increase in odds, consistent regardless of disease severity.

2. Lower feature_011, lower feature_006, lower feature_063, and lower
   feature_092, together with higher feature_099, identify additional
   independent favorable signals.

3. There is NO evidence that the benefit of feature_035 (or of any other
   identified clinical predictor) varies by race or insurance — the
   interaction tests are all non-significant, the within-race effect estimates
   are quantitatively similar, and the composite model's discrimination is
   nearly identical across racial groups.

4. After adjustment, neither race nor insurance independently predicts
   response. The very small crude race effect (~2.6 pp white-vs-other gap)
   does not survive multivariable analysis and is not differentially
   distributed by feature_057.

5. The presumed age proxy (feature_078) shows no main effect or interaction
   with feature_057, suggesting that the principal risk axes captured here
   are non-age clinical/biological variables.

Caveats
-------
The evidence comes from in-sample, single-cohort statistical tests with no
external validation; the modest pseudo-R^2 (~0.02) and AUC (~0.60) imply
substantial unexplained heterogeneity. Subgroup analyses for the smallest
race category ('other', n = 1,012) and for the most fragile feature_057 = 2
stratum (n = 7,437) are correspondingly less precise. Multiple-comparison
correction was not formally applied, although the strongest p-values reported
above (10^-18 to 10^-100) survive Bonferroni correction across the entire
feature panel by orders of magnitude.
