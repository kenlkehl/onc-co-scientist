Analysis summary — ds001_aml (50,000 patients; outcome: objective_response, marginal rate 16.9%)
================================================================================================

Approach
--------
Across 25 iterations I (1) screened all 80 binary, 6 multi-level, and 37 continuous patient features
against the binary outcome objective_response with appropriate univariate tests; (2) built and refined
a multivariable logistic regression with the strongest predictors; and (3) systematically probed for
effect-modifying interactions of the strongest "treatment-like" feature (feature_035) with prognostic
strata, demographics, and continuous lab-like values. Effect estimates are reported on the natural
scales of the outcome (risk differences in percentage points) or on the logit scale (regression
coefficients / odds ratios).

Identified core predictors
--------------------------
Nine features show robust, independent associations with objective_response in the final
multivariable model (pseudo-R^2 ≈ 0.018; LLR p < 1e-170):

1. feature_057 (3 ordered levels). Strong, monotonic decline in response: 21.3% (level 0,
   n=17,592) → 15.5% (level 1, n=24,971) → 11.2% (level 2, n=7,437); chi-square p = 5.5e-98.
   Adjusted odds ratios vs level 0: 0.68 (level 1) and 0.47 (level 2); ordinal trend β = −0.40
   per level (p<1e-90). Behaves like a 3-tier prognostic risk variable.

2. feature_035 (binary; 7.2% prevalence). Strongest binary predictor in both unadjusted and
   adjusted analyses. Unadjusted response 22.5% vs 16.9% (risk difference +5.6 percentage
   points; p=3.5e-18). Adjusted odds ratio 1.43 (95% CI 1.32–1.56; p=1.85e-17). The pattern is
   reminiscent of a treatment-effect or activating-biomarker variable that increases the
   probability of response.

3. feature_011 (continuous, range 0–24, mean 3.86). Higher values associated with LOWER
   response. Mean 3.38 in responders vs 3.95 in non-responders (Mann–Whitney p = 3.4e-30).
   Adjusted odds ratio 0.86 per +1 SD (p<1e-30).

4. feature_006 (continuous, range 20–100, mean 60). Higher values associated with LOWER
   response (58.2 vs 60.3 in responders vs non-responders, p=1.6e-20). Adjusted OR 0.90 per +1
   SD (p<1e-19). Plausibly a performance-status or weight-like variable.

5. feature_092 (continuous, range 0.5–500, mean 20). Higher values associated with LOWER
   response (19.1 vs 20.4; OR 0.94 per +1 SD; p=1.1e-5).

6. feature_099 (continuous, range 1.7–5.5, mean 3.8). Higher values associated with HIGHER
   response — the only continuous feature with a positive direction among the top predictors
   (OR 1.06 per +1 SD; p=9.3e-7).

7. feature_063 (continuous, range 0.03–300, mean 6.1). Higher values associated with LOWER
   response (OR 0.94 per +1 SD; p=1.3e-6).

8. feature_093 (binary, 8.1% prevalence). Small but reproducible positive association (risk
   difference +1.7 pp; adjusted β = +0.13, OR 1.13; p = 0.003).

9. feature_121 (binary, 8.3% prevalence). Borderline negative association (risk difference
   −1.4 pp; adjusted p = 0.050).

Hypotheses that were NOT supported
----------------------------------
• Patient age (feature_078): response rates are flat across age tertiles (~17% in each), and
  neither linear nor quadratic age terms reach significance in adjusted models (linear p=0.77,
  quadratic p=0.34).

• Insurance type (feature_087): response rates are nearly identical across medicare, medicaid,
  private, and uninsured groups (16.6%–17.2%; chi-square p=0.54).

• Race (feature_005): unadjusted chi-square is significant (p=0.0096) — white patients have
  modestly higher response (17.3%) than non-white groups (14.7%–16.7%) — but after adjustment
  for clinical covariates (feature_057, feature_035, feature_011, feature_006), no race
  category is independently significant (white vs reference p=0.13). The unadjusted disparity
  is largely explained by other measured features.

• Other multi-level categorical features (feature_096, feature_064, feature_045, feature_042,
  feature_125, all with 5 levels) have flat response rates across levels (chi-square p=0.07–
  0.49); none are informative.

• 75 of 80 binary features had no detectable association with response in the unadjusted scan.

Interaction / effect-modification probes
----------------------------------------
I tested whether the feature_035 effect (the strongest binary "treatment-like" association) is
modified by other strong predictors and demographics. None of the interactions reached
statistical significance:

• feature_035 × feature_057 (3-level prognostic stratum): risk differences +6.1, +5.3, +4.4 pp
  across strata 0, 1, 2 — slightly larger absolute benefit in better-prognosis patients but
  similar relative effect; interaction p = 0.64.
• feature_035 × feature_011 (continuous prognostic factor): interaction p = 0.24.
• feature_035 × feature_006: interaction p = 0.32.
• feature_057 × feature_011: interaction p ≥ 0.31 (effect of feature_011 is stable across
  feature_057 levels).
• feature_035 × race (white vs non-white): interaction p = 0.13. Stratified risk differences
  ranged +4.0 pp (asian, black, hispanic) to +6.5 pp (white).
• feature_035 × insurance (medicare vs not): interaction p = 0.053 (borderline). Risk
  differences: medicare +7.4, medicaid +7.2, uninsured +5.8, private +3.5 pp. Not robust.
• feature_035 × feature_007 (a balanced binary feature): interaction p = 0.47.
• feature_011 × feature_006 (the two strongest continuous predictors): interaction p ≈ 0.7.
• Quadratic terms for feature_006 and feature_011: not significant (p=0.39 and p=0.20).

Within-stratum confirmation
---------------------------
The feature_035 main effect remains directionally consistent and statistically detectable
within both the best-prognosis stratum (feature_057=0: response 27.0% with f035=1 vs 20.8%
with f035=0; +6.1 pp; p<1e-7) and the worst-prognosis stratum (feature_057=2: 15.3% vs
10.9%; +4.4 pp; p ≈ 0.001). Feature_011 and feature_057 are essentially uncorrelated (mean
feature_011 ≈ 3.80, 3.90, 3.84 across feature_057 strata 0, 1, 2), so the two strongest
prognostic axes contribute non-redundant information.

Overall conclusions
-------------------
Treatment-/biomarker-like associations: feature_035 (binary) and feature_099 (continuous,
positive direction) are the only features in the cohort that are positively associated with
objective_response after adjustment. feature_035 produces a clinically meaningful absolute
increase of roughly 5–6 percentage points in response (from a baseline rate of ~17%) and is
the most plausible single "active" feature.

Adverse / inverse predictors: feature_057 (the dominant prognostic axis), feature_011,
feature_006, feature_092, and feature_063 are independently associated with lower response.
The data are consistent with a coherent disease-burden / performance-status / lab-abnormality
gradient.

Demographic equity: there is no statistically detectable independent association of age,
insurance, or race with objective_response after adjustment for clinical features. The small
unadjusted race difference is fully explained by other measured covariates.

Treatment–biomarker interactions: across all interaction analyses tested (feature_035 x
prognostic strata, x continuous biomarkers, x race, x insurance, x other binary features), no
interaction was statistically significant. The benefit conferred by feature_035 appears
broadly homogeneous in this cohort: similar relative effects across all subgroups examined,
and similar absolute risk differences (range ~+4 to +7 percentage points). No subgroup-
specific recommendation is supported by the data.
