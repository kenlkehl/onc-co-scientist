Analysis summary — ds001_crc cohort (N = 50,000)
Outcome: pfs_months (mean 4.31, SD 2.29, range 0–16.25)
==========================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
This dataset includes 124 anonymized features (87 binary, 7 ordinal/multi-level integer, 35 continuous, 2 categorical: feature_064 = race-like with 5 levels [white/hispanic/black/asian/other]; feature_018 = insurance-like with 4 levels [medicare/private/medicaid/uninsured]). I used 15 iterations of hypothesis generation and statistical testing (linear regression, t-tests, ANOVA, multivariable OLS, interaction tests, joint Wald tests, and likelihood-ratio tests) to characterize main effects, subgroup heterogeneity, and treatment-biomarker interactions.

==========================================================
ITERATION-BY-ITERATION NARRATIVE
==========================================================

Iteration 1 — Lead candidate main effects.
I tested four pre-screened top-ranked predictors. All four were highly significant:
  • feature_078 (continuous, 30–90): r = +0.77, slope = +0.176 months per unit (p ≈ 0). This is the dominant univariate predictor.
  • feature_057 (ordinal 0–2): slope = −1.19 months per level (r = −0.35). Means 5.27 (lvl 0) → 4.05 → 2.91 (lvl 2). Behaves like a stage/severity grade.
  • feature_051 (binary): mean = 3.70 vs 5.06 (Δ = −1.35 mo, p ≈ 0). Strong negative prognostic factor.
  • feature_038 (binary): mean = 5.09 vs 4.12 (Δ = +0.97 mo, p ≈ 0). Behaves like a beneficial treatment or favorable factor.

Iteration 2 — Secondary continuous and binary features.
  • feature_099 (0–24.6): slope = −0.073 mo/unit (p ≈ 2×10⁻¹⁶⁷); negative.
  • feature_092 (1.5–5.5): slope = +0.47 mo/unit (p ≈ 3×10⁻¹¹⁵); positive (resembles albumin).
  • feature_013 (binary): Δ = −0.33 mo (p = 9×10⁻⁵⁶); negative.
  • feature_043 (binary): Δ = −0.31 mo (p = 5×10⁻⁴⁶); negative.
  • feature_009 (continuous, range 0–777): slope = −0.005 mo/unit (p = 3×10⁻¹⁷); small negative effect, plausibly a lab marker on a wide scale.

Iteration 3 — Categorical disparities (UNADJUSTED).
  • feature_064 (race-like): ANOVA p = 0.48; group means very tightly clustered (4.27–4.41). No significant unadjusted disparity.
  • feature_064 black vs white: −0.04 mo (p = 0.19, NS).
  • feature_018 (insurance): ANOVA p = 0.22; means 4.25 (uninsured) to 4.33 (private). NS.
  • feature_018 uninsured vs private: −0.08 mo (p = 0.13, NS).
This is already a striking finding — there is no detectable raw racial or insurance gap in pfs_months in this 50k cohort, in contrast to many real-world EHR studies of cancer outcomes.

Iteration 4 — Multivariable adjustment (R² = 0.858).
A model regressing pfs_months on the iteration-1/2 features plus race and insurance indicators confirmed the top main effects:
  • feature_078: β = +0.176 (p ≈ 0).
  • feature_057: β = −1.171 per level (p ≈ 0).
  • feature_051: β = −1.361 (p ≈ 0).
  • feature_038: β = +0.943 (p ≈ 0).
  • feature_099: β = −0.076 (p ≈ 0).
  • feature_092: β = +0.469 (p ≈ 0).
  • feature_013: β = −0.353; feature_043: β = −0.287; feature_109: β = −0.356 (all p < 1e−15).
  • feature_009: β = −0.005 (p ≈ 0); feature_067: NS after adjustment.
Total R² ≈ 0.86 — most variation in pfs_months is captured by these clinical covariates.

Iteration 5 — Disparities AFTER adjustment.
The already-small unadjusted black-white gap of −0.04 mo collapsed to +0.009 mo (p = 0.92, NS) after multivariable adjustment, and the −0.08-mo uninsured-vs-private gap shrank to −0.027 mo (p = 0.22, NS). No residual race or insurance effect on pfs_months survives.

Iteration 6 — Other ordinal candidates (feature_025, feature_075, feature_071, feature_026, feature_096, feature_033). All NS in univariate analysis (smallest p = 0.087 for feature_096). These features appear to be unrelated to pfs_months.

Iteration 7 — Bulk univariate screen of remaining 30 continuous features. Only a handful crossed p<0.05 (feature_006 p=0.01, feature_070 p=0.004, feature_028 p=0.04), but with effect sizes near zero (|r| < 0.013). Most lab-like continuous features are not informative for pfs_months.

Iteration 8 — Treatment-biomarker interactions for feature_038.
  • feature_038 × feature_057: interaction coef = −0.057 (p = 1.2×10⁻⁴). Significant: the benefit of feature_038 attenuates as feature_057 (stage-like) rises.
  • feature_038 × feature_092: interaction coef = −0.085 (p = 1.9×10⁻⁵). Significant: feature_038 benefit is larger when feature_092 is low.
  • feature_038 × feature_051: NS (p = 0.17). feature_038 works similarly regardless of feature_051 status.

Iteration 9 — feature_051 interactions.
  • feature_051 × feature_057: coef = +0.047 (p = 1.8×10⁻⁴). Statistically significant; the negative impact of feature_051 is slightly less per stage level than additive.
  • feature_051 × race (joint Wald test): p = 0.86 — no evidence of differential prognostic effect of feature_051 by race.
  • feature_051 × feature_078: coef = −0.007 (p = 1.1×10⁻¹⁶). Strongly significant: feature_051 has a larger negative effect at higher feature_078.

Iteration 10 — Subgroup screen on feature_007 and feature_067.
  • feature_007: NS (Δ = −0.006, p = 0.77). Possibly sex-like with no prognostic effect.
  • feature_067: Δ = +0.22 (p = 2×10⁻⁴), small positive prognostic factor.
  • feature_038 × feature_007 interaction: NS (p = 0.20). feature_038 effect is homogeneous across feature_007.

Iteration 11 — Nonlinearity of feature_078 and race interactions.
  • feature_078² coef = +6.5×10⁻⁴ (p = 3×10⁻⁹³): strongly nonlinear. The feature_078–PFS relationship accelerates upward at the high end.
  • Tertile contrast: low feature_078 mean PFS = 2.39 vs high tertile = 6.27 (Δ = −3.88 mo, p ≈ 0).
  • Joint Wald test feature_078 × race: p = 0.55 — feature_078 effect is identical across racial groups.

Iteration 12 — Fully adjusted disparity test (15-feature model, R² = 0.86).
Joint Wald test for feature_064 (race) indicators: p = 0.75; for feature_018 (insurance): p = 0.63. Black coefficient = −0.002 (p = 0.92). Uninsured = −0.027 (p = 0.22). No residual race or insurance disparity in pfs_months survives multivariable adjustment in this cohort.

Iteration 13 — Why the lack of disparity? Race-feature confounding.
I tested whether known prognostic features differ by race:
  • feature_078 by race: ANOVA p = 0.089 (means 64.7–65.3). No meaningful difference.
  • feature_057 by race: p = 0.63. Means almost identical (~0.79).
  • feature_051 by race: p = 0.38. Prevalence ~0.55 across all groups.
  • feature_092 by race: p = 0.78. Means within 0.02 of each other.
The clinical features themselves are balanced across racial groups, so no confounding with race exists, and adjustment cannot reveal hidden disparities — there are none to reveal.

Iteration 14 — Treatment access (feature_038) by race and insurance.
  • feature_038 receipt by race: 0.19–0.21 across groups (chi² p = 0.55).
  • feature_038 receipt by insurance: 0.19–0.20 across groups (chi² p = 0.59).
  • Logistic regression of feature_038 on race + feature_078 + feature_057: race indicators jointly NS (p = 0.55).
There is no evidence of differential treatment access by race or insurance status.

Iteration 15 — Sensitivity / synthesis.
  • R² of the full 18+-covariate adjusted model ≈ 0.858, essentially identical to the simpler iter-4 model (also 0.858) — most variation in pfs_months is captured by ~10 strong predictors; further additions add little.
  • Standardized (per-SD) effects: feature_078 ≈ +1.75 mo/SD, feature_099 ≈ −0.29 mo/SD, feature_092 ≈ +0.24 mo/SD, feature_009 ≈ −0.08 mo/SD. feature_078 dominates by an order of magnitude.
  • Likelihood-ratio test for adding feature_038 × feature_057 to the main-effects model: LR = 14.9, df = 1, p = 1.2×10⁻⁴. The interaction is real but small in magnitude (β = −0.057): the absolute treatment benefit of feature_038 is largest at low feature_057 stage and modestly attenuates at higher stages.

==========================================================
HYPOTHESES SUPPORTED
==========================================================
• feature_078 → pfs_months (positive, large): supported in univariate, multivariable, and standardized analyses.
• feature_057 (stage-like) → pfs_months (negative, per level): supported.
• feature_051 → shorter pfs_months: supported, large effect (−1.36 mo adjusted).
• feature_038 → longer pfs_months (treatment-like): supported (+0.94 mo adjusted).
• feature_092, feature_099 → opposite-sign continuous prognostic effects: supported.
• feature_013, feature_043, feature_109, feature_009: small but significant negative effects, all supported.
• feature_038 × feature_057 (negative interaction): supported (p = 10⁻⁴).
• feature_038 × feature_092 (negative interaction): supported (p = 10⁻⁵).
• feature_051 × feature_078 (negative interaction): supported (p = 10⁻¹⁶).
• feature_078 nonlinearity (quadratic): supported (p ≈ 10⁻⁹³).

==========================================================
HYPOTHESES REFUTED (or not supported)
==========================================================
• Racial disparity in pfs_months (feature_064): NOT supported, either unadjusted (p = 0.48) or adjusted (joint Wald p = 0.75).
• Insurance disparity in pfs_months (feature_018): NOT supported, either unadjusted (p = 0.22) or adjusted (joint Wald p = 0.63).
• Differential prognostic effect of feature_051 across race: NOT supported (joint Wald p = 0.86).
• Differential effect of feature_078 across race: NOT supported (joint Wald p = 0.55).
• Differential receipt of feature_038 (treatment) by race or insurance: NOT supported (χ² p = 0.55, 0.59).
• Ordinal features feature_025, feature_075, feature_071, feature_026, feature_096, feature_033: not associated with pfs_months in univariate analysis.
• Most secondary continuous lab features (feature_006, feature_055, feature_011, feature_014, feature_070, feature_044, feature_084, feature_028, feature_065, feature_118, feature_056, feature_103, feature_094, feature_121, feature_019, feature_059, feature_003, feature_010, feature_101, feature_020, feature_090, feature_119, feature_054, feature_062, feature_031, feature_082, feature_097, feature_016, feature_042, feature_123): individually not meaningfully associated with PFS (|r| typically < 0.01).
• feature_007 main effect: NOT supported (Δ = −0.006 mo, p = 0.77).
• feature_038 × feature_007 interaction: NOT supported.

==========================================================
OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
==========================================================
1. PFS in this cohort is largely explained by a small set of clinical features. A linear model with 10 predictors achieves R² ≈ 0.86. The dominant predictor is feature_078 (continuous, 30–90), which alone correlates r ≈ 0.77 with PFS and dominates standardized effect comparisons (1.75 months per SD). Its relationship is mildly nonlinear (quadratic term highly significant).
2. Two prognostic factors with large effects: feature_051 (−1.36 mo when present) and feature_057 (−1.17 mo per ordinal level).
3. feature_038 behaves like a beneficial treatment, contributing about +0.94 months to mean PFS after adjustment. Its benefit is heterogeneous: it is largest in patients with low feature_057 (low stage) and low feature_092, and homogeneous across race and feature_007 subgroups.
4. There is no evidence of racial or insurance-related disparities in PFS in this cohort, neither in raw outcomes nor in receipt of feature_038 (treatment), nor in baseline values of the leading prognostic features. The cohort appears equitable across these axes.
5. Significant interactions identified:
     – feature_038 × feature_057 (treatment benefit attenuates with higher stage)
     – feature_038 × feature_092 (treatment benefit attenuates with higher feature_092)
     – feature_051 × feature_078 (feature_051 detriment grows with feature_078)
     – feature_051 × feature_057 (mild positive interaction; the two negative effects do not stack additively)
6. Absent disparities and homogeneity of treatment-effect across race suggest this dataset is either a balanced (perhaps simulated) cohort or one where care delivery is unusually equitable; this is itself a finding worth flagging.

End of summary.
