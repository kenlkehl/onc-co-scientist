ANALYSIS SUMMARY — ds001_crc (n=50,000; outcome: pfs_months)
==============================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
This dataset contains 50,000 patient records with 122 anonymized features
and a single continuous outcome, pfs_months (mean 4.31, SD 2.29, range
0–16.25 months). I ran 25 iterations of propose-test-refine, beginning
with univariate screening and progressing to multivariable adjustment,
subgroup heterogeneity, and interaction analyses. Statistical tests used
Welch's t-test for binary features, ANOVA / linear trend for ordinal and
multi-category features, Pearson correlation / OLS slope for continuous
features, and nested-model F-tests for interactions, all on the natural
month scale of pfs_months. Significance was reported with raw p-values
(α = 0.05) and Bonferroni-adjusted in iteration 22 for the residual
binary screen.

DOMINANT MAIN EFFECTS (iterations 1-3)
--------------------------------------
The single strongest predictor of pfs_months is feature_078 (continuous,
range 30–90, mean 65 — plausibly an age-like or other monotone clinical
metric). Pearson r = +0.766 (p ≪ 1e-300), slope = +0.176 months per unit;
quartile means rose monotonically from 2.09 (Q1) to 6.59 (Q4),
Q4 − Q1 = +4.49 months. By itself feature_078 explains ~59% of pfs_months
variance.

The 3-level ordinal feature_057 (values 0/1/2) showed a strong negative
linear trend: group means 5.27 → 4.05 → 2.91 months, slope = -1.19 months
per level (p ≪ 1e-300). The ordering is consistent with a stage- or
performance-status-like indicator.

feature_051 (binary, prevalence 55%) was associated with -1.35 months
(p ≪ 1e-300), the largest single binary effect, and remained essentially
unchanged after adjustment for feature_078 (-1.35, p ≪ 1e-300; iter 6).

feature_038 (binary, prevalence 20%) was associated with +0.97 months
(p = 1.7e-216) and remained +0.93 months (p ≪ 1e-300) after adjustment
for feature_078, feature_057, and feature_051 (iter 7). This is the
largest positive binary effect in the dataset and behaves like a
beneficial treatment or favorable biomarker indicator.

Three additional binary features had robust negative effects that
persisted in the multivariable model (iter 17, R² = 0.80): feature_013
(-0.35, p ≈ 3e-306), feature_043 (-0.28, p ≈ 1e-194), and feature_109
(-0.36, p ≈ 1e-57). feature_067 had a smaller positive adjusted effect
(+0.18, p ≈ 6e-11; iter 18).

Continuous biomarker-like features showed modest but highly significant
correlations: feature_099 (range 0–24.6) r = -0.123, slope -0.073
months/unit (p = 2.2e-167); feature_092 (range 1.5–5.5) r = +0.102,
slope +0.47 months/unit (p = 3.3e-115); feature_009 (right-skewed,
range 0.02–777) r = -0.038 (p = 3.1e-17). Together feature_099 +
feature_092 added ΔR² = 0.025 over feature_057 alone (iter 20).

NULL / WEAK MAIN EFFECTS
------------------------
No main effect was detected for either of the two demographic-style
categorical features:
  - feature_064 (race: white/hispanic/asian/black/other): F = 0.87,
    p = 0.48 (iter 4); range across groups only 0.13 months.
  - feature_018 (insurance: medicare/private/uninsured/medicaid):
    F = 1.47, p = 0.22 (iter 4); range 0.08 months.
The 5-level multi-cat feature_025 was borderline (p = 0.047, range 0.13).
The plausible-sex binary feature_007 had no detectable effect (diff
-0.006 months, p = 0.77; iter 16).

A Bonferroni-adjusted screen of the 73 remaining binary features (iter
22, α = 6.85e-4) found 0/73 survived. The strongest residual binaries
were small (|b| ≈ 0.03 months) and unlikely to be clinically meaningful.

INTERACTION & SUBGROUP HETEROGENEITY
------------------------------------
1. feature_038 × feature_057 (iter 8). Statistically significant
   interaction (F = 5.35, df = 2, p = 0.0047), but the absolute benefit
   of feature_038 is similar across feature_057 levels: +0.93 (level 0),
   +1.00 (level 1), +0.90 (level 2). The "interaction" reflects subtle
   non-additivity, not large effect modification — the partial hypothesis
   that benefit is largest at the most-advanced level was refuted.

2. feature_051 × feature_057 (iter 9). Significant (F = 5.53, p = 0.0040)
   but small: -1.40, -1.35, -1.27 months for levels 0, 1, 2. The hypothesis
   that the negative effect is more severe at higher feature_057 levels
   was refuted; the magnitude actually attenuates slightly.

3. feature_038 × feature_051 (iter 10). NOT significant (F = 1.73,
   p = 0.19); benefit of feature_038 is +0.94 when feature_051 = 0 vs
   +1.01 when = 1. No meaningful effect modification by feature_051.

4. feature_038 × feature_064 race (iter 11). Interaction p = 0.076,
   not significant. Stratified benefit estimates: white +0.92, hispanic
   +1.11, asian +1.09, black +0.97, other +1.22. No evidence of
   differential benefit by race.

5. feature_038 × feature_018 insurance (iter 12). p = 0.13, not
   significant. medicare +0.97, private +0.93, uninsured +0.93,
   medicaid +1.11.

6. feature_099 × feature_057 (iter 13). p = 0.49, not significant.
   The slope of feature_099 on pfs_months is similar across stages
   (-0.071, -0.077, -0.062).

7. feature_038 × feature_092 (iter 14). Significant (F = 11.68,
   p = 6.3e-4), but small: benefit of feature_038 was +0.93 in patients
   with feature_092 ≥ median vs +1.03 below median — opposite to the
   hypothesized direction (hypothesis predicted larger benefit at higher
   feature_092; observed effect is slightly smaller at higher
   feature_092). Hypothesis refuted in direction even though interaction
   reached significance.

8. feature_038 × feature_078 quartile (iter 15). Not significant
   (p = 0.20). Benefit is roughly constant across age/feature_078
   quartiles (+1.03, +0.89, +0.96, +0.95).

9. feature_038 × feature_007 (iter 16). Not significant (p = 0.65).
   No sex/binary heterogeneity in feature_038 benefit.

10. Three-way feature_038 × feature_057 × feature_051 (iter 19).
    Significant (F = 9.87, df = 2, p = 5.2e-5) but explains very little
    additional variance — sample size makes small higher-order effects
    detectable.

11. feature_078 slope × feature_057 (iter 23). Significant (p ≪ 1e-300):
    slopes 0.180, 0.177, 0.163 across levels 0, 1, 2 — feature_078's
    positive contribution attenuates marginally at higher feature_057.

12. Race × feature_057 stage (iter 24). NOT significant (p = 0.87).

13. Quadratic feature_078 (iter 21). The quadratic term is statistically
    significant (b = +5.9e-4, p = 7e-34) but adds essentially nothing to
    R² (0.5869 → 0.5881); the relationship is essentially linear in
    feature_078.

CONSOLIDATED MULTIVARIABLE MODEL (iter 25)
------------------------------------------
A 10-predictor OLS model
   pfs_months ~ feature_078 + C(feature_057) + feature_051 + feature_038
              + feature_013 + feature_043 + feature_109 + feature_067
              + feature_099 + feature_092
achieved R² = 0.857, vs R² = 0.708 for feature_078 + C(feature_057)
alone (ΔR² = 0.149, F = 6515, p ≪ 1e-300). All ten predictors retained
the sign and significance reported in earlier iterations.

KEY CONCLUSIONS
---------------
1. The pfs_months outcome is dominated by three orthogonal axes: a
   continuous ordered factor feature_078 (very strong positive effect),
   an ordinal "stage-like" factor feature_057 (strong negative effect),
   and a binary risk factor feature_051 (strong negative effect). These
   together account for ~71% of the variance.
2. feature_038 is the clearest "favorable" binary indicator and acts
   approximately additively across feature_057 levels and across age,
   sex, race, and insurance subgroups — its benefit is essentially
   homogeneous (~+0.95 months) in this cohort.
3. Three additional binary features (feature_013, feature_043,
   feature_109) carry small but robustly negative adjusted effects
   (~ -0.28 to -0.36 months); feature_067 carries a small positive
   adjusted effect (+0.18 months).
4. Two continuous biomarkers — feature_099 (negative) and feature_092
   (positive) — contribute additional incremental variance but are
   secondary to the three dominant axes.
5. Demographic-style features (feature_064 race, feature_018 insurance,
   feature_007 putative sex) showed no main effect and no significant
   modification of feature_038 benefit; statistically detectable
   interactions involving feature_057 and feature_092 were small in
   absolute magnitude and largely contradicted the hypothesized direction
   of effect modification.
6. The final 10-feature model explains 85.7% of the variance in
   pfs_months — a strong, clinically interpretable signature dominated by
   feature_078, feature_057, and feature_051.
