Analysis Summary - Dataset ds001_crc (n=50,000 patients; outcome: pfs_months)
==================================================================================

OVERALL APPROACH
----------------
Across 25 iterations I screened all 124 features against pfs_months, characterized
the strongest main effects, examined demographic subgroups (race feature_064 and
insurance feature_018), tested treatment x biomarker interactions, and built a
multivariable adjusted model. All p-values are conventional two-sided tests; effect
estimates are signed on the natural pfs_months scale (months).

MAIN EFFECTS (univariate then adjusted)
---------------------------------------
1. feature_078 (continuous, range 30-90, very plausibly age in years)
   - Strongest single predictor. Spearman rho = +0.79.
   - Quartile-mean PFS: Q1 2.09 mo, Q2 3.71, Q3 4.90, Q4 6.59.
   - Adjusted slope: +0.176 mo per unit (p < 1e-300).
   - Direction: HIGHER feature_078 -> LONGER PFS. Effect is monotone and very large.
   - This direction is unusual for "age" as a prognostic factor in many cancers but
     consistent with a strong cohort-/dataset-level relationship; either way the
     sign and magnitude are unambiguous.

2. feature_057 (ordinal 0/1/2, plausibly stage or grade)
   - Clear dose-response: mean PFS 5.27, 4.05, 2.91 across levels 0, 1, 2.
   - Adjusted: level 1 -1.20 mo, level 2 -2.34 mo (vs reference 0); both p < 1e-300.

3. feature_051 (binary, ~55% prevalence; appears to mark a poor-prognosis or
   line-of-treatment-related feature)
   - Mean PFS 5.05 (=0) vs 3.70 (=1); crude diff -1.35 mo (p < 1e-300).
   - Adjusted -1.36 mo. Effect is consistent across stage strata (-1.40, -1.35, -1.27).

4. feature_038 (binary, ~20% prevalence; very plausibly a targeted therapy)
   - Crude: +0.97 mo (p ~ 1.7e-216), but the crude estimate is misleading because
     the effect is concentrated in a biomarker-defined subgroup (see Interactions).
   - In the biomarker-NEGATIVE subgroup (feature_013=feature_043=feature_109=0,
     n=17,372): +2.76 mo (p < 1e-300).
   - In the biomarker-POSITIVE subgroup (any of feature_013/043/109 = 1):
     +0.04 mo, p = 0.16. No benefit.

5. feature_092 (continuous, 1.5-5.5; plausibly serum albumin)
   - Adjusted slope +0.47 mo per unit (p < 1e-300). Favorable prognostic.

6. feature_099 (continuous, 0-24; plausibly comorbidity, smoking pack-years, or
   weight-loss-like quantity)
   - Adjusted slope -0.077 mo per unit (p < 1e-300).
   - Binned dose-response: 0 -> 4.59, (0,5] -> 4.39, (5,10] -> 4.10, (10,25] -> 3.63.

7. feature_009 (continuous, 0-777): -0.005 mo/unit (p ~ 2.7e-124). Small but precise.
8. feature_006 (continuous): -0.0005 mo/unit (p ~ 1.4e-33). Very small.

KEY PREDICTIVE BIOMARKERS (treatment x biomarker INTERACTIONS)
-------------------------------------------------------------
A central finding: feature_038's benefit on PFS is governed by three resistance
markers, feature_013, feature_043, and feature_109. Each substantially erodes the
benefit when positive:

- feature_038 x feature_013 interaction: -1.78 mo (p < 1e-300)
- feature_038 x feature_043 interaction: -1.47 mo (p < 1e-300)
- feature_038 x feature_109 interaction: -1.77 mo (p ~ 1.5e-102)

Importantly, feature_013 / feature_043 / feature_109 are NOT prognostic on their
own (i.e., among feature_038 = 0 patients, mean PFS is essentially identical
across these markers, all within 0.03 mo of each other; p > 0.5). They are
*purely predictive* of feature_038 response. This is the canonical pattern of a
predictive (rather than prognostic) biomarker, analogous to the role of
RAS/RAF status in anti-EGFR therapy in colorectal cancer.

A naive sensitivity-marker hypothesis for feature_067 was tested (univariate
interaction with feature_038 was +0.89 mo, p ~ 1.8e-56), but after adjusting for
feature_013/043/109 the feature_067 interaction shrinks to +0.07 mo (p = 0.15).
feature_067 appears partially confounded with the resistance set and is not an
independent sensitivity marker.

A pairwise screen of the remaining ~75 binary features for additional
feature_038 effect modifiers turned up several with p < 1e-4 (feature_086,
feature_030, feature_052, feature_041, feature_040, feature_001), but their
interaction magnitudes are <= 0.21 mo, an order of magnitude smaller than the
1.5-1.8 mo modifications driven by feature_013/043/109. These are likely real
but minor effect modifiers (or correlated proxies of unmeasured factors).

DEMOGRAPHIC SUBGROUPS (race feature_064; insurance feature_018)
---------------------------------------------------------------
A central question was whether outcomes or treatment effects differ by race or
insurance.

OUTCOME DIFFERENCES (main effects):
- Mean PFS by race: white 4.32, hispanic 4.31, asian 4.31, black 4.27, other 4.41.
  Range 0.13 mo across groups. Overall ANOVA p > 0.2. Not significant.
- Mean PFS by insurance: private 4.33, medicare 4.31, medicaid 4.28, uninsured 4.25.
  Range 0.08 mo. Not significant.

TREATMENT RECEIPT DIFFERENCES:
- feature_038 receipt by race: 0.192-0.211 (range 2 percentage points). Uniform.
- feature_038 receipt by insurance: 0.190-0.202. Uniform.
- Notably, feature_038 is administered at the SAME ~20% rate in biomarker-positive
  patients as in biomarker-negative patients across all race and insurance
  categories. This suggests biomarker testing/decision-making is not allocated
  along demographic lines, although there may be system-wide overuse of feature_038
  in patients who cannot benefit from it.

TREATMENT-EFFECT HETEROGENEITY:
- feature_038 benefit in the biomarker-negative subgroup, by race (asian as
  reference, +2.75 mo): black +0.030 (p=0.78), hispanic +0.093 (p=0.36),
  other +0.302 (p=0.08), white -0.056 (p=0.53). No significant heterogeneity.
- feature_038 benefit in the biomarker-negative subgroup, by insurance
  (medicaid reference, +2.81 mo): medicare -0.061 (p=0.35), private -0.094
  (p=0.16), uninsured -0.377 (p=0.0021). The single significant demographic
  interaction in the entire analysis: uninsured patients gain about 13% less
  from feature_038 than the reference group (still receive a large +2.4 mo
  benefit). Could reflect chance, residual confounding, or genuine adherence /
  follow-up differences.
- feature_051 effect heterogeneity by race: all interaction terms |coef| < 0.05,
  all p > 0.5. No race heterogeneity.
- feature_051 effect heterogeneity by insurance: all interaction terms < 0.05,
  all p > 0.05. No insurance heterogeneity.

OTHER INTERACTIONS
------------------
- feature_051 x feature_078 (age): interaction -0.0067 mo per year (p ~ 1e-17).
  feature_051 is slightly more harmful in older patients, but the cross-tertile
  spread is only 0.12 mo (-1.28 -> -1.40). Statistically robust, clinically minor.
- feature_051 x feature_038: interaction +0.034 (p = 0.08). Effects on PFS are
  approximately additive across these two features.
- feature_071 (ordinal 0-10): no monotone trend; adjusted slope -0.0007 mo/unit,
  p = 0.65. No outcome association.

SUPPORTED VS REFUTED
--------------------
SUPPORTED:
- feature_078 strong positive prognostic (h2.1)
- feature_057 dose-response negative (h3.1)
- feature_051 negative effect (h4.1)
- feature_038 positive effect (h5.1)
- feature_092 positive prognostic (h6.1), feature_099 negative prognostic (h6.2)
- feature_009 / feature_006 small negative prognostic (h7.1, h7.2)
- feature_013 / feature_043 / feature_109 as feature_038-resistance modifiers
  (h10.1, h10.2, h11.1, h12.1, h12.2)
- feature_013 / feature_043 / feature_109 are non-prognostic in absence of
  feature_038 (h13.1, h13.2, h22.1)
- No race heterogeneity in feature_038 or feature_051 effects (h14.1, h16.1)
- feature_071 has no PFS association (h19.1)
- feature_038 and feature_051 effects are additive (h18.1)
- Multivariable model explains ~90% of PFS variance (Adj R^2 = 0.898; h24.1)
- No meaningful demographic disparities (h25.1)

REFUTED / NOT SUPPORTED:
- Mean PFS differs by race (h8.1) -- NS
- Mean PFS differs by insurance (h8.2) -- NS
- feature_038 receipt differs by race (h9.1) or insurance (h9.2) -- NS
- feature_067 as an independent sensitivity marker (h11.2) -- NS after adjustment

PARTIALLY SUPPORTED:
- feature_038 benefit varies by insurance (h15.1): only uninsured-vs-medicaid
  contrast significant (-0.38 mo, p=0.002); other contrasts NS.
- Other binary features modify feature_038 (h20.1): statistically yes for ~6
  features, but magnitudes <= 0.21 mo are minor.

OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
-------------------
1. PFS in this CRC cohort is driven primarily by feature_078 (age-like; large
   positive), feature_057 (stage-like ordinal; large negative dose-response),
   feature_051 (binary marker; ~ -1.4 mo), and feature_092 (favorable continuous;
   +0.47 mo per unit).

2. feature_038 is a highly effective therapy WHEN GIVEN TO THE RIGHT PATIENTS.
   In patients with all three resistance markers (feature_013, feature_043,
   feature_109) negative, feature_038 confers about +2.8 mo of additional PFS.
   In patients with any of those markers positive, feature_038 has essentially
   no effect.

3. The resistance markers (feature_013, feature_043, feature_109) are PURELY
   PREDICTIVE: they identify who benefits from feature_038 but do not by
   themselves change baseline PFS.

4. There is essentially NO evidence of demographic disparity in this cohort.
   Outcomes, treatment receipt, and treatment effect are uniform across race
   (feature_064). Insurance differences are also negligible, with one minor
   exception: uninsured patients receive a slightly attenuated feature_038
   benefit (+2.44 vs ~+2.8 mo in other groups).

5. A 12-term multivariable model achieves Adj R^2 = 0.898 on the 50,000-patient
   cohort, reproducing the patterns above with high precision.

The dominant clinical message is biomarker-guided therapy: identifying the
~35% of patients who are resistance-marker-negative and reserving feature_038
for them would maximize benefit. The data show that this targeting is not
currently being done -- ~20% of biomarker-positive patients also receive
feature_038, with no measurable benefit -- but the over-treatment is uniform
across demographic groups.
