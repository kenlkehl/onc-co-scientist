Oncology dataset ds001_crc — Analysis summary (25 iterations)
================================================================

Dataset: 50,000 anonymized patient records, 123 de-identified features
(80 binary, 6 ordinal 5-level, 1 ordinal 11-level, 35 continuous, 2
nominal categoricals — feature_064 with 5 levels including
white/hispanic/black/asian/other and feature_018 with 4 levels including
medicare/private/medicaid/uninsured), and a single continuous outcome
pfs_months (mean 4.31, sd 2.29, range 0.00–16.25). All features were
analyzed against pfs_months across 25 iterations of propose-test-refine.

----------------------------------------------------------------
1. Headline findings (main effects)
----------------------------------------------------------------

(a) feature_078 (continuous, range 30–90, mean 65) is the single
strongest predictor of pfs_months. Spearman rho = +0.785, p effectively 0
(Iteration 1). Pearson r = +0.766. Quartile means of pfs_months by
feature_078 quartile rise monotonically: Q1 = 2.09, Q2 = 3.71, Q3 = 4.90,
Q4 = 6.59 — a 4.5-month gradient over the four quartiles. The direction
is positive: higher feature_078 maps to LONGER PFS.

(b) feature_051 (binary, prevalence 55%) is the single strongest
detrimental predictor. Mean pfs_months = 3.703 (n = 27,481) when
feature_051 = 1 versus 5.055 (n = 22,519) when feature_051 = 0; mean
difference = -1.352 months, Welch p ≈ 0; OLS β = -1.352, p ≈ 0
(Iteration 2). The effect size is large and stable.

(c) feature_038 (binary, prevalence 20%) is the single strongest
beneficial predictor. Mean pfs_months = 5.089 (n = 10,022) when
feature_038 = 1 versus 4.117 (n = 39,978) when feature_038 = 0; mean
difference = +0.972 months, p ≈ 1.7e-216 (Iteration 3). The pattern,
prevalence and direction are consistent with feature_038 being a
beneficial therapy assignment or a favorable biomarker.

(d) feature_057 (3-level ordinal 0/1/2) shows a strong monotonic
dose-response: mean pfs_months = 5.27, 4.05, 2.91 across levels 0/1/2,
ANOVA F = 3,529, p ≈ 0; Spearman rho = -0.36, p ≈ 0 (Iteration 4). The
1.18-month drop per level (5.27 → 4.05 → 2.91) and the clean monotonic
gradient suggest feature_057 is a stage- or burden-like ordinal.

(e) Smaller but real main effects: feature_099 (negative continuous, rho
= -0.12, p = 5.6e-160, n = 50,000), feature_092 (positive continuous,
rho = +0.10, p = 4.4e-117), feature_013 (binary, mean diff = -0.327, p
= 1.9e-58), feature_043 (binary, mean diff = -0.305, p = 1.0e-49),
feature_009 (right-skewed continuous, rho = -0.047, p = 1.3e-25),
feature_109 (rare binary, prev = 4.5%, mean diff = -0.228, p = 3.5e-7),
feature_067 (rare binary, prev = 3%, mean diff = +0.221, p = 7.3e-4)
(see Iteration 14 and the screen baked into Iterations 15–16).

----------------------------------------------------------------
2. Predictors that did NOT pan out
----------------------------------------------------------------

(a) The 5-level ordinals feature_025, feature_075, feature_026,
feature_096, and feature_033 (Iteration 5) showed no monotonic
association with pfs_months — every Spearman rho was within ±0.007 with
p ≥ 0.15. These appear to be irrelevant for PFS in this cohort despite
their orderable structure.

(b) Race (feature_064) showed no main-effect difference in pfs_months
across white/hispanic/black/asian/other (Iteration 6): mean PFS spans
4.27–4.41 across categories, ANOVA F = 0.87, p = 0.48. Each
race-vs-white pairwise comparison was non-significant. This is a notable
NEGATIVE finding for demographic disparity at the level of the outcome.

(c) Insurance (feature_018) showed no main-effect difference in
pfs_months across medicare/private/medicaid/uninsured (Iteration 7):
range 4.25–4.33, ANOVA F = 1.47, p = 0.22. Like race, insurance does
not move the outcome on its own.

(d) Six demographic-balance binary features (feature_007, feature_066,
feature_002, feature_047, feature_032, feature_063 — possibly sex,
laterality, smoking status, or similar) all gave |mean diff| < 0.06 and
p ≥ 0.08 (Iteration 22). None is a meaningful PFS predictor.

(e) The vast majority of the lab/vital-like continuous features
(feature_006, feature_070, feature_044, feature_084, feature_028,
feature_065, feature_118, feature_056, feature_103, feature_094,
feature_121, feature_019, feature_059, feature_003, feature_010,
feature_101, feature_020, feature_090, feature_119, feature_054,
feature_062, feature_031, feature_082, feature_097, feature_016,
feature_042, feature_123, feature_055, feature_011, feature_014) are
essentially noise relative to PFS (Iteration 16): only 4 of 30 reached
p < 0.05 — feature_070 (rho = -0.014, p = 2.4e-3), feature_028 (rho =
-0.012, p = 6.5e-3), feature_103 (rho = +0.009, p = 0.043), feature_094
(rho = -0.009, p = 0.040). Effect magnitudes are clinically negligible
even when statistically detectable, reflecting the n = 50,000 sample
size.

(f) Among the rare binary markers (Iteration 14), feature_035
(prev = 5%), feature_036 (prev = 3%), and feature_027 (prev = 0.5%) had
no detectable association.

----------------------------------------------------------------
3. Multivariable structure
----------------------------------------------------------------

A multivariable OLS containing the 11 strongest univariate predictors
(feature_078, feature_057, feature_051, feature_038, feature_099,
feature_092, feature_013, feature_043, feature_009, feature_109,
feature_067) plus race and insurance reaches R² = 0.858 (Iteration 8) —
a striking amount of variance explained for a clinical PFS outcome.
This means the 11 variables collectively capture almost all of the
predictable variation in pfs_months in this dataset; remaining residuals
are essentially noise. Each of feature_078, feature_051, feature_038,
feature_057, feature_099, feature_092, feature_013, feature_043,
feature_009, feature_109, feature_067 retained an independent
coefficient with the same sign as its univariate association and p < 1e-6.

----------------------------------------------------------------
4. Interaction and subgroup analyses
----------------------------------------------------------------

(a) feature_038 × feature_051 (Iteration 9): The benefit of feature_038
in feature_051 = 0 patients = +0.94 months; in feature_051 = 1 patients
= +1.01 months; interaction coefficient β = +0.07, p = 0.15. NOT
significant — feature_038 confers a similar absolute benefit regardless
of feature_051 status. (In the fully adjusted multivariable model of
Iteration 24, feature_051:feature_038 = +0.036, p = 0.066 — borderline
but again not significant.)

(b) feature_038 × feature_057 (Iteration 12): Effect of feature_038 by
feature_057 level was +0.93, +0.99, +0.90 across levels 0/1/2; joint
interaction F-test p = 0.23. No effect modification by feature_057
severity.

(c) feature_038 × feature_078 (Iteration 18): Effect of feature_038
across feature_078 quartiles was +1.03, +0.89, +0.96, +1.04 (Q1–Q4); the
continuous interaction term was small (β = -0.0017, p = 0.28). No
meaningful effect modification by feature_078.

(d) feature_038 × feature_092 (Iteration 13): Univariate interaction
β = -0.077, p = 0.13 — non-significant on its own. HOWEVER, in the fully
adjusted multivariable model (Iteration 24), feature_092:feature_038 =
-0.079, p = 4.1e-5. The interaction emerges as significant once the
model controls for feature_078 and feature_057 (which absorb confounded
variance), suggesting the feature_038 benefit is somewhat ATTENUATED at
higher feature_092 — modest effect modification.

(e) Race × feature_038 (Iteration 10): The benefit of feature_038
within each race group was +0.92 (white), +1.11 (hispanic), +1.09
(asian), +0.96 (black), +1.21 (other). Joint interaction F-test p =
0.032 (borderline). Although interaction is statistically detectable,
all subgroups still benefit substantially; the pattern is one of
quantitative, not qualitative, variation.

(f) Insurance × feature_038 (Iteration 11): Effects were +0.97
(medicare), +0.93 (private), +0.93 (uninsured), +1.07 (medicaid). Joint
interaction p = 0.16. No insurance-based effect modification.

(g) Race × feature_051 (Iteration 19): Effects of feature_051 within
race were -1.34 (white), -1.39 (hispanic), -1.33 (asian), -1.34 (black),
-1.27 (other). Joint interaction p = 0.69. The harm of feature_051 is
consistent across race.

(h) Three-way feature_038 × feature_051 × race (Iteration 21): Joint
3-way interaction F = 1.54, p = 0.19 — not significant. Within every
race × feature_051 stratum, feature_038 = 1 confers a positive PFS
advantage of ~+0.83 to +1.40 months, all p < 0.005. Effect is
remarkably uniform: feature_038 helps every subgroup tested.

----------------------------------------------------------------
5. Equity / disparities probe
----------------------------------------------------------------

(a) Adjusted race coefficients (Iteration 17): After adjusting for the
strongest clinical predictors, every race-vs-white coefficient is
within ±0.022 months and none reach p < 0.05. No residual disparity in
PFS by race after clinical adjustment.

(b) Adjusted insurance coefficients (Iteration 17): Every
insurance-vs-reference coefficient is within ±0.009 months and p ≥ 0.5.
No residual disparity by insurance.

(c) Differential access to feature_038 (Iteration 20): Prevalence of
feature_038 by race was 0.21 (asian), 0.20 (black), 0.20 (hispanic),
0.19 (other), 0.20 (white) — chi-square p = 0.55. Prevalence by
insurance was 0.20 (medicaid), 0.20 (medicare), 0.20 (private), 0.19
(uninsured) — chi-square p = 0.59. There is no detectable inequity in
exposure to the beneficial marker.

(d) Mediation-style check (Iteration 23): Adding feature_038 to a
race + insurance + clinical model shifted race coefficients by at most
±0.018 and insurance by at most ±0.007. Because the underlying
race/insurance effects are themselves near zero, this is consistent
with no mediation through differential feature_038 assignment.

OVERALL EQUITY READ: race and insurance neither influence pfs_months
on their own, nor are they confounded with feature_038 access, nor do
they modify treatment effects in a clinically meaningful way. This is
a uniformly favorable equity finding for the cohort.

----------------------------------------------------------------
6. Sensitivity / replication (Iteration 25)
----------------------------------------------------------------

Random split-half validation produced essentially identical estimates:
- Half A: feature_038 effect = +0.953, feature_051 effect = -1.333,
  feature_078 rho = +0.787.
- Half B: feature_038 effect = +0.991, feature_051 effect = -1.371,
  feature_078 rho = +0.783.
The three top main effects replicate in independent random halves, so
they are not artifacts of overfitting or sampling variance.

Shapiro-Wilk on a 5,000-row sample of multivariable residuals returned
p ≈ 3e-72 — at this sample size even tiny departures are flagged, so
this only indicates that residuals are not perfectly Gaussian; it does
not undermine the OLS point estimates, which can be read as descriptive
mean-shifts regardless.

----------------------------------------------------------------
7. Bottom line
----------------------------------------------------------------

In this 50,000-patient cohort, three signals dominate variation in
pfs_months: feature_078 (continuous; +0.79 Spearman), feature_051
(binary harm; -1.35 months), and feature_038 (binary benefit; +0.97
months), with feature_057 (ordinal stage-like; -1.18 months per level)
also major. A multivariable model with 11 features explains 86% of the
variance in PFS — exceptionally high for a clinical outcome.

Treatment-effect-modification analyses show that feature_038's benefit
is remarkably consistent: it operates with similar magnitude across
levels of feature_051, feature_057, feature_078 quartile, race,
insurance, and within every race × feature_051 cell tested. The only
significant modifier in the multivariable framework was feature_092
(continuous biomarker), which attenuates the feature_038 benefit at
higher levels (interaction p = 4.1e-5 in the adjusted model).

Demographic factors (race, insurance, six other balanced binaries) do
not predict pfs_months on their own, do not differ in feature_038
prevalence, and do not introduce residual disparity after clinical
adjustment. The headline equity finding is that feature_038's benefit
is uniformly distributed across every demographic stratum we examined.

Hypotheses supported (10): h1.1, h2.1, h3.1, h4.1, h8.1, h14.1
(partial), h15.1, h16.1 (the framing — most lab features ARE noise),
h25.1, plus h13.1 in its multivariable form (Iteration 24).
Hypotheses refuted (12): h5.1, h6.1, h7.1, h9.1, h11.1, h12.1, h17.1,
h17.2, h18.1, h19.1, h20.1, h20.2, h21.1, h22.1, h23.1, h23.2.
The clinical biology and demographics tell different stories: clinical
features (feature_078, feature_051, feature_038, feature_057) carry the
prognostic and treatment-effect signal; demographics carry essentially
no independent or interactive signal in this cohort.
