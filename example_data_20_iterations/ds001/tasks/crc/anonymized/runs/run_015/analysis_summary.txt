Analysis summary for ds001_crc (50,000 patients, outcome = pfs_months)
=====================================================================

Approach
--------
The bundle exposes 123 de-identified features (3 categorical, 7 multi-level
ordinal, 80 binary, 33 continuous) and a single outcome, pfs_months
(progression-free survival in months; mean 4.31, SD 2.29, range 0-16.25).
I worked through 25 iterations of propose-test-refine, beginning with a
univariate screen of every feature, then deep-dived the strongest signals,
tested categorical and ordinal subgroups, examined a curated set of
interactions (including stage x marker, marker x marker, age-like x marker,
race x marker and insurance x marker for fairness), and ended with a fully
adjusted multivariate model that combined main effects and the supported
interactions.

Iterations 1-13: main effects
-----------------------------
A Spearman / one-way-ANOVA univariate screen ranked features by p-value
against pfs_months. Out of 123 candidates, only ~10 features carried
material signal:

  * feature_078 (continuous, range 30-90, mean 65) was the dominant
    predictor: rho = +0.785 with PFS, OLS slope +0.176 months per unit,
    R^2 = 0.587 by itself. Mean PFS rose monotonically across quintiles
    from 1.86 (lowest) to 6.83 (highest). The relationship is essentially
    linear and clinically enormous - this single variable explains the
    bulk of the outcome variance and behaves like an age-, comorbidity-,
    or follow-up-time-like driver.

  * feature_057 (ordinal 0/1/2; n = 17,592 / 24,971 / 7,437) showed a clean
    monotonic decline in mean PFS: 5.27 -> 4.05 -> 2.91 months. ANOVA
    F = 3,529, p ~ 0; Spearman rho = -0.36. This is consistent with a
    stage- or grade-like clinical variable.

  * feature_051 (binary, prevalence 0.55) was the most adverse binary
    marker: PFS 3.70 vs 5.06 (delta -1.35 months, p ~ 0).

  * feature_038 (binary, prevalence 0.20) was the most favorable binary
    marker: PFS 5.09 vs 4.12 (delta +0.97 months, p ~ 0).

  * feature_099 (continuous, range 0-24.6) and feature_009 (continuous,
    range 0-777) were both modestly negative (rho ~ -0.12 and -0.05
    respectively); feature_092 (continuous, 1.5-5.5, plausibly a serum
    protein) was modestly positive (rho ~ +0.10).

  * feature_013 and feature_043 (binaries, prevalences 0.42 and 0.35)
    were both modestly negative (delta -0.33 and -0.31, p < 1e-45).

  * feature_109 (binary, prevalence 0.045) carried a small but real
    unfavorable signal that strengthened on adjustment (-0.21 months,
    p = 8e-23).

In contrast, the demographic categoricals showed no main effect on PFS:
  * Race (feature_064, 5 levels): means 4.27-4.41 months, ANOVA p = 0.48.
  * Insurance (feature_018, 4 levels): means 4.25-4.33, ANOVA p = 0.22.

Likewise the other multi-level ordinal features (feature_025, _026, _033,
_071, _075, _096) all had |rho| <= 0.007 and p >= 0.15 - i.e., they are
essentially noise variables in this cohort. feature_057 is the single
ordinal feature carrying the stage-like prognostic signal.

Iteration 14: multivariate main-effects model
---------------------------------------------
A nine-feature OLS regression of pfs_months on the strongest predictors
yielded an adjusted R^2 of 0.857. All nine coefficients were independent
and highly significant (|t| >= 21):
    feature_078  +0.176  per unit
    feature_057  -1.172  per ordinal level
    feature_051  -1.362
    feature_038  +0.942
    feature_099  -0.076  per unit
    feature_092  +0.470  per unit
    feature_013  -0.327
    feature_009  -0.005  per unit
    feature_043  -0.286
The size and direction of every coefficient matched the univariate result,
confirming none of the top signals are confounded artifacts.

Iterations 15-20: interaction analyses
--------------------------------------
Stage- (feature_057) and the dominant continuous driver (feature_078)
modify the binary-marker effects only modestly:

  * feature_078 x feature_057 - significant negative interaction at the
    highest ordinal level (-0.017 per unit, p = 8e-23). The slope of PFS
    on feature_078 is +0.180 at level 0 and +0.163 at level 2, i.e.,
    favorable feature_078 effect is slightly attenuated in the
    worst-stage subgroup.

  * feature_051 x feature_057 - small but significant attenuation:
    stratum-specific deltas were -1.40 / -1.35 / -1.27 at levels 0/1/2.
    The unfavorable impact of feature_051 is largest in patients with
    earlier-stage feature_057 (interaction p = 0.001 for the highest
    level), but in absolute terms the effect is dominant in every
    stratum.

  * feature_038 x feature_057 - significant negative interaction. Benefit
    of feature_038 = +0.93 / +1.00 / +0.90 at levels 0/1/2 (interaction
    p = 0.001 vs level 0). Practically, the favorable effect is preserved
    across stages.

  * feature_038 x feature_051 - NOT significant (p = 0.19). Benefits of
    feature_038 (+0.94 within feature_051=0 vs +1.01 within feature_051=1)
    are essentially identical; the two markers act additively.

  * feature_078 x feature_051 - significant (-0.0070 per unit, p = 2e-15).
    The unfavorable effect of feature_051 grows slightly with feature_078,
    but the absolute slope difference is tiny.

  * feature_078 x feature_038 - NOT significant (p = 0.27). Favorable
    feature_038 effect is constant across the feature_078 range.

Iterations 21-23: fairness / disparities
----------------------------------------
A particular concern in real-world oncology data is whether favorable
treatments confer the same benefit across race and insurance. Stratified
analyses + formal nested F-tests showed:

  * feature_038 effect by race (feature_064): deltas +0.92 white, +1.11
    hispanic, +1.09 asian, +0.97 black, +1.22 other; interaction F = 2.12,
    p = 0.076. Not significant.
  * feature_051 effect by race: deltas -1.34 white, -1.39 hispanic, -1.33
    asian, -1.35 black, -1.52 other; interaction F = 0.31, p = 0.87.
    Highly homogeneous.
  * feature_038 effect by insurance (feature_018): deltas +0.97 medicare,
    +0.94 private, +0.93 uninsured, +1.11 medicaid; interaction F = 1.89,
    p = 0.13. Not significant.

In short, the favorable and unfavorable marker effects are statistically
homogeneous across racial and insurance subgroups in this cohort - we did
not detect bias in either direction.

Iteration 24: do small-effect markers carry real independent signal?
--------------------------------------------------------------------
After adjusting for the four largest covariates (feature_078, feature_057,
feature_051, feature_038), eight smaller-effect features were re-tested:
  * feature_109 -0.21 (p = 8e-23) - SIGNIFICANT, real adverse marker
  * feature_067 +0.17 (p = 2e-11) - SIGNIFICANT, real favorable marker
  * feature_089 +0.03 (p = 0.03)  - borderline
  * feature_028, _094, _103, _070, _102 - NS after adjustment.
This refined the picture: the cohort contains roughly 11 independently
informative variables; the rest are effectively noise.

Iteration 25: full model and overall conclusion
-----------------------------------------------
A model that augments the 9 main effects with the three supported
interactions (feature_078 x feature_051, feature_057 x feature_051,
feature_057 x feature_038) raised adj R^2 from 0.8574 to 0.8576
(nested F = 21.6 across 5 interaction df, p = 1.3e-21). The interactions
are statistically real but contribute only a few thousandths of variance;
PFS in this cohort is overwhelmingly explained by additive main effects.

Take-home conclusions
---------------------
1. feature_078 is the single strongest determinant of PFS, behaving as a
   continuous, near-linear driver that explains ~59% of variance on its
   own.
2. feature_057 is a stage-like ordinal that subtracts ~1.2 months of PFS
   per level.
3. feature_051 (-1.4 months) and feature_038 (+0.9 months) are the two
   strongest binary prognostic markers, and they act independently of
   each other (no significant interaction).
4. feature_099, feature_092, feature_013, feature_009, feature_043,
   feature_109, and feature_067 contribute smaller but independent
   signal in the adjusted model.
5. Demographic features (race, insurance) and the other multi-level
   ordinal features show no main effect and no interaction with the
   prognostic / treatment-like markers - this cohort does not exhibit
   measurable racial or insurance-based disparities in PFS or in
   treatment-effect modification.
6. The dataset is dominated by additive main effects; effect-modification
   is statistically detectable but practically tiny.
