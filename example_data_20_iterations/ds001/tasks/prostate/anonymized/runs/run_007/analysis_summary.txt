Analysis summary — ds001_prostate (n = 50,000; outcome: pfs_months)
================================================================

Approach
--------
The analysis proceeded across 25 iterations of propose / test / refine. The
first six iterations characterized the strongest unadjusted main effects of
clinical features on pfs_months, picking up the dominant signal from
correlations and group-mean comparisons. Iteration 7 consolidated those
findings into a multivariable linear model. Iterations 8-11 tested
sociodemographic disparities (race, insurance) both unadjusted and adjusted
for clinical baseline. Iterations 12-15 followed up on previously
uncharacterized binary features that appeared after adjustment to be
treatment indicators with positive effects, and discovered a strong positive
multiplicative interaction between two of them. Iterations 16-17 tested
whether that combination benefit was delivered equitably across race and
insurance. Iterations 18-20 explored interactions among the dominant
clinical features themselves. Iterations 21-24 audited additional
features that displayed small but robust adjusted effects. Iteration 25
returned to the disparity question by checking whether access to the
favorable combination treatment was distributed equally by race.

Dominant clinical predictors of pfs_months
------------------------------------------
Six baseline features account for almost all explainable variation in
pfs_months (multivariable adjusted R^2 = 0.946):

  feature_078 (continuous, range 30-90, mean ~65, plausibly age in years):
      OLS coefficient +0.174 per unit (p ~ 0). This is the single largest
      driver — Pearson r with pfs_months = +0.856 unadjusted. Higher
      feature_078 is associated with markedly longer PFS in this cohort.
      [SUPPORTED: H1.]

  feature_057 (3-level integer 0/1/2, plausibly performance status):
      Adjusted coefficient -1.151 per level (p ~ 0). Mean PFS drops
      monotonically across levels 0/1/2 (~4.7 -> 3.5 -> 2.3 months).
      [SUPPORTED: H2.]

  feature_013 (continuous, right-skewed, range 0.1-3622, plausibly PSA):
      Adjusted coefficient -0.0027 per unit (p ~ 0). Higher feature_013
      predicts shorter PFS. [SUPPORTED: H3.]

  feature_006 (continuous, range 0-24): adjusted coefficient -0.076 per
      unit (p ~ 0). Higher feature_006 predicts shorter PFS, consistent
      with a disease-burden interpretation. [SUPPORTED: H4.]

  feature_009 (continuous, range 1.5-5.5, plausibly serum albumin):
      adjusted coefficient +0.479 per unit (p ~ 0). Higher feature_009
      predicts longer PFS. [SUPPORTED: H5.]

  feature_051 (binary biomarker, prevalence ~55%): adjusted coefficient
      -0.376 (p ~ 0). The strongest binary main-effect predictor in the
      data; presence is associated with ~0.5 months shorter unadjusted
      mean PFS. [SUPPORTED: H6.]

The joint multivariable model (H7) confirms each effect is independent
in sign and magnitude.

Sociodemographic disparities
----------------------------
Both unadjusted (H8, H9) and adjusted (H10, H11) tests of mean PFS across
race (feature_018) and insurance (feature_045) categories were null:

  Race unadjusted: F(4, 49995) = 0.564, p = 0.69. Adjusted: F(4, 49989)
      = 0.114, p = 0.978. All adjusted contrasts vs. white reference are
      < 0.01 month and statistically indistinguishable from zero.
  Insurance unadjusted: F(3, 49996) = 0.344, p = 0.79. Adjusted:
      F(3, 49990) = 0.66, p = 0.575.

[REFUTED: H8, H9, H10, H11.] On these two demographic axes, this cohort
shows no evidence of PFS disparity, with or without clinical adjustment.

Treatment indicators and a strong combination effect
----------------------------------------------------
Two binary features, feature_109 and feature_039, each showed small but
highly significant positive adjusted effects on PFS (~ +0.15 months;
H12, H13 both SUPPORTED). Cross-tabulation revealed they are nearly
independent (each ~10% prevalence) with 529 patients positive for both.
Iteration 14 tested for interaction:

  Mean PFS by cell:
      109=0, 039=0: 3.74 months (n = 40,435)
      109=1 alone:  3.67 months (n = 4,467)
      039=1 alone:  3.67 months (n = 4,569)
      both=1:       5.22 months (n =   529)

  Adjusted main 109: +0.003 (p = 0.63); main 039: -0.003 (p = 0.66);
  interaction 109:039: +1.440 months (p ~ 0).

This is a very large, very robust positive interaction — neither feature
on its own meaningfully changes PFS, but their combination produces
about 1.4 months of additional PFS. This pattern is the signature of a
combination therapy whose benefit emerges only when both components are
present. [SUPPORTED: H14.]

Iteration 15 confirmed the combination benefit is preserved within both
strata of feature_051 (combo effect +1.55 months in feature_051=0,
+1.45 months in feature_051=1; both p < 1e-13). [SUPPORTED: H15.]

Was the combination benefit delivered equitably?
------------------------------------------------
H16 tested combination x race interaction. Race-stratified unadjusted
effects: white +1.53, black +1.75, hispanic +0.99, asian +1.44 months.
The combo benefit is large and significant in every race category;
the apparent attenuation in hispanic patients (interaction term p ~ 0.007
vs asian reference) is borderline given multiple comparisons and modest
subgroup n. Overall: clinically meaningful benefit in all races; weak
evidence of effect modification. [PARTIALLY SUPPORTED: H16.]

H17 tested combination x insurance interaction. The combination benefit
in private-insurance patients is statistically smaller than in medicaid
patients (interaction -0.29, p < 0.001), though the absolute benefit
remains > 1 month in all insurance categories. [SUPPORTED: H17.]

H25 closed the loop: receipt of the combination is statistically
indistinguishable across race (chi^2 = 1.76, p = 0.78). The absence of
an overall racial PFS gap is not masking a hidden access disparity in
this cohort. [SUPPORTED: H25.]

Interactions among clinical features
------------------------------------
H18: feature_051 x feature_057 (presence of biomarker x performance
status). Interaction coef +0.028 per (051 * 057) (p = 9e-6). Statistically
robust but small — feature_051 imposes a similar PFS penalty (~ -0.5
months) at all PS levels. [SUPPORTED in direction; clinically modest.]

H19: feature_051 x feature_078 (biomarker x age). Interaction -0.0043 per
unit (p ~ 1e-23). The adverse impact of feature_051 grows with age but
remains < 0.5 months across the full age range. [SUPPORTED.]

H20: feature_013 x feature_051 (PSA-like x biomarker). Interaction +0.0153
(p ~ 0). The negative slope of PFS on feature_013 is much steeper in
feature_051=0 patients than in feature_051=1 — i.e., feature_013 is a
much weaker prognostic signal once feature_051 is present. [SUPPORTED;
the largest within-clinical interaction observed.]

Other features
--------------
H21: feature_067 (5-level, range 6-10, plausibly Gleason). Adjusted
coefficient -0.011 per unit (p = 1.5e-7), small but real. [SUPPORTED.]

H22: feature_092 (range 48-830). Effectively no unadjusted correlation
(r = -0.017) but a robust adjusted effect (-0.0005 per unit, p = 4.7e-81),
suggesting suppression by collinear clinical features. [SUPPORTED.]

H23: feature_027 (binary, ~60%). Adjusted +0.022 months PFS (p = 3e-7);
unadjusted t-test was non-significant (p = 0.27). [SUPPORTED with
adjustment.]

H24: feature_043 (binary, ~20%). Adjusted -0.022 months PFS (p = 3.7e-5).
[SUPPORTED.]

Overall conclusions
-------------------
1. PFS in this 50,000-patient cohort is overwhelmingly determined by six
   baseline clinical features (multivariable adjusted R^2 = 0.946):
   feature_078 (age-like), feature_057 (PS-like), feature_013 (lab,
   PSA-like), feature_006 (burden-like), feature_009 (albumin-like),
   and the binary biomarker feature_051.

2. There is no evidence of pfs_months disparity by race (feature_018) or
   insurance (feature_045), either before or after clinical adjustment.

3. Two binary features (feature_109 and feature_039) display a striking
   positive interaction: alone, neither matters; together they produce
   ~+1.4 months of PFS. This pattern is the most plausible candidate for
   an effective combination treatment in the dataset.

4. The combination benefit is preserved across feature_051 strata,
   delivered with similar frequency across races, and produces large
   absolute benefit in every demographic subgroup. There is weak evidence
   of attenuated benefit in hispanic patients and in private-insurance
   patients, but these are not large enough to reverse the overall
   conclusion that the combination is broadly effective.

5. Several smaller adjusted effects (feature_067, feature_092,
   feature_027, feature_043) are real but clinically modest; they
   contribute to model fit but not to gross prognosis.

6. The most clinically actionable summary: in this cohort, the strongest
   driver of progression-free survival improvement that is plausibly
   modifiable is exposure to feature_109 + feature_039 together. The
   strongest negative prognostic signals are feature_057 (PS), feature_051,
   feature_013 (PSA-like), and feature_006 (burden-like).
