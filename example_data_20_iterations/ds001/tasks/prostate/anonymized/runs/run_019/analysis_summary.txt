ANALYSIS SUMMARY — ds001_prostate (50,000 patients, 122 anonymized features, outcome pfs_months)
=================================================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
The cohort comprises 50,000 records with progression-free survival (pfs_months) ranging 0–14.6 months
(mean 3.74, median 3.72). I screened all 122 features (mix of binary, ordinal, continuous, plus race
and insurance categoricals), examined main effects, then tested multivariable adjustment, demographic
disparities, two-way clinical-by-clinical interactions, and treatment-feature × subgroup interactions.
feature_007 is constant in this dataset and was excluded.

KEY MAIN-EFFECT PREDICTORS OF pfs_months
----------------------------------------
1. feature_078 (continuous, 30–90, mean 65) — strongest single predictor of PFS. Each one-unit
   increase yields +0.174 mo of PFS (p<1e-300, univariable R^2=0.73; Spearman ρ=+0.85). Direction is
   the OPPOSITE of what one would naively expect if this were patient age in a real prostate-cancer
   cohort, so the variable is more likely a favorable-prognosis lab/biomarker than chronological age.

2. feature_057 (3-level ordinal 0/1/2) — strongest categorical predictor. Mean PFS drops monotonically:
   4.68 → 3.49 → 2.38 mo. Adjusted coefficient at level 2 is −2.29 mo vs level 0 (p<1e-300). Behaves
   like a disease-stage / severity indicator.

3. feature_013 (right-skewed continuous, 0.12–3622) — log-transformed, each unit reduces PFS by 0.38 mo
   (p<1e-300; Spearman ρ=−0.22). Adverse biomarker (e.g., burden of disease).

4. feature_009 (continuous 1.5–5.5) — favorable; +0.49 mo per unit (p~1e-166).

5. feature_006 (continuous 0–24.6) — adverse; −0.075 mo per unit (p~1e-225).

6. feature_067 (5-level ordinal 6–10, the values are evocative of Gleason grade) — surprisingly NOT
   associated with PFS (p=0.49, range across levels 0.05 mo). After multivariable adjustment the term
   becomes mildly negative (p~1e-12) but the magnitude is trivial (~−0.011 mo per level).

A parsimonious model with these terms reaches adjusted R^2 ≈ 0.97 — pfs_months is mostly explained by
a handful of clinical features.

DEMOGRAPHIC FACTORS — NO DETECTABLE DISPARITY IN OUTCOMES
---------------------------------------------------------
Race (feature_018; asian/black/hispanic/white/other) and insurance (feature_045; medicaid/medicare/
private/uninsured) showed essentially flat PFS distributions:

  • Race-stratified mean PFS: asian 3.80, black 3.74, hispanic 3.74, other 3.75, white 3.74 mo
    (range 0.06 mo, ANOVA p=0.69).
  • Insurance-stratified mean PFS: medicaid 3.74, medicare 3.75, private 3.74, uninsured 3.73 mo
    (range 0.03 mo, ANOVA p=0.79).

After adjusting for the clinical predictors above, every race indicator had p>0.66 and every insurance
indicator p>0.88, with adjusted |coefficients| <0.003 mo. Adding C(feature_018) or C(feature_045) to
the final model produced ΔR^2 < 0.0001. There is no residual demographic signal in PFS.

Clinical features themselves were also balanced across race and insurance (feature_078, feature_057,
feature_067, feature_013, feature_006, feature_009 all distributed near-identically across groups),
so the absence of an outcome disparity is not the result of opposing covariate imbalances.

THE CENTRAL FINDING — A TWO-FEATURE SYNERGY
-------------------------------------------
Among the 77 binary features, two stood out after multivariable adjustment:

  • feature_109 (prevalence 10.0%): +0.152 mo, p ~ 2e-183
  • feature_039 (prevalence 10.2%): +0.149 mo, p ~ 3e-177

But that adjusted-additive view is misleading. When the interaction is fitted explicitly:

  pfs_months ~ feature_109 * feature_039 + clinical covariates

the main effects collapse to zero (feature_109 coef ≈ 0.001, p=0.90; feature_039 coef ≈ 0.000, p=0.94)
and the entire benefit lives in the interaction term, +1.434 mo (p<1e-300). Cell-mean confirmation:

       feature_039=0     feature_039=1
  109=0    3.74 (n=40,435)   3.67 (n=4,569)
  109=1    3.67 (n=4,467)    5.22 (n=529)

In other words, neither flag alone shifts PFS — the prolongation only appears when both are
co-occurring (≈1% of the cohort). The pattern is consistent with two interacting therapies that are
clinically inert as monotherapy but synergistic in combination, or two co-required biomarkers that
unlock a favorable trajectory.

EQUITY OF ACCESS TO THE COMBO
-----------------------------
Receipt of the combo (feature_109=1 AND feature_039=1) is essentially uniform across demographics:

  • By race: asian 0.99%, black 0.96%, hispanic 1.03%, other 0.82%, white 1.10% (range 0.28 pp; n.s.).
  • By insurance: medicaid 1.10%, medicare 1.06%, private 1.02%, uninsured 1.20% (n.s.).

So while the combo is rare, it is delivered roughly equally — there is no evidence of a disparity in
access by race or insurance status.

HETEROGENEITY OF THE COMBO BENEFIT
----------------------------------
Although access is equal, the magnitude of benefit is statistically heterogeneous:

  • Combo × race (asian = reference): black −0.26 mo (p=0.0004), hispanic −0.23 mo (p=0.0009),
    white −0.19 mo (p=0.002), other +0.33 mo (p=0.01). Within-race raw deltas of combo+ vs combo−:
    asian +1.44 (n=30), black +1.75 (n=58), hispanic +0.99 (n=78), other +2.81 (n=8), white +1.53
    (n=355). Several cells are tiny (asian, other), so point estimates are unstable; the regression
    p-values are real but should be interpreted with the small-n caveat.

  • Combo × insurance (medicaid = reference): medicare −0.118 (p=0.006), private −0.270 (p=4.6e-10),
    uninsured −0.030 (p=0.70). The combo's PFS gain is larger among Medicaid patients than among
    privately insured — the opposite of the typical "private payers do better" pattern.

  • Combo × feature_057 (severity): level 1 −0.116 mo (p=2e-4), level 2 +0.033 mo (p=0.44). Direction
    is mixed — no clean monotone severity gradient.

  • Combo × feature_078: −0.0069 mo per unit (p=4e-7) — the benefit attenuates modestly with rising
    feature_078.

CLINICAL × CLINICAL INTERACTIONS
--------------------------------
Several robust interactions among the strongest predictors:

  • feature_078 × feature_057 — slope of feature_078 on PFS is much flatter at higher severity
    (interaction coef at level 2 = −0.021, p<1e-300).
  • feature_078 × log1p(feature_013) — interaction = −0.0034 (p~3.6e-151); the favorable feature_078
    effect is attenuated by adverse feature_013.
  • C(feature_057) × log1p(feature_013) — interaction at level 2 = +0.052 (p~2e-38); the negative
    impact of feature_013 is dampened in already-poor-prognosis level-2 patients (likely a
    floor / reduced remaining variance).
  • feature_009 × feature_078 — interaction = +0.0043 (p~5e-42); the two favorable effects compound.
  • feature_006 × feature_057 — interaction at level 2 = +0.0088 (p~5e-12); adverse effect of
    feature_006 is somewhat attenuated at higher severity.

OTHER FEATURES — NULL
---------------------
The multilevel ordinal features feature_026 (0–10), feature_016, feature_071, feature_096, feature_033,
feature_064 (each 0–4), and feature_005 (6–22) showed at most 0.16-mo group-mean range and Spearman
|ρ| < 0.013 — none has a clinically meaningful main-effect signal. Among the 77 binary features, only
feature_027 (~60% prevalence) reached an adjusted effect size > 0.02 mo (+0.025, p~1e-14); other
nominal-significance binaries (feature_043, feature_121, feature_058) all have |effects| < 0.026 mo
and are unlikely to be clinically important.

OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
-------------------
1. PFS in this cohort is determined primarily by a small set of clinical and biomarker variables —
   feature_078 (favorable, very strong), feature_057 (severity), log(feature_013), feature_006,
   and feature_009 — explaining ~97% of the variance.

2. feature_067 — although structured like a Gleason-grade ordinal — is essentially uninformative
   here; the canonical pathologic-grade interpretation does not apply.

3. The dominant treatment-like signal is a synergistic interaction between feature_109 and
   feature_039: each is null on its own; together they confer ~1.5 months of additional PFS.

4. There is no detectable racial/ethnic or insurance-related disparity in either pfs_months or in
   receipt of the combo. Magnitude of combo benefit, however, does show statistical heterogeneity
   across both race and insurance — small subgroup sizes argue for cautious interpretation.

5. Several clinical-by-clinical interactions are detectable (feature_078×feature_057,
   feature_078×log(feature_013), feature_057×log(feature_013), feature_009×feature_078,
   feature_006×feature_057). They reflect attenuation of favorable predictors in higher-severity or
   higher-burden strata.
