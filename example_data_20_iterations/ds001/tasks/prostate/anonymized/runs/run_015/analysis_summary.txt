Analysis Summary — ds001_prostate (N=50,000; outcome: pfs_months)

OVERVIEW
========
The dataset contains 50,000 prostate cancer patients with one outcome (pfs_months;
mean 3.74, range 0–14.6) and 122 anonymized features comprising 75 informative
binary indicators, 8 ordinal/multi-level integer features, 35 continuous features,
race (feature_018: white/hispanic/black/asian/other) and insurance
(feature_045: medicare/private/medicaid/uninsured). Across 25 iterations I tested
55 hypotheses focused on (a) demographic disparities, (b) main prognostic
predictors, (c) treatment-effect heterogeneity by race/insurance/clinical
factors, and (d) treatment–treatment interactions.

ITERATION-BY-ITERATION HYPOTHESES AND RESULTS
============================================

Iter 1 — Race main effects on PFS.
  Hypotheses: black vs white, hispanic vs white, any difference across the 5 race
  groups.
  Results: Welch t-tests gave p>0.88 for both pairwise comparisons; group means
  cluster within ~0.01 mo of each other. NULL — no race-based difference.

Iter 2 — Insurance main effects.
  Hypotheses: medicaid<private, uninsured<private, medicare>private.
  Results: All p>0.36. Largest absolute mean gap is 0.018 mo. NULL.

Iter 3 — feature_078 (range 30–90, suspected age) effect on PFS.
  Hypothesis: positive linear association.
  Result: β=+0.174 mo per unit, p<1e-300. Quartile means: Q1=1.56,
  Q2=3.13, Q3=4.33, Q4=6.00 mo — strict monotonic increase. STRONGLY SUPPORTED.
  Note: direction is opposite the typical "older = worse" pattern, but the
  signal is unambiguous. feature_078 is the dominant single continuous
  predictor in the dataset.

Iter 4 — feature_038 (range 6–18, suspected hemoglobin) and feature_057 (0–2,
  suspected ECOG performance status).
  Results: feature_038 univariate β=-0.012 (p=0.022, but flips to NS with
  adjustment). feature_057: PS0 mean PFS 4.68, PS1 3.49, PS2 2.38; OLS slope
  -1.16 mo per unit (p<1e-300). PS hypothesis STRONGLY SUPPORTED;
  hemoglobin hypothesis essentially refuted after adjustment.

Iter 5 — Other major continuous predictors: log feature_013 (PSA-like),
  log feature_092 (ALP-like), feature_009 (albumin-like, 1.5–5.5),
  feature_006 (0–24.6).
  Results: log_f013 β=-0.348/log-unit; log_f092 β=-0.078/log-unit;
  feature_009 β=+0.495/unit; feature_006 β=-0.075/unit. All p<1e-30, all
  retained significance after multivariable adjustment. ALL SUPPORTED.

Iter 6 — feature_067 (Gleason-like, 6–10) and other ordinal features
  (feature_016, 071, 026, 096, 033, 064).
  Results: feature_067 ANOVA p=0.37; group means flat. The other six ordinal
  features all had |β|<0.011 with p>0.20. REFUTED — no monotonic
  ordinal–PFS relationships besides feature_057.

Iter 7 — Top binary predictor: feature_051 (55% prev) and whether its effect
  is mediated by PSA.
  Results: feature_051=1 mean PFS 3.51 vs =0 mean 4.03; Welch p=1.6e-181.
  After adjustment for log_f013, β attenuates to -0.002 (p=0.65). feature_051
  is essentially a high-PSA flag (log-PSA mean 3.50 vs 1.99 in =0 group).
  SUPPORTED; mediation hypothesis CONFIRMED.

Iter 8 — Adjusted effects of two treatment-like features: feature_109 (10%) and
  feature_039 (10%).
  Results: Both showed adjusted β≈+0.15 mo (p<1e-179) — strongest binary
  positive effects. Initial interpretation: effective treatments. (Iter 13
  later revealed this signal is entirely driven by the dual-positive
  subgroup.)

Iter 9 — Other binary effects after adjustment.
  Results: feature_027 (60% prev) β=+0.025, p=6e-15; feature_043 (20%) β=-0.016,
  p=3.5e-5. All other binary features had |adjusted β|<0.025 mo. The four
  features feature_109/039/027/043 carry the bulk of the binary signal.

Iter 10 — Disparities in feature_109 receipt by race and insurance.
  Results: Receipt rates were 9.4%–10.7% across races and 9.9%–10.9% across
  insurance categories — chi-square p>0.30 in both. NO ACCESS DISPARITY.

Iter 11 — feature_109 treatment-effect heterogeneity by race and insurance.
  Results: Race-stratified adjusted effects ranged +0.137 to +0.157 mo, all
  highly significant; race × treatment interaction terms all p>0.19. The
  medicaid × feature_109 interaction was +0.044 (p=0.006) — DIRECTION OPPOSITE
  the disparity hypothesis (medicaid patients gained slightly MORE benefit).

Iter 12 — feature_109 effect heterogeneity by PS, log_PSA, and feature_078
  quartile.
  Results: PS interaction p=0.14; log_f013 interaction p=0.082 (borderline,
  larger benefit at lower PSA); quartile-stratified effects +0.14 to +0.21,
  uniformly positive. Effect is largely homogeneous.

Iter 13 — feature_039 treatment-effect heterogeneity by race; whether
  feature_109 alone is meaningful.
  Results: Race-stratified f039 effects +0.122 to +0.213, all highly sig
  individually; black-vs-white interaction p=0.056 (borderline). Critically:
  fitting indicators for "f109 only" and "f039 only" vs neither showed both
  monotherapy coefficients ≈0 (p>0.91), but the dual indicator β=+1.43
  (p<1e-300). The apparent +0.15 main effect of either drug was a marginal
  artifact of the dual-positive subgroup.

Iter 14 — Drug synergy: explicit f109 × f039 interaction; group means.
  Results: Adjusted interaction β=+1.434 mo (p<1e-300); main effects ≈0.
  Cell means: neither (n=40,435) 3.74, only f109 (n=4,467) 3.67, only f039
  (n=4,569) 3.67, both (n=529) 5.22. Clear supra-additive synergy:
  monotherapy yields no benefit; combined therapy yields +1.5 mo PFS.
  STRONGLY SUPPORTED.

Iter 15 — Synergy consistency across PS strata and across race strata.
  Results: Dual−neither difference: PS0 +1.71, PS1 +1.32, PS2 +1.63 mo
  (synergy persists). By race: white +1.51, black +1.76, hispanic +0.99,
  asian +1.42, other small n. Hispanic point estimate is lower but not
  formally a significant interaction (small n_dual=78).

Iter 16 — Disparities in dual-treatment receipt.
  Results: Dual receipt was 0.82%–1.10% across races (chi² p=0.78) and
  1.02%–1.20% across insurance (chi² p=0.85). NO disparity in access to the
  effective combination therapy.

Iter 17 — Race heterogeneity in prognostic effects of anemia (low f038)
  and log_PSA.
  Results: All race × clinical interactions NS (smallest p=0.078 for
  hgb_low × black). The prognostic strength of these markers is uniform
  across races.

Iter 18 — Race heterogeneity in feature_006 effect; insurance heterogeneity
  in feature_078 (age) effect.
  Results: feature_006 × race interactions all p>0.10. feature_078 ×
  medicaid interaction +0.001 (p=0.044, marginal but clinically tiny);
  others NS.

Iter 19 — Other continuous features: feature_005, feature_054, feature_120.
  Results: All NS (p≥0.079). No additional continuous prognostic markers
  beyond those identified in iter 5.

Iter 20 — feature_027 effect homogeneity and access.
  Results: Adjusted main β=+0.026, all race interactions p>0.5; receipt
  rates 59.3%–62.5% across races and 59.1%–60.1% across insurance — no
  meaningful disparity. Uniform small benefit confirmed.

Iter 21 — Synergy benefit by feature_078 quartile; dominance of dual-treatment
  in adjusted models.
  Results: Dual-treated quartile means consistently exceed neither-treated
  by 1.0–1.7 mo. f109:f039 interaction is the largest binary coefficient in
  the fully adjusted model, dwarfing every other binary β.

Iter 22 — Adjusted race and adjusted insurance effects (formal disparity
  test after controlling for clinical factors).
  Results: All adjusted race coefficients p>0.6; all adjusted insurance
  coefficients p>0.38. Joint Wald tests for both NS. NO DISPARITIES after
  adjusting for the prognostic burden mix.

Iter 23 — Final continuous-predictor multivariable model; whether
  hemoglobin (feature_038) survives adjustment.
  Results: Adjusted estimates: feature_078 +0.174/unit; feature_009
  +0.478/unit; feature_006 -0.077/unit; log_f013 -0.349/log-unit;
  log_f092 -0.101/log-unit; PS1 -1.18; PS2 -2.29. Model R²≈0.85. feature_038
  fully attenuates to β=5e-5 (p=0.95) — its univariate signal was confounded.

Iter 24 — Rare binary features (<2% prevalence); within-PS-stratum
  consistency of feature_009 effect.
  Results: feature_058 (1.9%) β=+0.023, p=0.044 (just significant). Other
  rare features NS. feature_009 within-PS β≈+0.48 in every stratum
  (p<1e-50 each) — independent prognostic, not confounded by PS.

Iter 25 — Composite final hypothesis.
  Result: Confirmed. Strongest determinants of PFS in order: feature_057
  (PS, β -1.18 to -2.29), feature_078 (+0.174/unit), feature_009
  (+0.478/unit), log feature_013 (-0.349/log-unit), feature_006
  (-0.077/unit), log feature_092 (-0.101/log-unit), and dual receipt of
  feature_109+feature_039 (interaction +1.43 mo). Race and insurance show
  NO independent association with PFS and NO disparities in treatment
  receipt or in treatment effectiveness in this cohort.

OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
===================

(1) Prognostic factors. Six clinical/laboratory variables explain most of
    PFS variance (model R²≈0.85): performance status (feature_057), the
    age-like feature_078, an albumin-like marker (feature_009), a PSA-like
    log-marker (feature_013), feature_006, and an ALP-like log-marker
    (feature_092). The Gleason-like feature (feature_067) and hemoglobin-like
    feature (feature_038) carry NO independent prognostic information once
    these six are in the model.

(2) Treatment effects. Two binary treatment-like flags (feature_109 and
    feature_039) appear individually weakly beneficial in unadjusted
    analyses (β≈+0.15 mo), but explicit interaction modeling shows that this
    apparent main effect is entirely an artifact of the small dual-positive
    subgroup (n=529): monotherapy with either feature_109 or feature_039
    yields zero PFS benefit, while the combination yields a robust +1.43 mo
    synergistic benefit (p<1e-300). The synergy is consistent across
    performance-status strata and (within sample-size limits) across race.

(3) Treatment-biomarker heterogeneity. No strong heterogeneity was detected
    for the dual treatment by PS, age, or PSA. A borderline negative
    interaction with log-PSA (p=0.082) hints the combination may work
    slightly better in lower-PSA patients but does not survive significance.

(4) Demographic disparities. Across all univariate, adjusted, and
    interaction analyses:
     • PFS is statistically indistinguishable across white, black, hispanic,
       asian, other race groups (all p>0.6 after adjustment, joint p>0.85).
     • PFS is statistically indistinguishable across medicare, private,
       medicaid, uninsured insurance categories (all p>0.38 after adjustment).
     • Receipt of the highly effective dual combination (feature_109+feature_039)
       does not differ by race (chi² p=0.78) or insurance (chi² p=0.85).
     • Treatment-effect interactions with race and insurance are uniformly
       null or, where significant, point in the OPPOSITE direction of a
       disparity (e.g. medicaid × feature_109 interaction +0.044 mo, p=0.006).
    No disparity pattern is supported by the data in this cohort.

(5) The single largest negative-direction "binary" predictor (feature_051,
    diff -0.52 mo, p=1.6e-181) is fully explained by its function as a
    high-PSA dichotomization — a useful methodological warning that not
    every strong binary effect signals a treatment or biology variable.

The analyses are consistent with a clinical picture in which biological
prognostic burden (PS, lab markers, the age-like covariate) and a single
synergistic two-drug combination drive PFS, while measured demographic
factors are not associated with outcomes once that prognostic information
is accounted for.
