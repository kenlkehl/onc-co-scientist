ANALYSIS SUMMARY — ds001_prostate
=================================

Cohort: 50,000 anonymized prostate-cancer EHR records, 122 opaque features and one
outcome (pfs_months, range 0.0–14.6, mean 3.74, SD 2.02). The features include two
demographic categoricals (feature_018 = race with 5 levels; feature_045 = insurance
with 4 levels), 35 continuous variables (mostly lab-like or vital-sign-like), 8
ordinal counts (3-, 5-, or 11-level), and 77 binary 0/1 indicators.

Across 21 iterations of the propose-test-refine loop, the analyses converged on
the following picture.

1. DEMOGRAPHIC NULL FINDINGS (iterations 1, 5, 7, 11, 21)
---------------------------------------------------------
- Race (feature_018): no signal in pfs_months. ANOVA F=0.564, p=0.69; pairwise
  tests (black/hispanic/asian/other vs white) all NS with absolute mean
  differences <0.06 months.
- Insurance (feature_045): no signal. ANOVA F=0.344, p=0.79; uninsured-vs-private
  difference -0.010 months (p=0.83).
- Race × disease severity: black-vs-white differences in feature_057 (stage-like),
  feature_051 (binary disease marker), feature_013 (PSA-like), feature_009
  (albumin-like), feature_006 (burden-like), feature_092 (ALP-like) were all
  non-significant. This is a notable departure from real-world prostate cancer
  epidemiology and points to a synthetic / engineered cohort.
- Receipt of the two candidate therapeutic indicators (feature_109, feature_039)
  and of their combination did NOT differ across race or insurance categories
  (chi-square p in 0.26–0.85 range).
- After fitting a final adjusted model, residual demographic associations with
  pfs_months were all |r|<0.01.

Hypotheses POSITED (h1_race_pfs, h1_uninsured_vs_private, h5_race_severity,
h7_*, h11_*) were REFUTED. The only nuanced findings on demographics were
modest differences in the magnitude (not direction) of the combination
treatment effect (see point 4).

2. CORE PROGNOSTIC AXIS (iterations 1–4)
----------------------------------------
- feature_078 (continuous, 30–90, mean 65 — appears age-shaped, but the
  direction is reversed): Pearson r = +0.86 with pfs_months. Quartile means
  rise monotonically from 1.83 mo (Q1, range 30-58) to 5.78 mo (Q4, 72-90).
  This single variable explains roughly 73% of pfs_months variance and is by
  far the dominant predictor; the originally posited hypothesis (negative
  correlation, h1_age_pfs) was REFUTED in direction but supported in
  magnitude.
- feature_057 (3-level ordinal): means 4.68 / 3.49 / 2.38 at levels 0/1/2;
  spearman rho = -0.39, p~0. Behaves like extent-of-disease / stage
  (h3_f057_stage SUPPORTED).
- feature_051 (binary, ~55% prevalence): -0.52 mo unadjusted, -0.376 mo
  adjusted, p<<1e-180. Behaves like a binary metastatic-status indicator
  (h3_f051_neg SUPPORTED).
- feature_013 (continuous, median 16, range 0.12–3622, PSA-like): r=-0.15;
  adjusted coefficient -0.0027 mo per unit, p<1e-300 (h2_psa SUPPORTED).
- feature_006 (continuous, 28% zeros, range 0–24.6, plausibly burden / pain
  / lesion count): r=-0.14 unadjusted; adjusted -0.076 per unit, p<1e-300
  (h2_f006 SUPPORTED).
- feature_009 (continuous, 1.5–5.5, albumin-like): r=+0.12 unadjusted;
  adjusted +0.479 mo per g/dL, p<1e-300 (h2_alb SUPPORTED).
- feature_092 (continuous, ALP-like): marginal r=-0.017 only; but adjusted
  for the other six core variables, coefficient -0.00049 per unit, p=2e-80.
  Independent eighth axis (h13/h14 SUPPORTED).
- feature_038 (hemoglobin-like) and feature_067 (Gleason-like, 6-10) have
  essentially NO association with pfs_months (h2_hgb refuted in magnitude;
  h1_f067_pfs REFUTED).

A six-variable OLS model (feature_078, feature_057, feature_051, feature_013,
feature_006, feature_009) achieves R²=0.946. Adding feature_092 raises R² to
0.952. Adding the combination treatment indicator brings R² to 0.952 as well.

3. THERAPEUTIC EFFECT — COMBINATION ONLY (iterations 6, 8, 10)
--------------------------------------------------------------
After adjusting for the core six predictors, only two binary features
emerged with |coef|>0.05 and p<1e-50: feature_109 (+0.156, p=6e-110;
prevalence 10%) and feature_039 (+0.146, p=7e-99; prevalence 10%). All
other binaries had |coef|<0.04.

A 2×2 decomposition revealed a striking synergistic interaction:
  feature_109=0, feature_039=0  → mean pfs 3.74 (n=40,435)
  feature_109=0, feature_039=1  → 3.67 (n=4,569)
  feature_109=1, feature_039=0  → 3.67 (n=4,467)
  feature_109=1, feature_039=1  → 5.22 (n=529)

Adjusted regression with both main effects and an interaction term:
  feature_109 alone: +0.0034 mo, p=0.63 (NS)
  feature_039 alone: -0.0031 mo, p=0.66 (NS)
  feature_109 × feature_039: +1.440 mo, p~0.

Hypothesis h10_combo_synergy SUPPORTED in a strong, almost archetypal form:
neither agent has any independent effect; the combination produces a roughly
+1.44 month PFS benefit. h10_solo_null also SUPPORTED.

The treatment-effect heterogeneity tests:
- Stage (feature_057) × combo: f057×combo NS (p=0.86; h9_109_by_stage
  REFUTED in formal interaction sense).
- feature_051 × combo: combo benefit slightly attenuated in feature_051=1
  patients (-0.113 mo, p=0.004) but remains strongly positive in both
  strata (h15_combo_x_f051 SUPPORTED in modest form).
- feature_092 × combo: -0.00139 per unit, p=2.5e-9. High-ALP patients gain
  roughly 0.28 mo less from combination per 200-unit increment but still
  benefit substantially (h14_combo_x_alp SUPPORTED).
- feature_078 × combo: stratified analyses show similar combo effect across
  feature_078 quartiles; no formal interaction.

4. DEMOGRAPHIC × TREATMENT INTERACTIONS (iterations 8, 12)
----------------------------------------------------------
The most clinically relevant question on bias was whether demographic
groups derive different benefit from the combination.

Race × combo (joint LRT chi²=16.57, df=4, p=0.0023; SUPPORTED in formal
sense but small magnitude):
  asian   +1.62 mo (n_combo=30)
  black   +1.39        (58)
  hispanic +1.36       (78)
  white   +1.44        (355)
  other   +1.90        (8)
All groups gain substantially; the spread (~0.5 mo across the five
race categories) is small relative to the +1.4 main effect. No race
group shows null or negative benefit.

Insurance × combo (joint LRT chi²=33.74, df=3, p=2.2e-7; SUPPORTED):
  medicare  +1.50 mo (n_combo=224)
  private   +1.30        (203)
  uninsured +1.61        (24)
  medicaid  +1.59        (78)
Privately-insured patients gain ~0.20–0.30 mo less than the other three
groups. All groups still gain substantially. Notably, the heterogeneity
runs OPPOSITE to a stereotypical bias direction — medicaid and uninsured
patients gain at least as much as privately insured patients.

5. OTHER FINDINGS
-----------------
- feature_007 is constant (all zeros, h19 SUPPORTED).
- Vital signs: feature_031 (HR-like, -5e-4 per bpm, p=0.007) and feature_082
  (SpO2-like, +0.0026 per %, p=0.022) retain small but real residual
  associations after core adjustment. BMI-like, SBP-like, DBP-like do not
  (h17 PARTIALLY supported).
- feature_013 effect on PFS does not differ across stage strata
  (interaction p=0.31; h16 REFUTED).
- The remaining 70+ binary indicators have essentially no signal after
  multivariable adjustment.

6. OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
----------------------
In this cohort:
(a) feature_078 is the dominant prognostic axis and is positively
    associated with PFS, contradicting the typical age-PFS direction in
    real-world prostate cancer datasets.
(b) feature_057 (stage-like), feature_051, feature_013 (PSA-like),
    feature_009 (albumin-like), feature_006 (burden-like), and
    feature_092 (ALP-like) are independent and clinically directionally
    plausible prognostic axes.
(c) The therapeutic signal is concentrated in a SYNERGISTIC combination
    of feature_109 and feature_039 — neither agent helps alone. The
    combination yields a ~1.4-month PFS benefit and is given to ~1% of
    patients.
(d) Race and insurance carry essentially no information about pfs_months
    once disease-severity and treatment indicators are adjusted for.
    The cohort behaves as if engineered to lack demographic confounding.
    The only demographic-related findings are modest differences in the
    MAGNITUDE (not direction) of combination benefit — and even those
    do not run in the direction of disadvantaging traditionally
    underserved groups (medicaid and uninsured patients gain at least as
    much as private-insurance patients from the combination).
(e) A final compact model with the eight core predictors plus the combo
    indicator explains 95.2% of pfs_months variance with no large
    residual demographic structure.
