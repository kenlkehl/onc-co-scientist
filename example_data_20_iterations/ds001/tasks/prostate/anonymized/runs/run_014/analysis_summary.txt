Analysis Summary: ds001_prostate (n = 50,000; outcome = pfs_months)
=====================================================================

Headline conclusions
--------------------
1. The variance in pfs_months is dominated by six predictors. A linear model with these explains 97.4% of variance.
2. The single largest source of heterogeneity is feature_078 (a continuous variable, range 30-90, mean ~65). Higher feature_078 is monotonically associated with longer pfs_months and alone explains R^2 ~0.73.
3. A 3-level integer feature_057 acts as a stage-like prognostic factor: PFS 4.68 -> 3.49 -> 2.38 mo across levels 0/1/2 (rho = -0.39, p < 1e-300).
4. Two continuous biomarkers are unfavorable (feature_013 on a log scale, feature_006); one is favorable (feature_009).
5. A novel pure synergy was identified: feature_109 alone has no effect, feature_039 alone has no effect, but patients carrying BOTH (n = 529, ~1% of cohort) gain about +1.4 mo of pfs_months on top of all other prognostic adjustments (interaction p < 1e-300; effect homogeneous across stages 0, 1, 2).
6. Race (feature_018) and insurance (feature_045) have NO main-effect disparity in PFS, even unadjusted, suggesting equitable outcomes on average. Two small but real interaction signals do appear, however: the synergy benefit of feature_109+feature_039 is ~15% smaller in non-asian (excluding "other") and ~17% smaller in privately-insured patients.

Walk-through of iterations 1-25
-------------------------------

Iterations 1-4 were a univariate screen across continuous, binary, multi-level integer, and categorical features. The screen rapidly localized signal:
- Continuous: feature_078 rho = +0.853 (huge); feature_013 rho = -0.223; feature_006 rho = -0.134; feature_009 rho = +0.118. All other 30 continuous features had |rho| < 0.02.
- Binary: feature_051 stood out with -0.521 mo difference between groups (p ~ 1e-181); next strongest binary was an order of magnitude smaller.
- Multi-level integers: feature_057 (3 levels) rho = -0.39 (very strong, monotone). The Gleason-shaped feature_067 (levels 6-10) was NOT prognostic at all (p = 0.37). Other 5-level integer features (feature_016, 026, 033, 064, 071, 096) were also non-prognostic.
- Race and insurance: F-test p = 0.69 and p = 0.79 respectively. Mean PFS was 3.74-3.80 across all racial categories and 3.73-3.75 across all insurance groups -- no main-effect disparity.

Iterations 5-7 quantified the top predictors on the natural pfs_months scale (months) and tested for nonlinearity. feature_078 has +0.174 mo per unit linearly, with a small but highly significant positive quadratic component (curve accelerates upward at high values). feature_009 has +0.495 mo per unit. feature_013 was log-transformed because of its long right tail; the log-transformed slope is -0.382 mo per log-unit and the quartile gradient of mean PFS is monotone (Q1 4.31 -> Q4 3.11).

Iteration 8 revealed an important confounding finding. When feature_051 was added to a model already containing feature_078, feature_057, log(feature_013), feature_009, and feature_006, its coefficient collapsed from -0.521 mo (univariate) to +0.0006 mo (p = 0.87). Iteration 14 isolated the source of confounding: feature_051 = 1 patients have log(feature_013) of 3.55 versus 2.18 in feature_051 = 0 patients (p ~ 1e-300), while feature_078, feature_057, feature_009, and feature_006 are perfectly balanced between the two groups. So feature_051 is a surrogate marker for high feature_013, not an independent prognostic factor.

Iterations 9, 15, 16 probed effect modification by feature_057. The slope of feature_078 is shallower at higher stages (interaction with stage 2 = -0.022 mo/unit, p ~ 1e-112), the negative slope of log(feature_013) is mildly attenuated at higher stages (~13% reduction at stage 2), and the protective slope of feature_009 is ~12% smaller at stage 2. All three effect modifications are statistically robust but small relative to the underlying main effects.

Iterations 10-13, 17-18 examined race and insurance as effect modifiers. After multivariable adjustment, race and insurance contributed essentially nothing to PFS prediction. feature_051 x race and feature_051 x insurance interactions were not significant. feature_078 x race showed a tiny black-vs-asian slope difference (-0.002 mo/unit, p = 0.013); feature_078 x insurance showed a tiny medicare-vs-medicaid difference (-0.0015, p = 0.003). Magnitudes are < 1% of the main effect and not clinically meaningful.

Iteration 19 ran an adjusted screen across all 81 binary features holding the established prognostic covariates fixed. Two binaries jumped out far above the rest: feature_109 (+0.153 mo, p ~ 1e-183) and feature_039 (+0.149 mo, p ~ 1e-177). Both have ~10% prevalence and are protective. The next-largest adjusted binary effect (feature_027) was only +0.025 mo.

Iteration 20 tested feature_109's effect modification. Effect was homogeneous across stages and races. Insurance interaction showed a single significant contrast: feature_109's protective effect was ~25% smaller in privately insured patients (-0.045 vs medicaid reference, p = 0.006).

Iteration 21 was the surprise of the analysis. Cross-tabbing (feature_109 x feature_039) showed: neither = 40,435 patients (mean PFS 3.74), feature_039 only = 4,569 (3.67), feature_109 only = 4,467 (3.67), BOTH = 529 (5.22). The three single-positive cells are statistically indistinguishable from the neither cell, but the BOTH cell sits ~1.5 mo above. After full multivariable adjustment, the main effects of feature_109 and feature_039 are essentially zero (p = 0.90 and 0.94), but the interaction feature_109:feature_039 is +1.434 mo (p < 1e-300). This is a pure-synergy treatment-combination signature: neither component is active alone, but the combination produces a large prognostic benefit.

Iteration 22 confirmed the synergy holds within each level of feature_057: stage 0 +1.48, stage 1 +1.37, stage 2 +1.52 (all p < 1e-170). The combination effect does not require any particular stage and is not concentrated in low- or high-risk patients.

Iterations 23 and 24 tested whether the synergy is uniform across race and insurance, the most important policy-relevant interactions. Three-way interaction with race: synergy is +1.63 in asian (reference), +1.38 in black, +1.39 in hispanic, +1.43 in white, +1.93 in "other". Black, hispanic, and white patients therefore gain about 0.20-0.25 mo less than asian patients (p = 0.002-0.004). Three-way interaction with insurance: synergy is +1.59 in medicaid (reference), +1.46 in medicare, +1.33 in private, +1.60 in uninsured. Privately insured patients gain about 0.27 mo less than medicaid patients (p ~ 3e-8). Both findings indicate effect-modification of the combination's benefit, even though no main-effect race/insurance disparity is visible. (Caveat: these effects are small relative to the +1.4-1.9 mo synergy, and the 'other' race result goes the opposite direction from the rest -- statistically meaningful but interpretation should be careful.)

Iteration 25 was the consolidating multivariable check. The final model is:
  pfs_months ~ feature_078 + C(feature_057) + log(feature_013) + feature_009 + feature_006 + feature_109 * feature_039
Coefficients: feature_078 +0.174 mo/unit, feature_057=1 -1.178 mo, feature_057=2 -2.288 mo, log(feature_013) -0.383 mo/log-unit, feature_009 +0.479 mo/unit, feature_006 -0.077 mo/unit, feature_109:feature_039 +1.434 mo. R^2 = 0.974, R^2_adj = 0.974. The model leaves negligible adjusted effects for any other binary feature.

Hypotheses supported (with quantified effects)
----------------------------------------------
- Higher feature_078 -> longer PFS (+0.174 mo/unit, with mild upward curvature). [iter 1, 5, 7]
- feature_057 acts as a stage-like prognostic factor (mean PFS 4.68 -> 3.49 -> 2.38 mo). [iter 3]
- log(feature_013), feature_006 unfavorable; feature_009 favorable, all monotone. [iter 1, 5, 6]
- feature_051's univariate -0.521 mo difference is fully explained by the imbalance in feature_013. [iter 8, 14]
- feature_078 effect is weaker at higher stages (interaction with stage 2: -0.022 mo/unit). [iter 9]
- feature_109+feature_039 combination is a pure-synergy effect: +1.434 mo PFS bonus only when BOTH are present, no effect if either alone. [iter 21]
- Synergy holds across all stages. [iter 22]
- Synergy magnitude differs by race (asian/other > black/hispanic/white) and by insurance (medicaid/uninsured > private). [iter 23, 24]

Hypotheses refuted
------------------
- feature_067 (levels 6-10, Gleason-like) is NOT prognostic for PFS in this cohort (p = 0.37). [iter 3]
- Race and insurance have NO main-effect impact on PFS, unadjusted or adjusted. [iter 4, 10, 11]
- feature_051 is NOT an independent prognostic factor (effect collapses to zero on adjustment). [iter 8]
- feature_051 effect does NOT vary by race or insurance. [iter 12, 13]
- The five other 5-level integer features (feature_016, 026, 033, 064, 071, 096) are NOT prognostic. [iter 3]

Overall picture of treatment-biomarker-outcome relationships
-----------------------------------------------------------
- The dominant axes of PFS heterogeneity are demographic/baseline-state (feature_078 and feature_057) and three biomarkers (feature_013, feature_009, feature_006). These together explain ~97% of variance.
- There is no meaningful main-effect disparity by race or insurance.
- Among the binary "treatment-or-comorbidity" features, only the combination of feature_109 and feature_039 has prognostic relevance, and only when both are present. This pattern is consistent with a true combination therapy / two-hit biomarker logic rather than independent additive effects.
- The synergy benefit, while large, is somewhat smaller in non-asian and privately insured subgroups -- suggesting differential treatment efficacy or differential treatment quality / adherence by these social strata, even though baseline PFS is the same. This is a clinically and policy-relevant signal worth confirming in independent data.
- feature_067 (the Gleason-shaped variable) is uninformative in this dataset, meaning analyses that rely on Gleason as a stratifier should be reexamined.
