Analysis Summary — ds001_prostate cohort (50,000 patients, outcome: pfs_months)
================================================================================

Overview
--------
The dataset contains 50,000 prostate-cancer patient records, 122 de-identified
features (1 categorical race, 1 categorical insurance, 5 ordinal 0-4 features,
2 ordinal multi-level features, 1 ordinal 6-10 feature, 35 continuous numeric
features, and 77 binary 0/1 features), and one continuous outcome `pfs_months`
(progression-free survival, range 0-14.6 mo, mean 3.74). Across 25 iterations
I screened each feature univariately, then built up multivariable models, then
probed interactions, then tested for racial/insurance disparities and for
treatment-effect heterogeneity by race and insurance.


Strongest main-effect predictors (multivariable model, R^2 = 0.97)
------------------------------------------------------------------
The final model with 11 predictors explains 97.0% of variance in pfs_months.
Every coefficient retains the sign it had univariately. Listed by absolute
contribution (coefficient × range observed):

  1. feature_078 (continuous, range 30-90, mean 65)
     β = +0.174 mo / unit (p < 1e-300). Dominant predictor — alone explains
     R^2 = 0.73 of PFS variance, and decile mean PFS rises monotonically from
     0.85 mo (lowest decile) to 6.86 mo (highest). A small but highly
     significant quadratic term (+0.0008, p ~ 2e-112) adds <0.3% R^2.
     feature_078 is essentially orthogonal to every ordinal feature
     (|r| < 0.01 with feature_057, feature_067, feature_026, and the 0-4
     ordinals), so its effect is not confounded by them.

  2. feature_057 (3-level ordinal, 0/1/2 with n = 17592 / 24971 / 7437)
     β = -1.151 mo per level (p < 1e-300). Mean PFS 4.68 / 3.49 / 2.38 mo at
     levels 0/1/2. A significant feature_078 × feature_057 interaction
     (β = -0.0098, p ~ 8e-100) means the feature_078 slope flattens slightly
     as feature_057 rises, but adds <0.1% R^2.

  3. log10(feature_013) (continuous, raw range 0.12-3622, very right-skewed)
     β = -0.805 mo per log10 unit (p < 1e-300). Mean PFS by raw quartile is
     4.31 / 3.93 / 3.62 / 3.11 — strictly decreasing. The PSA-shaped
     distribution (skew, range, 99th pct ~417) supports a PSA-like role.
     A small log_f013 × feature_057 interaction (+0.042, p = 1.5e-12)
     attenuates the negative slope at higher feature_057 levels.

  4. feature_009 (continuous, range 1.5-5.5, mean 3.80, std 0.50)
     β = +0.477 mo / unit (p < 1e-300). Distribution is consistent with a
     blood-chemistry marker (e.g., albumin in g/dL).

  5. feature_006 (continuous, range 0-24.6, 27.5% zeros)
     β = -0.077 mo / unit (p < 1e-300). The presence indicator (>0) alone
     carries -0.39 mo (p ~ 2e-84), but the magnitude beyond zero matters
     too — both the zero/non-zero split and the linear slope are informative.

  6. feature_092 (continuous, range 48-830, mean 224)
     β = -0.0005 mo / unit (p < 1e-100). Per-unit effect is tiny but the
     ~780-unit range yields ~ -0.4 mo of effect at the extremes.

  7. feature_109 (binary, prevalence 0.10) β = +0.151 mo (p ~ 1e-181).
  8. feature_039 (binary, prevalence 0.10) β = +0.149 mo (p ~ 3e-179).
     These two confer essentially identical adjusted benefit (~5 days each).
     They do not interact (β = -0.005, p = 0.86), so their effects appear
     additive and independent.

  9. feature_027 (binary, prevalence 0.60) β = +0.025 mo (p ~ 2e-15).
 10. feature_043 (binary, prevalence 0.20) β = -0.017 mo (p ~ 3e-5).
 11. feature_121 (binary, prevalence 0.05) β = +0.019 mo (p = 0.007).


Apparent predictors that are confounded
---------------------------------------
feature_051 (binary, prev 0.55) had the largest univariate effect of any
binary feature (-0.521 mo, p ~ 2e-181). However, feature_051 is strongly
linked to feature_013 (median 32.9 vs 7.4, t = 150 on log10 scale). Once
log10(feature_013) enters the multivariable model, feature_051's coefficient
collapses to -0.001 (p = 0.75): the apparent effect is fully explained by
the underlying PSA-like marker.


Features without informative signal
-----------------------------------
Several variables one might suspect to be prognostic were null:

  • feature_067 (ordinal 6-10, the natural Gleason range): mean PFS spans
    only 3.70-3.76 mo across grades 6-10; r ~ 0.000, p > 0.5.
    This is unexpected for a true Gleason measure but unambiguous in this
    dataset.

  • The 0-4 ordinal features (feature_016, 071, 096, 033, 064): all have
    |r| < 0.01 with PFS (all p > 0.2). None modifies the effect of
    feature_051 or feature_109 (all interaction p > 0.10).

  • feature_026 (0-10 ordinal): |r| < 0.002, p = 0.80.

  • feature_120 (continuous 20-508): r = +0.001 (p = 0.83), adjusted
    coefficient -3e-5 (p = 0.18). No association.

  • Most binary features beyond the 11 retained in the final model add
    ≤ 0.01 mo of adjusted effect; the cumulative contribution of the
    remaining ~70 binaries is negligible.


Race and insurance: no disparities
----------------------------------
This was a primary investigation across multiple iterations.

  • feature_018 (race: white 64.8%, hispanic 15.1%, black 12.1%, asian 6.0%,
    other 2.0%) — marginal one-way ANOVA F = 0.56, p = 0.69. Mean PFS spans
    just 3.737-3.797 mo across the five groups (a 1.6% relative spread).
    After adjustment for the six clinical predictors, all race coefficients
    are < 0.005 mo with p > 0.6.

  • feature_045 (insurance: medicare 42.1%, private 39.7%, medicaid 14.2%,
    uninsured 4.0%) — marginal ANOVA F = 0.34, p = 0.79. Mean PFS spans
    3.726-3.754 mo. Adjusted insurance coefficients are essentially zero
    (each |β| < 0.001, p > 0.9).

  • Race-conditional analyses within each insurance class also showed no
    residual race effects (every race × insurance cell had p > 0.2 with
    coefficients in the range -0.04 to +0.01 mo).


Treatment-effect heterogeneity
------------------------------
For the four candidate treatment-like binaries (feature_109, feature_039,
feature_043, feature_027) I ran joint F-tests for treatment × race and
treatment × insurance interactions, and stratified estimates within each
demographic class.

  • Treatment × race: all four joint F-tests non-significant (p = 0.21-0.97).
    Stratified coefficients for feature_051 (legacy primary candidate) range
    only -0.48 to -0.57 mo across asian/black/hispanic/other/white. No
    racial heterogeneity.

  • Treatment × insurance: feature_039 × insurance F = 4.19, p = 0.006 and
    feature_109 × insurance F = 2.74, p = 0.041. The other two treatments
    showed no insurance heterogeneity (p > 0.85). Stratified coefficients,
    though, span very narrow ranges: feature_039 effects are 0.13-0.19 mo
    (~2 days difference between groups) and feature_109 effects are
    0.14-0.18 mo (~1 day). Statistically detectable at n = 50,000 but
    clinically negligible.


Interactions of note
--------------------
  • feature_078 × feature_057: β = -0.0098, p = 8e-100. Higher-stage
    patients receive less benefit from each additional unit of feature_078.
  • log_f013 × feature_057: β = +0.042, p = 1.5e-12. The PSA-like marker's
    negative slope is attenuated at higher feature_057 levels.
  • Three-way feature_078 × feature_057 × feature_051: β = -0.005,
    p = 6e-10. Statistically real but small in magnitude.
  • feature_109 × feature_039: NOT significant (p = 0.86); they act
    additively.
  • feature_009 × feature_057: NOT significant (p = 0.83).
  • feature_078 × feature_009: NOT significant.


Hypotheses supported vs refuted
-------------------------------
Supported (with the predicted direction confirmed):
  - Strong positive linear effect of feature_078 (h1).
  - Negative ordinal effect of feature_057 (h1).
  - Negative effect of log_f013 / quartile gradient of feature_013
    (h1, h17).
  - Negative effect of feature_006 and the >0 indicator (h2, h16).
  - Positive effect of feature_009 (h1).
  - Multivariable independence of feature_078, feature_057, log_f013,
    feature_006, feature_009, feature_092 (h3, h23).
  - Positive adjusted effects of feature_109, feature_039, feature_027,
    feature_121 (h7, h20).
  - Negative adjusted effect of feature_043 (h7).
  - feature_051 effect is confounded by feature_013 (h4).
  - Quadratic feature_078 component (h13) — significant but small.
  - feature_078 × feature_057 interaction (h8); log_f013 × feature_057
    interaction (h9); 3-way feature_078 × feature_057 × feature_051 (h14).
  - feature_078 orthogonality to ordinal features (h24).

Refuted:
  - feature_067 as a prognostic Gleason-like marker (h2_f067).
  - Marginal and adjusted racial differences in PFS (h5, h25).
  - Marginal and adjusted insurance differences in PFS (h6, h25).
  - feature_120 association with PFS (h15).
  - 0-4 ordinal main effects and ordinal × treatment interactions (h12).
  - feature_009 × feature_057 interaction (h9).
  - Treatment × race interactions (h10, h25).
  - feature_109 × feature_039 synergy (h21).
  - feature_078 × feature_009 interaction (h22).
  - Race effects within insurance strata (h19).

Weakly supported but clinically negligible:
  - Treatment × insurance interactions for feature_109 and feature_039
    (h11) — F-test p < 0.05 but stratified-effect spread < 1 week.


Bottom line
-----------
PFS in this prostate-cancer cohort is governed almost entirely by six
clinical/biomarker variables — feature_078, feature_057, log10(feature_013),
feature_006, feature_009, and feature_092 — supplemented by four small but
robust binary effects (feature_109, feature_039, feature_027, feature_043)
plus minor contributions from feature_121. Together these explain 97% of
the variance in pfs_months. The strongest binary candidate (feature_051)
disappears after adjustment for the PSA-like marker (feature_013) and is
best understood as a downstream proxy. Race and insurance show no main-effect
disparity in PFS (marginal or adjusted), and treatment-effect heterogeneity
across racial and insurance subgroups is either absent or, where statistically
detectable, of magnitude smaller than one week. The cohort behaves as if
clinical biology — not demography or coverage — drives the time-to-progression
outcome.
