Analysis Summary — ds001_prostate (n = 50,000; outcome: pfs_months)
===================================================================

Overview
--------
Across 25 iterations I tested 68 hypotheses about main effects, demographic
disparities, and multivariable / subgroup interactions in a 50,000-patient
prostate-cancer cohort with 122 anonymized features (3 of which retained
human-readable categorical labels: feature_018 race, feature_045 insurance).
The single outcome was pfs_months (mean 3.74, range 0–14.6 months). All
analyses used OLS or rank-based tests on the full cohort with no missing data.

Strongest predictors of pfs_months
----------------------------------
1.  feature_078 (continuous, range 30–90, mean 65 — clearly an age-like
    variable). Univariate OLS slope = +0.174 months/year (p ≈ 0). Spearman
    r = +0.85. Mean PFS rises monotonically across deciles from 0.85 mo
    (lowest decile, mean value 47.5) to 6.86 mo (highest decile, mean value
    82.4). Direction is positive in this dataset — i.e., higher feature_078
    is strongly associated with longer PFS — which is the opposite of typical
    age–survival relationships in cancer; reported as observed.
2.  feature_057 (3-level ordinal, plausibly disease stage / risk group).
    Mean PFS: 4.68 mo at level 0, 3.49 at level 1, 2.38 at level 2.
    Adjusted contrasts vs level 0: −1.19 mo (level 1) and −2.32 mo (level 2),
    both p ≈ 0.
3.  feature_013 (continuous, range 0.1–3622, plausibly serum PSA).
    Spearman r = −0.22, p ≈ 0. Mean PFS fell stepwise across feature_013
    quartiles: 4.31, 3.93, 3.62, 3.11 mo (linear-trend coef = −0.39 mo/quartile,
    p ≈ 0). Adjusted log1p(feature_013) coef = −0.38 mo per log-unit.
4.  feature_051 (binary, ~55% positive — possibly a metastasis/disease-burden
    flag). Mean PFS: 4.03 mo (=0) vs 3.51 mo (=1); difference −0.52 mo,
    p < 1e-180.
5.  feature_009 (continuous, 1.5–5.5, mean 3.8 — resembles serum albumin).
    Spearman r = +0.12, adjusted coef = +0.48 mo per unit, p ≈ 0.
6.  feature_006 (continuous, 0–24.6). Spearman r = −0.13, adjusted coef
    = −0.077 mo/unit, p ≈ 0.
7.  feature_109 and feature_039 (binary, each ~10% prevalent, both protective
    in adjusted models): adjusted coefs +0.150 and +0.148 mo respectively,
    each p ≈ 1e-180.

A multivariable OLS containing the seven clinical predictors above plus
demographics achieved R² = 0.969; adding the feature_078:feature_051 and
feature_057:feature_051 interaction terms (iteration 25) raised R²_adj to
0.970. The dataset's PFS is therefore essentially fully explained by a small
set of clinical features.

Interactions and effect heterogeneity
-------------------------------------
*   feature_078 × feature_051 (age × disease-burden flag, iter 8/25):
    interaction coef ≈ −0.0045 mo per (year × flag), p < 1e-46. The PFS
    penalty associated with feature_051=1 grows roughly linearly with age.
*   feature_057 × feature_051 (stage × disease-burden, iter 9/25): jointly
    significant (p ≈ 0.007 in iter 9; in the final model the level-2 contrast
    is +0.057 mo, p ≈ 4e-9). The negative feature_051 effect is slightly
    attenuated at higher stages, presumably because PFS is already short.
*   feature_013 × feature_057 (PSA × stage, iter 12): jointly significant
    (p < 1e-4). Within-stage Spearman correlations: stage 0 r = −0.25;
    stage 1 r = −0.24; stage 2 r = −0.24 — direction is preserved across
    strata; the interaction reflects differing slope, not sign.
*   feature_109 × feature_039 (combined-flag synergy, iter 16): in the
    age/stage-adjusted model, having only one of the two flags positive
    yielded effectively no benefit (+/-0 mo, p = 0.62), but having both
    positive added +1.42 mo (p ≈ 0). This is far larger than the additive
    sum of the individual main effects (each ≈ 0.15 mo) and is the most
    striking super-additive interaction in the dataset.
*   No interactions with race or insurance were significant: race ×
    feature_051/109/039/043/035 all p > 0.2, age × race p = 0.54, PSA × race
    p = 0.34, stage × race p = 0.85, stage × insurance p = 0.48,
    insurance × top binary features all p > 0.10.

Demographic findings (race, insurance)
--------------------------------------
The cohort is 64.8% white, 15.1% hispanic, 12.1% black, 6.0% asian, 2.0%
other; 42.1% medicare, 39.7% private, 14.2% medicaid, 4.0% uninsured.
*   No main effect of race on PFS: ANOVA p = 0.69; group means span only
    3.74 to 3.80 months. Pairwise vs white: black −0.001 (p = 0.99),
    hispanic −0.004 (p = 0.89), asian +0.056 (p = 0.15), other +0.008
    (p = 0.90). Effects remain null after adjusting for age, stage, PSA,
    and other clinical covariates (all p > 0.6).
*   No main effect of insurance on PFS: ANOVA p = 0.79; group means span
    only 3.73 to 3.75 months. Pairwise vs private all p > 0.36 unadjusted
    and all p > 0.93 in the adjusted model.
*   No racial or insurance disparity in the prevalence of any of the
    high-impact clinical features tested (feature_051, feature_109,
    feature_039, feature_043, feature_057): all chi-square p > 0.19,
    prevalence ranges < 1.5 percentage points across racial groups and
    < 2 percentage points across insurance groups.
*   Mean feature_078 (age) is essentially identical across races (range
    64.96–65.23 yrs, ANOVA p = 0.67) and across insurance groups (range
    64.82–65.00 yrs, ANOVA p = 0.88). Notably, uninsured patients are not
    younger than medicare patients in this dataset, which is unusual.
*   Stratified by race or by insurance, the feature_051 effect was directionally
    consistent and significant in every subgroup (white −0.53, black −0.51,
    hispanic −0.53, asian −0.54, other −0.37 mo; private −0.51, medicare
    −0.52, medicaid −0.54, uninsured −0.60 mo, all p < 0.005).

Hypotheses supported vs refuted
-------------------------------
Supported (significant in expected direction):
*   feature_078 + on PFS (h1.1, h25.1).
*   feature_057 negative ordinal effect (h2.1, h11.1, h25.1).
*   feature_013 (log) negative on PFS (h2.2, h11.1, h12.2, h12.3).
*   feature_051 negative on PFS (h2.3), within every race & insurance stratum
    (h13.1–h13.4, h14.1–h14.4) and across stages (h21.1).
*   feature_006 negative, feature_009 positive (h3.1, h3.2, h21.1).
*   feature_109 positive, feature_039 positive (h3.3, h3.4, h10.2, h10.3).
*   age × feature_051 negative interaction (h8.1, h25.3).
*   stage × feature_051 interaction (h9.1, h25.2).
*   PSA × stage interaction (h12.1).
*   feature_109 × feature_039 super-additive synergy (h16.1).

Refuted / null:
*   Race main effect on PFS (h1.2, h5.1–h5.3) — null overall and on every
    pairwise comparison.
*   Insurance main effect on PFS (h1.3, h5.4–h5.6) — null overall and pairwise.
*   Race × clinical-feature interactions (h6.1–h6.5, h17.1, h18.1, h19.1).
*   Insurance × clinical-feature interactions (h7.1–h7.3, h20.1).
*   age × feature_109, age × feature_039, age × feature_043, age × feature_035
    interactions (h8.2, h8.3, plus the others, all p > 0.2).
*   Differential prevalence of clinical features by race or insurance
    (h22.1–h22.3, h23.1–h23.3).
*   Differential age structure by race or insurance (h24.1, h24.2).
*   Continuous biomarker × feature_051 interactions for feature_009 and
    feature_006 (h15.1, h15.2). Only feature_094 × feature_051 was
    marginally significant (p = 0.035) and the magnitude was tiny.

Overall conclusions
-------------------
PFS in this cohort is overwhelmingly determined by a small set of clinical
features — feature_078 (age-like, the dominant driver), feature_057 (stage-
like ordinal), feature_013 (PSA-like, log-scale), feature_051 (binary disease-
burden flag), feature_006, feature_009 (albumin-like), and the binary flags
feature_109 and feature_039 — together explaining >97% of variance.

There is no evidence of demographic disparity in this dataset. Race
(feature_018) and insurance (feature_045) have:
   (a) no measurable main effect on PFS,
   (b) no interaction with any of the high-impact clinical features,
   (c) no differential prevalence of those clinical features, and
   (d) no compositional age differences.

The most clinically interesting non-additive findings are:
*   The disease-burden flag (feature_051) interacts with both age and stage:
    its negative effect on PFS amplifies in older patients but attenuates at
    later stages.
*   Co-occurrence of feature_109 and feature_039 yields a strongly
    super-additive PFS benefit (~+1.4 mo vs additive ~+0.3 mo expectation),
    suggesting these flags may identify a population that responds
    synergistically to a combination of (or in the presence of) two
    favorable conditions or interventions.
