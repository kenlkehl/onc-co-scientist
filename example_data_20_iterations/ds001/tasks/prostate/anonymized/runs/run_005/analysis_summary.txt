Analysis summary — ds001_prostate (50,000 patients; outcome: pfs_months)

Overview
--------
Across 13 iterations of propose-test-refine analysis, I screened all 122 anonymized
features for univariate, multivariable, subgroup, and interaction associations with
pfs_months. The dataset is dense (no missing values) and well powered. A small set
of features explains the great majority of variance (full multivariable OLS
R-squared = 0.948), and effects are stable across race and insurance strata.

Dominant main effects (multivariable-adjusted, signed in natural units of months)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.  feature_078 (continuous, range 30-90, mean ~65): coef = +0.174 mo per unit
    (t = +833, p ~ 0). Pearson r = +0.86 univariate. By far the strongest
    predictor of pfs_months; mean feature_078 rises monotonically from 53 in the
    bottom pfs quintile to 77 in the top quintile. Behaves like a "baseline
    expected outcome" or strong continuous prognostic score.
2.  feature_057 (ordinal 0/1/2; severity-like): coef = -1.152 mo per level
    (t = -376, p ~ 0). Mean pfs_months 4.68 -> 3.49 -> 2.38 across levels.
3.  feature_006 (continuous, 0-24.6): coef = -0.077 mo per unit (t = -142).
4.  feature_013 (continuous, 0.12-3622): coef = -0.0027 mo per unit (t = -116).
5.  feature_009 (continuous, 1.5-5.5): coef = +0.478 mo per unit (t = +115).
6.  feature_051 (binary, ~55% prevalence): coef = -0.375 mo (t = -86); raw
    Welch t-test gave -0.521 mo, p = 1.6e-181.
7.  feature_092 (continuous, 48-830): coef = -0.0005 mo per unit (t = -19,
    p = 1.7e-82). Despite a near-zero univariate Pearson r, becomes clearly
    significant once other prognostic features are adjusted for.
8.  feature_109 and feature_039 (binary, ~10% prevalence): both +0.15 mo
    adjusted (p ~ 1e-100); these behave like small-benefit treatment markers.

Hypotheses refuted or not supported
-----------------------------------
* Race (feature_018) and insurance (feature_045) had no detectable association
  with pfs_months either marginally (ANOVA p = 0.69 and 0.79) or after
  multivariable adjustment (all dummy p > 0.15). Mean pfs_months by race ranges
  only 3.74 to 3.80; by insurance 3.73 to 3.75.
* Other multi-level integer features (feature_067, feature_016, feature_026,
  feature_033, feature_064, feature_071, feature_096) showed no useful
  monotonic trend with pfs_months (univariate Spearman |rho| < 0.005, all
  p > 0.20). Only feature_067 reached modest adjusted significance (t = -5.4,
  p = 5.7e-8) but with a tiny effect (-0.011 mo per level).
* feature_007 is constant (single observed value 0) and is uninformative; its
  apparent regression t-statistic is a numerical artifact from collinearity
  with the intercept.
* No race x treatment interactions were detected. The harm of feature_051 is
  essentially identical across asian, black, hispanic, white, other (range
  -0.37 to -0.54 mo, all interaction terms p > 0.20). The benefit of
  feature_109 is positive in every race subgroup with no significant
  modification (smallest interaction p = 0.59).
* feature_109 x insurance interactions are mostly null; only the
  "private vs medicaid" contrast reaches p < 0.01 and the modulation is small.

Significant interactions (clinically meaningful effect modification)
--------------------------------------------------------------------
1.  feature_078 x feature_057: coef = -0.0095 (p = 3e-209). The dominant
    interaction in the dataset. The per-level penalty of feature_057 grows by
    ~0.10 mo for every 10-unit rise in feature_078; equivalently, the per-unit
    benefit of feature_078 attenuates as severity rises.
2.  feature_051 x feature_078: coef = -0.0043 (p = 4.6e-24). The harm from
    feature_051 amplifies as feature_078 increases (raw effect rises from
    -0.45 mo in the lowest feature_078 quartile to about -0.54 mo in upper
    quartiles).
3.  feature_051 x feature_057: coef = +0.0268 (p = 1.7e-5). The harm from
    feature_051 attenuates at higher severity (feature_057=0 -> -0.56 mo;
    feature_057=2 -> -0.47 mo). Direction is opposite to interaction #2 — at
    the highest severity there is less remaining "room" for additional harm.
4.  feature_078 x feature_006: coef = -0.00062 (p = 1.1e-29). The negative
    effect of feature_006 amplifies with feature_078.
5.  feature_078 x feature_009: coef = +0.0044 (p = 2.5e-25). The positive
    effect of feature_009 amplifies with feature_078.
6.  feature_057 x feature_006: coef = +0.0042 (p = 2.1e-7). Severity attenuates
    the harm of feature_006.
7.  feature_057 x feature_009: coef = -0.022 (p = 3.2e-4). Severity attenuates
    the benefit of feature_009.
8.  feature_109 x feature_092: coef = -0.00021 (p = 0.015). The benefit of
    feature_109 is reduced at higher feature_092 levels — modest signal.
9.  Four lower-prevalence binaries (feature_112, feature_046, feature_108,
    feature_040) show p < 0.01 interactions with feature_078 but would not
    survive strict multiplicity correction across 77 tested features and have
    very small effect magnitudes.

Overall conclusions
-------------------
* pfs_months in this synthetic prostate-cancer cohort is largely a function of
  feature_078 (strong positive continuous prognostic score) and feature_057
  (strong negative ordinal severity), with secondary contributions from a
  small set of biomarker-like continuous features and one strongly negative
  binary feature (feature_051).
* The data are consistent with a model in which feature_078 acts as a global
  "expected pfs" baseline that is then modulated up or down by other
  predictors, while feature_057 acts as a severity ceiling that compresses
  the magnitude of those modulations.
* There is no evidence of bias by race or insurance: pfs_months distributions
  are nearly identical across feature_018 categories and across feature_045
  categories, and treatment-like features (feature_051, feature_109,
  feature_039) act consistently regardless of race or insurance.
* Treatment-relevant signals (positive: feature_109, feature_039; negative:
  feature_051) are robust and reproducible after adjustment, and their
  effects are partially modulated by the dominant prognostic axes
  (feature_078 and feature_057) rather than by demographic factors.
