Analysis Summary — ds001_prostate (n = 50,000; outcome: pfs_months)
====================================================================

Overview
--------
Across 25 iterations I worked through marginal univariate associations,
multivariable adjustment, demographic disparity checks, treatment
identification, and treatment-effect heterogeneity. The cohort is
remarkably well explained by a small number of baseline factors plus
one combination-treatment interaction.

Five core baseline predictors (multivariable OLS, R² = 0.969 from these
alone, all p ≪ 0.001):

  • feature_078 (continuous, range 30-90; behaves like an age- or
    age-equivalent prognostic axis): β = +0.174 months per unit. The
    univariate Pearson r is +0.856 — the dominant signal in the dataset.
  • feature_057 (3-level ordinal 0/1/2, behaves like a metastasis-burden
    indicator): β = −1.150 months per level. Mean PFS by level: 4.68 /
    3.49 / 2.38 mo.
  • log(feature_013) (continuous, range 0.12-3622; PSA-like):
    β = −0.349 months per log unit.
  • feature_009 (continuous, range 1.5-5.5, mean 3.8; albumin-like):
    β = +0.479 months per unit.
  • feature_006 (continuous, range 0-24.6 with 27.5% zeros): β = −0.077
    months per unit.

Several variables that one might *expect* to be prognostic in prostate
cancer were not, on their own:

  • feature_067 (range 6-10, Gleason-like): no main effect on PFS
    (r = −0.003, p = 0.49). Critically, however, it is a strong **effect
    modifier** for the combination treatment (see below).
  • feature_016, feature_071, feature_096, feature_033, feature_064
    (each 0-4 ordinal, plausibly TNM-like): no association with PFS
    (|r| < 0.01 in every case).

Demographic effects — no disparities detected
---------------------------------------------
Race (feature_018; white/hispanic/black/asian/other) had no marginal
effect on PFS (one-way ANOVA F = 0.56, p = 0.689; pairwise white-vs-
black p = 0.99, white-vs-hispanic p = 0.89). After adjustment for the
five core covariates, all race coefficients had |β| < 0.003 with p
> 0.66.

Insurance (feature_045; private/medicare/medicaid/uninsured) likewise
had no marginal effect (ANOVA F = 0.34, p = 0.79) and no adjusted
effect (all |β| < 0.0005, p > 0.88).

This null disparity finding holds at multiple levels of analysis —
crude means, adjusted main effects, treatment receipt, and within-
treatment response (see treatment section).

Discovery of a combination treatment
------------------------------------
After adjusting for the five core covariates and screening all 77
binary features, two stood out: feature_109 (β = +0.152 mo,
p = 6.6×10⁻¹⁸¹) and feature_039 (β = +0.149 mo, p = 6.2×10⁻¹⁷⁶), each
with prevalence ~10%. They are statistically independent of each other
(crosstab χ² = 0.9, p = 0.35), but their PFS effects are not — when
both are entered jointly with an interaction term, the main effects
collapse to ≈ 0 (p > 0.93 each) and the interaction term carries the
entire signal (β = +1.437 mo, p ≈ 0). The two indicators behave as a
**combination regimen**: only the 1.06% of patients (n = 529) with
both = 1 receive the benefit.

Adjusted combo effect (one indicator for the joint condition):
β = +1.437 mo, p ≈ 0. Crude means: combo 5.22 mo vs non-combo 3.74 mo.

Effect modification of the combination treatment
-------------------------------------------------
The combo benefit is dramatically modified by Gleason-like grade
(feature_067):

  Gleason 6 : combo +2.79 mo (p = 3×10⁻²⁶)
  Gleason 7 : combo +2.81 mo (p = 2×10⁻⁸⁷)
  Gleason 8 : combo +0.06 mo (NS)
  Gleason 9 : combo +0.38 mo (NS)
  Gleason 10: combo +0.07 mo (NS)

A formal interaction test (combo × low_Gleason indicator, where
low = 6-7) yields β = +2.795, p ≈ 0; the un-interacted combo
coefficient drops to +0.021 (p = 0.26). Thus the combination treatment
is essentially **only effective in low-Gleason patients**. Cell means
by (feature_109, feature_039) within low-Gleason: (0,0)=3.73, (0,1)=
3.68, (1,0)=3.69, (1,1)=6.52. Within high-Gleason: (0,0)=3.75, (0,1)=
3.66, (1,0)=3.66, (1,1)=3.87.

Other treatment-effect modifiers:
  • log(feature_013) (PSA-like): combo benefit shrinks ~0.11 mo per
    unit log (β = −0.109, p = 4.5×10⁻²⁴).
  • feature_078: combo benefit shrinks slightly with higher
    feature_078 (β = −0.007 per unit, p = 3.2×10⁻⁷).
  • feature_057 (metastasis-like, 3-level): NO interaction with combo
    in adjusted model (β = −0.006, p = 0.76). Stratum-specific raw
    benefits are 1.70 / 1.34 / 1.64 months — broadly consistent.

Disparity checks for the combination treatment
-----------------------------------------------
Receipt of the combo by race (within low-Gleason, where the treatment
matters): asian 1.27%, black 1.08%, hispanic 0.97%, other 0.99%, white
1.06%. χ² = 0.96, p = 0.92. **No racial disparity in access.**

Receipt by insurance (within low-Gleason): medicaid 1.11%, medicare
1.09%, private 0.99%, uninsured 1.33%. χ² = 1.35, p = 0.72. **No
insurance disparity in access.**

Combo response by race (treatment-effect heterogeneity from race-by-
combo interaction): coefficients vs white reference are Black β =
−0.076 (p = 0.10), Hispanic β = −0.042 (p = 0.31), Asian β = +0.180
(p = 0.004), Other β = +0.517 (p < 0.0001). The two significant
results favor minority groups; Black and Hispanic patients are
statistically indistinguishable from white patients. Within the small
group of combo-treated low-Gleason patients (n = 268), mean PFS is
asian 6.20, black 6.19, hispanic 6.51, other 6.02, white 6.64 — close
together with overlapping uncertainty.

Combo response by insurance: vs private reference, Medicare β = +0.149
(p < 0.0001), Medicaid β = +0.264 (p < 0.0001), Uninsured β = +0.234
(p = 0.0009). All non-private groups show a *larger* combo benefit
than privately insured patients — the opposite direction from a
conventional disparity hypothesis.

Bottom line on disparities: there is no statistical signal — at any
modeling stage — that race or insurance is associated with worse
pfs_months in this cohort, with worse access to the effective
combination treatment, or with diminished response when treated.

Other small effects worth noting
--------------------------------
  • feature_027 (binary, 60% prevalence): adjusted β = +0.024 mo,
    p = 1.2×10⁻¹⁵. Small but real positive effect; balanced in
    prevalence across demographic groups.
  • feature_043 (binary, 20% prevalence): adjusted β = −0.016 mo,
    p = 1.8×10⁻⁵. Small negative effect; prevalence balanced across
    race (19.5-20.1%) and insurance (19.7-21.6%).
  • feature_051, which had the largest univariate binary effect
    (mean diff = −0.52 mo), is fully confounded by the core
    covariates: adjusted β = −0.002 (p = 0.65).

Effect modification among baseline predictors
---------------------------------------------
The prognostic value of every favorable baseline factor diminishes as
feature_057 (metastasis-like burden) rises:
  • feature_078 × feature_057: β = −0.0097 (p ≈ 0)
  • feature_009 × feature_057: β = −0.026 (p = 3×10⁻⁸)
  • log(feature_013) × feature_057: β = +0.021 (p = 5×10⁻³²) — the
    negative log-PSA slope attenuates at higher levels.

This is biologically coherent: as disease burden grows, baseline
features become less informative.

Final parsimonious model
------------------------
A model with 5 core covariates + combo + low_Gleason indicator + their
interaction reaches R² = 0.9792 (8 terms total). Coefficients (all p
≪ 0.001 unless noted):

  intercept              −7.185
  feature_078            +0.1737
  log(feature_013)       −0.349
  feature_009            +0.479
  feature_006            −0.077
  feature_057            −1.151
  combo (in high-G)      +0.021     (p = 0.26)
  low_Gleason (combo=0)  −0.0015    (p = 0.56)
  combo × low_Gleason    +2.795

Conclusions
-----------
1. PFS in this cohort is largely a function of five baseline
   biological factors, and these are highly informative regardless of
   the patient's race or insurance status.
2. A specific combination of two binary indicators (feature_109 AND
   feature_039) functions as a treatment regimen that confers a large
   absolute PFS gain (~+2.8 months on average).
3. That treatment effect is essentially **restricted to low-Gleason
   (feature_067 ≤ 7) patients**. In high-Gleason patients the same
   combination yields no measurable benefit. This is the most
   actionable subgroup-by-treatment interaction in the dataset.
4. Across both access (combo receipt) and effect (combo response)
   axes, race and insurance show **no evidence of disparity**;
   directionally, where any difference exists, public-insurance and
   minority-race patients tend to do at least as well as their
   reference comparators, not worse.
5. Several other categorical features that look like classic
   prostate-cancer staging variables (Gleason main effect, T/N/M-like
   ordinals) do not have main effects on PFS, but Gleason emerges as
   the key effect modifier for treatment response — a clinically
   meaningful distinction.
