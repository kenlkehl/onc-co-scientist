Analysis Summary — ds001_breast (n=50,000; outcome: pfs_months)
=================================================================

Overview
--------
The cohort comprises 50,000 patients with 127 anonymized features (82 binary,
7 ordinal/integer, 36 continuous, plus race [feature_011] and insurance
[feature_089] as categoricals) and a single continuous outcome, pfs_months
(progression-free survival in months; mean 4.69, median 4.54, range
0.00–16.66). No missing values were present.

Across 25 iterations of hypothesis generation and statistical testing, the
data revealed a small set of dominant effects:

(1) a powerful linear continuous prognostic factor (feature_080),
(2) a strong ordinal stage-like prognostic factor (feature_063),
(3) two uniformly negative binary prognostic factors (feature_056,
    feature_048),
(4) a striking treatment-biomarker interaction in which feature_042 confers a
    ~+2.9-month PFS gain only when a specific composite biomarker
    (feature_111=1 AND feature_015=0 AND feature_040=0) is present, and zero
    benefit otherwise,
(5) three additional independent continuous prognostic covariates
    (feature_067, feature_019, feature_101), and
(6) no detectable disparity in outcomes, treatment receipt, or treatment
    benefit by race (feature_011) or insurance (feature_089).

The final multivariable model with these effects achieves Adj R² = 0.876,
indicating that the dataset's outcome variance is largely captured by this
parsimonious structure.


Iteration-by-iteration narrative
--------------------------------

Iteration 1 (continuous screening). I examined Pearson correlations of all 36
continuous features with pfs_months. feature_080 (range 30–90, mean ~65,
plausibly age) emerged as the dominant predictor with r = +0.7015 (p < 1e-300)
— positively associated with PFS. By itself it explains ~49% of pfs_months
variance.

Iteration 2 (ordinal screening). Among the 7 multi-level integer features,
feature_063 (3 levels with marginals 0:35.2%, 1:49.9%, 2:14.9%) had a strong
monotone negative association with PFS (mean 5.64 → 4.44 → 3.29 mo across
levels; Spearman ρ = -0.334, p < 1e-300). This is the only ordinal feature
with prognostic signal — the other six ordinals (feature_021, 024, 016, 127,
001, 073) all had |ρ| < 0.006 and p > 0.20 (Iteration 7).

Iterations 3–5 (binary screening). The strongest binary main effects
(unadjusted Welch t-tests) were:
  feature_056 (prev 0.30): -1.54 mo (p<1e-300)
  feature_042 (prev 0.35): +1.10 mo (p<1e-300)
  feature_048 (prev 0.10): -1.05 mo (p=1.5e-178)
  feature_015 (prev 0.35): -0.56 mo (p=6.1e-144)
  feature_111 (prev 0.70): +0.57 mo (p=3.7e-143)
  feature_040 (prev 0.18): -0.46 mo (p=6.1e-70)
  feature_039 (prev 0.64): +0.36 mo (p=5.8e-59)
All other binary features had |Δ| < 0.15 mo and most had p > 0.05.

Iteration 6 (other continuous covariates). Three additional continuous
features showed modest but highly significant prognostic correlations:
feature_067 (r=-0.116), feature_019 (r=+0.100), feature_101 (r=-0.091).

Iteration 8 (race and insurance, unadjusted). Crude mean PFS was nearly
identical across racial groups (4.68–4.76 mo; ANOVA p=0.49) and across payers
(4.69–4.73 mo; p=0.82). No demographic disparity in raw outcome.

Iteration 9 (multivariable adjustment). Fitting an OLS model with feature_080,
feature_063, and the top eight binary features confirmed that six remained
strongly significant after adjustment (feature_056 -1.55 mo, feature_042
+1.10 mo, feature_048 -0.97 mo, feature_111 +0.54 mo, feature_015 -0.59 mo,
feature_040 -0.47 mo). The univariately significant features feature_039,
feature_034, feature_068, feature_028, feature_020 all attenuated to
nonsignificance (|β|<0.02 mo, p>0.13), indicating their crude effects were
driven by collinearity with the dominant predictors. Adjusted R² = 0.76.

Iterations 10–12 (pairwise interactions). I scanned binary×binary interactions
among the top six binary features. Three very strong interactions emerged:
  feature_042 × feature_111: +1.58 mo (p<1e-300) — feature_042 helps only when
    feature_111=1 (within-stratum diff: +1.58 mo if 111=1, -0.02 mo if 111=0).
  feature_042 × feature_015: -1.67 mo (p<1e-300) — feature_042 helps only
    when feature_015=0 (within-stratum: +1.66 if 015=0, +0.04 if 015=1).
  feature_042 × feature_040: -1.38 mo (p<1e-300) — feature_042 helps only
    when feature_040=0 (within-stratum: +1.35 if 040=0, -0.01 if 040=1).
By contrast, feature_056×feature_042, feature_056×feature_111, etc. all had
|coef|<0.05 and p>0.25 — no interaction.

Iteration 13 (composite biomarker). Defining a composite "biomarker_eligible"
indicator = feature_111=1 AND feature_015=0 AND feature_040=0 yielded a
near-perfect treatment–biomarker dichotomy:
  - Biomarker-positive (n=18,681; 37.4% of cohort): feature_042 +2.91 mo
    (7.21 vs 4.30; p<1e-300).
  - Biomarker-negative (n=31,319): feature_042 +0.003 mo (p=0.91).
The treatment is clinically valuable only in a defined subgroup.

Iterations 14–15 (effect homogeneity within biomarker-positive). Within the
biomarker-positive subgroup, the magnitude of feature_042's benefit was
essentially constant across feature_063 stage (Δ ~+2.92 mo at all three
levels) and across feature_080 quartiles (Δ ~+2.80 to +2.98 mo). The
treatment effect is large, uniform, and not modified by stage or by the
dominant continuous prognostic factor.

Iteration 16 (uniformity of negative-prognostic features). feature_056 and
feature_048 showed uniformly negative effects across all candidate biomarker
strata (feature_056: -1.5 to -1.6 mo across feature_111, feature_015,
feature_040, feature_063 levels; feature_048: -0.9 to -1.1 mo). These behave
as generalized adverse prognostic factors (e.g., comorbid disease or visceral
metastasis) rather than as predictive biomarkers tied to any specific
treatment.

Iterations 17–19 (demographic equity). I tested two crucial fairness
questions for this dataset:
  - Does race or insurance modify the treatment effect of feature_042 within
    the biomarker-positive subgroup? Effect magnitude by race: white +2.89,
    asian +2.98, black +2.90, hispanic +2.96, other +2.87 mo (all
    p<1e-15). By insurance: medicaid +3.02, private +2.93, medicare +2.86,
    uninsured +2.77 mo (all p<1e-31). All within ~0.25 mo of each other; no
    disparity in benefit.
  - Are receipt rates of feature_042 within the biomarker-positive subgroup
    equal across race/insurance? Yes: race chi-square p=0.85, insurance
    chi-square p=0.21. Treatment uptake among eligible patients is equitable.

Iteration 20 (upstream stage equity). Distribution of feature_063 (the
stage-like covariate) was nearly identical across races (chi-square p=0.89)
and payers (p=0.69), ruling out an upstream "patients of group X present at
later stage" disparity.

Iterations 21–22 (continuous covariate quartile patterns). feature_080 has a
monotone, approximately linear association: Q1 mean 2.28 mo to Q5 mean
7.21 mo. feature_067 (Q1 4.98 → Q4 4.29), feature_019 (Q1 4.39 → Q4 4.98),
and feature_101 (Q1 4.94 → Q4 4.31) each retained independent significance
after multivariable adjustment (β = -0.077, +0.482, -0.019 mo per unit
respectively; all p<1e-300).

Iteration 23 (residual demographic disparity test). In the fully adjusted
model (containing feature_080, C(feature_063), feature_056, feature_048,
feature_111, feature_015, feature_040, feature_042 + interaction with
biomarker, feature_067, feature_019, feature_101, race, insurance), all race
coefficients had |β|<0.03 mo and p>0.11; all insurance coefficients had
|β|<0.04 mo and p>0.14. No residual demographic association with PFS.

Iteration 24 (final model fit). The multivariable model attains Adj R² =
0.876 on n=50,000. The largest standardized contributors are: feature_080
(t=445), feature_063 levels (t=-137 to -192), feature_042:biomarker_eligible
(t=184), feature_056 (t=-181), feature_048 (t=-74), feature_019 (t=61),
feature_067 (t=-75), feature_101 (t=-58). Notably, feature_042's main effect
shrinks to +0.004 mo (p=0.71) once the biomarker interaction is included —
the entire treatment benefit is concentrated in the biomarker-eligible
subgroup.

Iteration 25 (residual binary scan). A systematic scan of feature_042 ×
each-other-binary interaction (adjusting for feature_080 and feature_063)
detected only the previously identified four (feature_111, feature_015,
feature_040, feature_039 — where feature_039 is largely collinear with
feature_111) as having coefficients above ±1 mo. The next-strongest, feature_034
× feature_042 = +0.40 mo (p=2.8e-42), is roughly fourfold smaller and likely
reflects partial overlap with the biomarker structure. No additional
predictive biomarker is hidden in the remaining 75 binary features.

Overall conclusions
-------------------
1. The pfs_months outcome in this cohort is overwhelmingly driven by:
   - a single dominant linear continuous prognostic factor (feature_080,
     plausibly age or a hormone-receptor quantitative variable),
   - a 3-level ordinal stage-like factor (feature_063),
   - two uniform negative binary prognostic factors (feature_056,
     feature_048; possibly comorbidity, visceral metastasis, or unfavorable
     histology), and
   - three additional continuous prognostic covariates (feature_067,
     feature_019, feature_101).

2. There is one clear treatment–biomarker relationship in the dataset:
   feature_042 confers ~+2.9 months of PFS only in patients defined by
   feature_111=1 AND feature_015=0 AND feature_040=0 (37% of the cohort).
   In the remaining 63% of patients the treatment is precisely null.
   The benefit is homogeneous across stage and across the dominant
   continuous prognostic factor (consistent with a true predictive
   biomarker rather than effect-modification by prognosis).

3. The dataset shows no evidence of demographic disparity. Race
   (feature_011) and insurance (feature_089) are not associated with
   pfs_months either crudely or after multivariable adjustment, do not
   modify the treatment effect of feature_042 within the biomarker-
   positive subgroup, are not associated with treatment receipt rates among
   biomarker-eligible patients, and are not associated with stage at
   presentation. From the perspective of this cohort, care delivery and
   outcomes appear equitable.

4. A parsimonious model containing the above structure (≈12 terms)
   explains 88% of pfs_months variance, suggesting that the remaining ~120
   binary, ordinal, and continuous features carry essentially no
   independent prognostic or predictive signal beyond what is already
   captured.
