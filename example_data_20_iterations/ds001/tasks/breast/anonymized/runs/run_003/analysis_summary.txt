Analysis Summary — ds001_breast (n = 50,000; outcome = pfs_months)
===================================================================

Overview
--------
This 25-iteration analysis explored 127 anonymized patient features against
the continuous outcome pfs_months (mean 4.69, SD 2.50, range 0–16.7 months).
Two object-typed features were treated as categorical (feature_011 race/
ethnicity; feature_089 insurance), 89 features were integer-valued (82 binary,
7 low-cardinality ordinal), and 37 features were continuous floats including
feature_080 (age-like, 30–90 years). The cohort had no missing values.

Iterative process
-----------------
Iterations 1–8 ran a screening pass over individual features, prioritized
by univariate t-tests / Pearson correlations / one-way ANOVAs. Iterations
9–11 built joint multivariable models and tested demographics. Iterations
12–22 examined non-linearity, two-way interactions, and a 3-way subgroup
that explains the dominant treatment-effect heterogeneity. Iterations 23–25
addressed subgroup heterogeneity by race, residual continuous candidates,
and a final consolidated model.

Hypotheses supported by the evidence
------------------------------------
1.  Patient age (feature_080, 30–90 years) is a strong positive correlate of
    pfs_months: Pearson r = +0.702 (p ≈ 0); decile means rise monotonically
    from 1.70 months in the youngest decile (<52 yr) to 7.84 months in the
    oldest decile (>78 yr). The relationship is also slightly convex —
    adding an age² term remains highly significant (p ≈ 6 × 10⁻²⁵, positive
    curvature). In a fully adjusted model the per-year effect is +0.18
    months / yr (95% CI ≈ +0.175 to +0.177).

2.  feature_063 (ordinal 0/1/2; level frequencies 35%/50%/15%) is a strong
    negative predictor of pfs_months. Means by level: 0 → 5.64 mo, 1 → 4.44 mo,
    2 → 3.29 mo (Pearson r = −0.32, adjusted slope −1.17 mo per level,
    p ≈ 0). Behaviour is consistent with a performance-status / disease-
    severity ordinal.

3.  feature_056 (binary, 30% prevalence) is a strong negative prognostic
    factor: mean pfs_months 3.62 with feature_056 = 1 vs 5.15 with
    feature_056 = 0, difference −1.54 months (Welch p ≈ 0). The effect
    persists in every age stratum and in every level of feature_063, with
    only a small, statistically significant but quantitatively trivial
    interaction with feature_080 (−0.011 mo / yr, p ≈ 3 × 10⁻¹²) and
    feature_063 (+0.07 mo / level, p = 0.04). Adjusted main effect ≈ −0.92
    to −1.55 months depending on the model specification.

4.  feature_048 (binary, 10% prevalence) is a strong negative prognostic
    factor: mean PFS 3.75 vs 4.80, adjusted effect −0.97 months
    (p < 1 × 10⁻¹⁷⁸).

5.  Three continuous features carry independent prognostic information:
       feature_067 (range 0–25, mean 3.84) — adjusted effect −0.076 / unit,
                                              p < 1 × 10⁻⁵⁰
       feature_019 (range 1.5–5.5, mean 3.80) — adjusted effect +0.48 / unit,
                                                 p < 1 × 10⁻³⁰⁰
       feature_101 (range 1–100, mean 15.5)  — adjusted effect −0.019 / unit,
                                                 p < 1 × 10⁻¹⁰⁰

6.  The most important *predictive* (treatment-effect-modifying) finding is
    a 3-way subgroup interaction governing the effect of feature_042
    (binary, 35% prevalence). In its raw univariate test feature_042 is
    associated with +1.10 months (4.31 → 5.41, p ≈ 0). However:
       • feature_042 × feature_111: pairwise interaction +1.59 (p ≈ 9e-229);
       • feature_042 × feature_015: pairwise interaction −1.62 (p ≈ 4e-255);
       • feature_042 × feature_040: pairwise interaction −1.36 (p ≈ 2e-118).
    Defining the predictive subgroup as feature_111 = 1 AND feature_015 = 0
    AND feature_040 = 0 (n = 18,681; 37% of the cohort), the feature_042
    effect within that subgroup is +2.91 months (4.30 → 7.21, p ≈ 0),
    whereas in the complementary subgroup (n = 31,319) the feature_042
    effect is +0.003 months (p = 0.91). The benefit of feature_042 is
    therefore essentially confined to a molecular subgroup.

7.  Once feature_111, feature_015, and feature_040 are entered together with
    their feature_042 interactions in a single OLS model, their independent
    main-effect coefficients shrink to |coef| < 0.005 (p > 0.89). They
    therefore behave as predictive markers (treatment-effect modifiers)
    rather than prognostic markers. The same model retains an R² of 0.84
    and confirms feature_042's positive main effect (+0.83) only in the
    presence of the predictive markers.

Hypotheses refuted (or not supported)
-------------------------------------
1.  Self-reported race/ethnicity (feature_011) has no detectable association
    with pfs_months. Unadjusted means: white 4.68, black 4.72, asian 4.74,
    hispanic 4.71, other 4.76 (n's 32498/5889/2970/7631/1012); ANOVA p ≈ 0.40.
    After adjusting for the top prognostic features, all race indicators
    have |coef| < 0.03 with p > 0.28. There is also no race × feature_042
    treatment-effect heterogeneity within the predictive subgroup (effect
    +2.33 to +2.46 months across all five categories).

2.  Insurance type (feature_089) has no detectable association: ANOVA
    p ≈ 0.82; adjusted indicators all |coef| < 0.02, p > 0.59.

3.  feature_039 appeared in iteration 8 as a small positive marginal
    predictor (+0.36, p ≈ 6e-59) and showed a pairwise interaction with
    feature_042 (+1.09, p ≈ 3e-117). However once the larger feature_042 ×
    feature_111 / × feature_015 / × feature_040 interactions are jointly
    fit, the feature_039 main effect and the feature_042 × feature_039
    interaction both lose significance (|coef|<0.05, p ≥ 0.06). It is
    therefore a confounded surrogate for the dominant predictive markers,
    not a true independent treatment-effect modifier.

4.  feature_034's small marginal effect (+0.114, p ≈ 1e-6) is not retained
    in the multivariable model (p = 0.88).

5.  Low-cardinality ordinal features feature_021, feature_024, feature_127,
    feature_001, feature_073, feature_016 — none significant (ANOVA p ≥ 0.27).

6.  feature_125 (integer 3–36, mean 15) — Pearson r = −0.001, p = 0.80; not
    informative.

7.  feature_087 (range 88–100, mean 96; possibly a KPS-like score) and
    feature_007 (range 128–152, mean 140; possibly a sodium-like lab) — both
    null (|r| ≤ 0.005, p ≥ 0.35).

Overall conclusions
-------------------
• pfs_months in this cohort is dominated by a strong positive age effect
  (~+0.18 mo/yr), an ordinal severity-like factor feature_063, and four
  binary/continuous prognostic factors (feature_056, feature_048,
  feature_067, feature_019, feature_101).
• The treatment/biomarker structure of the dataset is best summarized as
  a single intervention (feature_042) whose benefit (~+2.9 months in PFS)
  is restricted to a molecular subgroup defined jointly by three binary
  markers: feature_111 must be positive, while feature_015 and feature_040
  must both be negative. Outside that subgroup, feature_042 confers no
  PFS benefit.
• Demographic variables (feature_011 race; feature_089 insurance) do not
  independently affect pfs_months and do not modify the feature_042
  treatment effect.
• Apparent univariate signals from feature_039 and feature_034 are fully
  explained by their correlation with the genuine predictive markers and
  do not represent independent biology.
• A parsimonious model with main effects of the seven prognostic factors
  plus the three feature_042 × biomarker interactions and an age² term
  achieves R² ≈ 0.84 — a high level of explanation for a clinical PFS
  outcome — and is consistent across age strata, levels of feature_063,
  and racial/ethnic groups.
