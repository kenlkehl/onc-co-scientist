ANALYSIS SUMMARY — ds001_nsclc cohort (N=50,000, objective_response rate = 16.9%)
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
The dataset contains 122 anonymized features (77 binary, 35 continuous, 7
ordinal-integer, 4 categorical) and one binary outcome (objective_response).
I executed 25 iterations of propose-test-refine. The protocol began with broad
univariate screens, moved to multivariable adjustment, and then drilled into
interactions, subgroup analyses (histology, smoking, race, insurance, age,
severity), dose-response patterns, joint phenotypes, and out-of-sample
validation.

TOP-LINE FINDINGS
-----------------
A small cluster of features carries virtually all of the explainable signal.
After multivariable adjustment for one another, nine predictors remain robustly
associated with objective response (all p < 0.001) with stable directions:

NEGATIVE predictors (lower response when feature is high/present):
  feature_051 — ordinal 0/1/2 (likely a severity/stage proxy). Strongest
    single signal in the dataset. Stratum response rates 21.1% / 15.7% / 11.0%;
    OR per +1 unit ~ 0.66; multivariable adjusted-OR per +1 SD = 0.77;
    p ~ 1.5×10⁻⁹⁵.
  feature_013 — high-prevalence binary (~65% positive). Adjusted OR = 0.74;
    p = 1.5×10⁻³⁴. Univariate response 15.4% (=1) vs 19.6% (=0).
  feature_067 — moderate-prevalence binary (~25%). Adjusted OR = 0.77;
    p = 4.0×10⁻¹⁹. Univariate 14.4% vs 17.7%.
  feature_011 — continuous, range 0–28, mean 3.8. Strong monotonic dose-response
    (rate 19.3% at 0, falling to 12.8% above 10). Adjusted OR per +1 SD = 0.86;
    p = 1.7×10⁻³³. Likely encodes disease burden (e.g., metastatic-site count).
  feature_063 — continuous lab marker, right-skewed, range 0.03–296.88.
    Quartile rates fall from 18.1% to 16.0%. Adjusted OR per +1 SD = 0.96;
    p = 5.2×10⁻⁴. Pattern is consistent with an unfavorable lab marker.
  feature_112 — low-prevalence binary, modest negative effect (adjusted
    OR = 0.90, p = 0.029).

POSITIVE predictors (higher response when feature is high/present):
  feature_006, feature_007, feature_039 — three uncorrelated binaries with
    individually modest positive effects (univariate ORs 1.07–1.12). Each
    independently significant in the multivariable model. Notably, they show
    pairwise positive interactions on the log-odds scale: feature_006×feature_039
    (p=4×10⁻⁴), feature_007×feature_039 (p=2×10⁻³), feature_006×feature_007
    (p=4×10⁻³). Patients with all three positive (n=3,216) respond at 23.0%
    vs 16.1% for none (a 6.9 pp absolute boost; trend test p=1×10⁻¹⁰).
  feature_099 — continuous, range 1.7–5.5. Quartile rates rise from 16.0% to
    17.7%. Adjusted OR per +1 SD = 1.05; p = 1.1×10⁻⁴. Pattern consistent
    with a favorable lab marker (e.g., albumin-like).
  feature_076 — low-prevalence binary, modest positive (adjusted OR = 1.10,
    p = 0.022).

WHAT IS NOT ASSOCIATED
----------------------
  Age (feature_078, range 30–90): completely flat across quartiles (16.5–17.5%);
    univariate p = 0.67.
  Smoking (feature_057, never/former/current): response rates 16.4 / 16.8 /
    17.2%; chi-square p = 0.27.
  Histology (feature_043, adeno vs squamous): adeno 16.7% vs squamous 17.4%,
    p = 0.078; non-significant after adjustment (p = 0.15).
  Race (feature_123): borderline raw p = 0.045 (white slightly lower at 16.5%
    vs 17–18.5% in other groups), but non-significant after multivariable
    adjustment for biomarkers and severity.
  Insurance (feature_005): non-significant raw (p = 0.23) and adjusted.
  Six other ordinal integer features (feature_026/033/064/018/045/042): all
    near-null with no monotonic pattern.
  ~70 of the 77 binary features and ~30 of the 35 continuous features show no
    meaningful univariate or adjusted association with response.

INTERACTION/SUBGROUP RESULTS
----------------------------
A core question was whether any of the top predictors interact with one another
or are modified by patient demographics or histology. The clean answer is
mostly “no, the effects are additive on the log-odds scale”:

  • Histology × top predictors: all p_int > 0.22. Effects replicate within
    both adenocarcinoma (n=36,099) and squamous (n=13,901) subgroups.
  • Race × top predictors: all p_int > 0.30. No effect modification by race.
  • Smoking × top predictors: all p_int > 0.20. Effect modification not
    detected.
  • Severity (feature_051) × biomarker score: p = 0.41. The ~6 pp boost from
    biomarker positivity is preserved across severity strata.
  • feature_011 × feature_006: p_int = 0.135 (NS, slight trend toward
    attenuation at higher burden).
  • feature_013 × feature_067: p_int = 0.50. The two negative predictors
    behave independently; joint OR ≈ product of marginals (≈0.58 vs neither).

The only positive interactions detected are within the favorable trio
(feature_006/007/039), driving the pronounced jump in response when all three
are present.

DOSE-RESPONSE AND PHENOTYPE EXTREMES
-------------------------------------
feature_011 shows a clear continuous dose-response (rate falls from 19.3% at 0
to 12.8% above 10). feature_051 shows a near-linear-on-log-odds drop across
its three categories. Combining favorable predictors yields a small "best"
profile (low burden, low severity, all 3 positive markers, no negative markers;
n=85) with response rate 29.4%, while the worst profile (high burden, high
severity, both negative markers; n=102) responds at 8.8% — a ~3.3-fold
relative spread.

OUT-OF-SAMPLE VALIDATION
-------------------------
A 50/50 random split (seed 42) gave a held-out AUC of 0.604 (train AUC 0.599)
for a 9-predictor logistic model. The near-identical train/test performance
shows the effects are real signal, not overfitting. Discrimination remains
modest (AUC ~0.60, pseudo-R² ~0.02), reflecting the fundamentally stochastic
nature of objective response: with a 17% base rate and a 50,000-patient
cohort, even very small effects achieve high statistical power, but most
patient-level variation in response remains unexplained by the recorded
features.

HYPOTHESES SUPPORTED VS REFUTED
--------------------------------
SUPPORTED:
  • At least one binary feature is associated with response (h1).
  • At least one continuous feature is associated with response (h2).
  • feature_051 shows monotonic negative dose-response (h3a).
  • Top predictors retain independent effects in the multivariable model (h4).
  • Pairwise positive interactions exist among feature_006/007/039 (h5).
  • Combined biomarker score has a monotonic positive trend with response (h6).
  • Effects of top predictors replicate across histology subgroups (h7).
  • Race, smoking, insurance do not modify top predictor effects (h8/h9/h10).
  • feature_099 positive dose-response, feature_063 negative dose-response (h14).
  • Demographics add no value after adjustment for clinical predictors (h16).
  • Best/worst profile risk stratification holds (h21).
  • Top binaries are pairwise uncorrelated (independent effects) (h24).
  • Model generalizes to held-out data (h25).

REFUTED OR NOT SUPPORTED:
  • Age has no association with response (h11 – null hypothesis confirmed).
  • Severity × biomarker score interaction not significant (h15).
  • feature_013 × feature_067 interaction not significant (h17).
  • feature_011 × feature_006 interaction not significant (h22).
  • A categorical (race/insurance/smoking/histology) directly drives response —
    not after adjustment (h3b).

OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
-------------------
This NSCLC cohort exhibits an interpretable but quantitatively modest set of
biomarker–outcome relationships:

1. Disease severity (feature_051) and disease burden (feature_011) are by
   far the strongest negative predictors of objective response, each acting
   in a clean dose-response fashion.
2. Two large-prevalence binaries (feature_013, feature_067) act as
   independent unfavorable predictors, each cutting response odds by ~25%.
3. Three uncorrelated binaries (feature_006, feature_007, feature_039)
   form a favorable panel: any one of them confers a small benefit, but
   patients with all three respond ~7 pp above baseline. Effects are
   super-additive on the log-odds scale.
4. Two laboratory markers (feature_099 favorable, feature_063 unfavorable)
   contribute small but reliable additional information.
5. Patient demographics (age, sex-equivalents, race, insurance) and smoking
   status do NOT contribute beyond the clinical/biomarker variables.
6. None of the strong predictors interact with histology, smoking, or race —
   their effects are uniform across these subgroups.
7. The signal is reproducible (held-out AUC ≈ training AUC ≈ 0.60) but
   modest — most response variation is unexplained by the available features,
   consistent with the inherent unpredictability of single-agent oncology
   outcomes at the individual-patient level.
