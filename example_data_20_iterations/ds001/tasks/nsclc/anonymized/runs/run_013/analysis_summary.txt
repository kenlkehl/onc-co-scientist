Oncology Dataset Analysis — ds001_nsclc
=========================================

Cohort
------
50,000 NSCLC patients with 123 anonymized features (`feature_001` … `feature_123`)
plus the binary outcome `objective_response`. Overall response rate: 16.89%
(8,447 / 50,000). Features include 77 binary indicators, 7 ordinal integers
(2–11 levels), 35 continuous floats, and 4 named categorical fields:
feature_057 (smoking: former / current / never), feature_043 (histology:
adenocarcinoma / squamous), feature_123 (race: white / hispanic / black /
asian / other), feature_005 (insurance: medicare / private / medicaid /
uninsured).

Analytic strategy
-----------------
Across 25 iterations I (i) screened every feature univariably against
`objective_response` (Welch t-tests for continuous variables, χ² tests for
binary/categorical/ordinal), (ii) built a single multivariable logistic
regression including the top univariable hits and the demographic
covariates, (iii) tested a focused set of pre-specified clinical
interactions (treatment marker × performance status, treatment marker ×
histology, treatment marker × smoking), (iv) examined demographic
heterogeneity (race, insurance), and (v) ran exploratory pairwise and
three-way interaction scans among the top binary features and continuous
biomarkers. Effect estimates are reported on the natural logistic-coefficient
scale or as response-rate differences; p-values are noted as raw and (for
the high-dimensional scans) flagged when they survive Bonferroni correction.

Main findings
-------------
1. **Performance-status-like ordinal feature_051 dominates.** Response rate
   declined monotonically from 21.08% at level 0 (n=17,557) to 15.70% at
   level 1 (n=24,962) to 11.05% at level 2 (n=7,481), χ² p ≈ 3 × 10⁻⁹³.
   In the multivariable model feature_051 had the largest |z| of any
   predictor (coef = −0.381, OR = 0.683, p = 1.1 × 10⁻⁹⁵). Hypothesis
   strongly **supported**.

2. **Tumor-burden-like count feature_011 (range 0–28) is inversely
   associated with response.** Univariate OR per unit = 0.962 (p = 1.8 ×
   10⁻³²); binned response rate fell from 19.1% (0–1) to 12.8% (≥11).
   Effect persisted in the multivariable model (OR 0.86 per SD, p < 10⁻³²).
   **Supported.**

3. **Three independent negative biomarker-like binaries (feature_013,
   feature_067, feature_063 continuous) are independently associated with
   lower response.** feature_013 (1 vs 0): 15.4% vs 19.6%, adj OR 0.74,
   p = 1.4 × 10⁻³⁴. feature_067: 14.4% vs 17.7%, adj OR 0.77, p = 4.5 ×
   10⁻¹⁹. Continuous feature_063: adj OR 0.96 per SD, p ≈ 5 × 10⁻⁴.
   All **supported**.

4. **Three positive binary biomarkers (feature_006, feature_007,
   feature_039) all individually predict higher response.** Adj ORs ≈
   1.08–1.14; all p < 0.005 in the multivariable model.

5. **Albumin-like continuous feature_099 (range 1.7–5.5) is positively
   associated with response.** Adj OR 1.05/SD, p ≈ 1 × 10⁻⁴. **Supported.**

6. **A combinatorial biomarker pattern is the clearest predictive signal.**
   feature_006, feature_007, and feature_039 show synergistic pairwise
   interactions (all p < 0.005) and a robust three-way positive
   interaction (coef = +0.391, p = 1.8 × 10⁻⁴): the triple-positive
   subgroup (n = 3,216) had a 22.98% response rate, 6–7 percentage points
   above the rate in any other (feature_006, feature_007, feature_039)
   combination cell, all of which clustered tightly between 16.1% and
   16.9%.

7. **A continuous biomarker feature_092 (range 0–0.798, ~10% mass at zero)
   shows U-shaped marginal behaviour with a clear high-expression
   responder subset.** Response by bin: 17.3% at zero, 16.4–16.6% in
   intermediate bins, 18.2% at ≥0.5. Dichotomization at 0.5 yields
   OR = 1.13 (p = 2.9 × 10⁻⁵). The pattern is consistent with what one
   would expect from a PD-L1-like tumor-proportion-score variable.

8. **feature_092 strongly modifies the effect of feature_006, feature_007
   and feature_039.** Continuous-by-binary interactions all have
   p ≤ 1.6 × 10⁻⁷ in the same positive direction. Concretely, when
   feature_092 < 0.5 the effect of feature_006 on response is small
   (OR 1.05, p = 0.14); when feature_092 ≥ 0.5 it is large (OR 1.47,
   p = 1.8 × 10⁻¹³). A composite biomarker-positive subgroup
   (feature_092 ≥ 0.5 AND feature_006 = 1, n = 3,150, 6.3% of the
   cohort) had a response rate of 22.5% vs 16.5% in everyone else
   (OR 1.47, p = 5.6 × 10⁻¹⁸). This is the cleanest predictive
   biomarker–treatment relationship in the data set.

Demographic and clinical effects
--------------------------------
9. **Age (feature_078) has no detectable independent effect** after
   adjustment (coef = 0.003 per SD, p = 0.78). **Refuted** as a meaningful
   prognostic variable in the adjusted model. Stratified by performance
   status, age slope on response was statistically null in every stratum.

10. **Histology (squamous vs adeno) was not independently predictive**
    after adjustment (adj OR 1.04, p = 0.15). Crude rate slightly higher in
    squamous (17.4% vs 16.7%). No histology-by-biomarker interactions
    survived testing.

11. **Smoking status was not independently predictive after adjustment.**
    Crude pattern matched the typical immunotherapy-era expectation
    (current 17.2% > former 16.9% > never 16.4%) but adjusted ORs were
    essentially 1.0. **Refuted** at the adjusted level.

12. **Race showed a small but real adjusted effect.** Crude response was
    highest for "other" (18.5%) and lowest for white (16.6%); after
    adjusting for the top seven biomarkers the only individually-significant
    contrast was black vs white (OR 1.08, p = 0.031), and the combined
    non-white-vs-white contrast was OR 1.07 (p = 0.011). Importantly,
    race did NOT modify the effect of treatment-marker feature_013 on
    response (LR test 4 df, p = 0.53) — i.e., the small adjusted race
    effect appears to be a main-effect difference, not heterogeneity in
    treatment benefit.

13. **Insurance showed a small adjusted effect favouring private vs
    medicaid** (OR 1.09, p = 0.019); medicare and uninsured were not
    individually significantly different from medicaid. The pattern did
    NOT show a clean socioeconomic gradient (uninsured ≈ medicare ≈
    private). Insurance × feature_013 interaction was non-significant
    (LR test 3 df, p = 0.55).

14. **Performance status (feature_051) did not modify any of the eight
    leading binary feature effects.** Smallest interaction p = 0.10
    (feature_076); all other p > 0.13.

15. **Exploratory binary-feature interaction scans surfaced two further
    suggestive findings.** feature_006 × feature_027 had a large negative
    interaction (coef = −0.266, p = 4.3 × 10⁻⁴, survives Bonferroni
    correction over the 77-feature scan); feature_013 × feature_034 had
    a moderate negative interaction (coef = −0.215, p = 0.0078, does NOT
    survive Bonferroni). The first warrants follow-up; the second is
    flagged as exploratory only.

Overall conclusions
-------------------
The cohort behaves as one would expect from a real-world NSCLC dataset
treated in the modern era of biomarker-driven systemic therapy:

• **Performance status** (feature_051) is by far the strongest single
  prognostic factor.
• **Tumor burden / extent of disease** (feature_011) is the second
  strongest negative prognostic factor.
• A small set of **binary indicators** (feature_013, feature_067 negative;
  feature_006, feature_007, feature_039 positive) exhibit independent
  associations with response that are stable in the multivariable model.
  feature_006, feature_007 and feature_039 act synergistically — the
  triple-positive subgroup (~6.4% of the cohort) has the highest response
  of any unconditioned cell.
• **One continuous biomarker (feature_092) has clean predictive value at
  the high end of its range and acts as an effect modifier for the
  feature_006/007/039 group**, consistent with a PD-L1-tumor-proportion-
  score-like variable being the dominant biomarker for these treatments.
• **Demographics are weakly informative.** Race shows a small adjusted
  main-effect advantage for non-white patients; private insurance shows a
  small advantage over medicaid. Crucially, neither variable modifies the
  effect of any treatment marker — i.e., there is no evidence in this
  cohort of differential treatment benefit by race or insurance.
• **Most of the variance in response remains unexplained.** Despite the
  large sample, the final 17-feature logistic model achieves only McFadden
  pseudo-R² = 0.020 and the best-defined biomarker-positive subgroup
  reaches a 22.5% response rate. Substantial residual heterogeneity is
  consistent with the well-known fact that available clinical/molecular
  features in real-world EHR-derived NSCLC cohorts capture only a fraction
  of inter-patient variability in objective response.

Hypotheses supported vs refuted (summary)
-----------------------------------------
Supported (p < 0.001 in adjusted analyses, direction matched):
  feature_051 ↓ response, feature_011 ↓, feature_013 ↓, feature_067 ↓,
  feature_063 ↓, feature_006 ↑, feature_007 ↑, feature_039 ↑, feature_099 ↑,
  feature_092 ≥0.5 ↑, feature_006×feature_039 synergistic ↑,
  three-way feature_006/007/039 ↑, feature_092 × feature_006/007/039 ↑,
  composite (feature_092 ≥0.5 ∧ feature_006=1) ↑,
  feature_006 × feature_027 antagonistic ↓.

Suggestive only (p < 0.05 but not Bonferroni-robust or small magnitude):
  race non-white ↑, insurance private ↑, feature_013 × feature_034 ↓,
  feature_076 ↑, feature_112 ↓.

Refuted in adjusted analysis:
  age (feature_078) main effect, histology (feature_043) main effect,
  smoking (feature_057) adjusted effect, race × feature_013 interaction,
  insurance × feature_013 interaction, performance-status × biomarker
  interactions for any of the eight top binary features.
