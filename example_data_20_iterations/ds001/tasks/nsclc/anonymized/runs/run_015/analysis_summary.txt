Analysis Summary — ds001_nsclc (n = 50,000; outcome: objective_response)
========================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
The cohort comprises 50,000 patients with 123 anonymized features and a binary
outcome objective_response (response rate 16.89%, 8447/50000). Features
breakdown: 4 categorical (smoking, histology, race, insurance), 7 ordinal
integers, 77 binary integers, and 35 continuous floats. There are no missing
values. Across 25 iterations I screened all features univariately, then built
an adjusted multivariable logistic regression and probed pairwise interactions
and stratified subgroups.

WHAT THE DATA SHOW
------------------

1. The dominant predictor is feature_051, a 3-level ordinal score (0/1/2 with
   proportions 35/50/15%). Response rate falls monotonically from 21.1% at
   level 0 → 15.7% at level 1 → 11.1% at level 2 (univariate logit coefficient
   −0.375 per level, p = 1.7e-93). Its size, ordinal structure, and direction
   are all consistent with a global severity / performance-status–like measure.

2. Two binary features carry strong negative effects:
   • feature_013 (prevalence 65%): OR 0.74, p = 1.3e-33; response 19.6% → 15.4%.
   • feature_067 (prevalence 25%): OR 0.78, p = 2.0e-18.
   Both retain near-univariate magnitude after adjusting for one another and
   for feature_051 — they convey largely independent information.

3. The strongest continuous negative predictor is feature_011 (right-skewed,
   range 0–28). Univariate logit coefficient −0.039 per unit (p = 1.8e-32); the
   binned response gradient is monotone (=0 → 19.3%, 0–1 → 18.3%, 1–3 → 17.0%,
   3–5 → 17.0%, 5–10 → 15.1%, >10 → 12.8%).

4. Two more continuous predictors reach Bonferroni-level significance:
   • feature_099 (range 1.7–5.5): coef +0.091/unit, p = 1.4e-4 (positive).
   • feature_063 (right-skewed, max 297): a log-transform fits better; on
     log(feature_063+1) the coef is −0.071, p = 1.8e-6.
   feature_092 (continuous, 0–0.8) is positively associated (coef +0.166,
   p = 3.2e-3) with a J-shaped pattern — the highest response (18.2%) is in
   the >0.5 stratum, suggesting a thresholded enrichment biomarker.

5. Two binary features show a positive synergistic interaction:
   feature_006 × feature_007 (LRT p = 0.0043, joint coefficient +0.156). Joint
   response rates: (0,0) 16.1%, (0,1) 16.7%, (1,0) 16.9%, (1,1) 19.7% — the
   double-positive cell exceeds the additive prediction. Both features also
   retain their main effects in the multivariable model.

6. Three additional binary predictors retain independent (modest) signals in
   the adjusted multivariable model: feature_039 (coef +0.076, p = 0.002),
   feature_076 (+0.099, p = 0.022), and feature_112 (−0.102, p = 0.027).

WHAT THE DATA DO NOT SHOW (HYPOTHESES THAT WERE REFUTED)
--------------------------------------------------------

• Treatment-by-severity heterogeneity. The negative effect of feature_013 does
  not differ significantly across feature_051 strata (LRT p = 0.116). ORs
  across levels 0/1/2 are 0.75 / 0.71 / 0.84 — directionally similar.

• Histology heterogeneity. All five top predictors (feature_013, feature_067,
  feature_006, feature_051, feature_011) have nearly identical coefficients in
  adenocarcinoma vs squamous. feature_013 × feature_043 LRT p = 0.40.

• Smoking-status heterogeneity for feature_011. Slopes are similar across
  current/former/never smokers (LRT p = 0.38).

• Race heterogeneity. feature_013 OR ranges 0.69–0.85 across the five race
  groups, with overlapping confidence intervals; race itself drops out (all
  group coefficients p > 0.13) once biomarkers and clinical predictors are
  included.

• Wide interaction screens. Pairwise interaction tests of feature_013 (76
  binary partners, ≤ 2 nominally significant) and feature_051 (76 partners,
  1 nominally significant) found fewer hits than chance. Likewise for
  feature_011 × any binary feature (1 hit out of 77). The data therefore look
  largely additive on the log-odds scale, with the single notable exception
  of feature_006 × feature_007.

• Age (feature_078, range 30–90, mean 65) is unrelated to response (p = 0.67).

• feature_064 (integer count 0–10) is unrelated to response (p = 0.78).

• Demographics (race, smoking, histology, insurance) are not independently
  associated with objective_response after adjustment for the
  biomarker/clinical predictors.

OVERALL CONCLUSIONS
-------------------
1. There is a small but highly statistically secure prognostic signal in this
   cohort. The likelihood-ratio test of the full model versus a null model
   gives p = 1.6e-185, but the pseudo-R² is only ~0.02 — i.e., the model
   correctly orders patients but cannot explain most of the variance in who
   does and does not respond.

2. The strongest signal is a global severity-like ordinal score (feature_051),
   followed by two binary "negative" markers (feature_013, feature_067) and a
   right-skewed continuous "tumor burden / disease activity"–like measure
   (feature_011). These four variables alone carry the majority of the
   predictable signal; adding the others contributes only marginal lift.

3. The data are remarkably free of subgroup or interaction effects. With one
   exception (feature_006 × feature_007), every interaction and stratified
   analysis I ran returned a null result, well below chance for the number of
   tests performed. Treatment-marker, biomarker-histology, and biomarker-race
   interactions all came back null. Effects therefore appear largely additive
   on the log-odds scale, and the cohort behaves as if it were a single
   relatively homogeneous population with respect to these features.

4. Demographics (race, smoking, histology, insurance, age) carry no
   independent association with objective_response once the clinical /
   biomarker variables are in the model. The marginal univariate signal for
   race (chi-square p = 0.045) is fully accounted for by other variables.

5. Several null findings are themselves informative for downstream work: age
   (feature_078) and the count-like feature_064 are unrelated to response and
   can be dropped from prognostic modeling in this cohort.
