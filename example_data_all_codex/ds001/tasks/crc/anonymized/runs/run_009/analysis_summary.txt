Dataset ds001_crc contains 50000 patients, opaque feature_* columns, and outcome pfs_months. I loaded dataset.parquet locally, screened all feature columns, fit a full OLS main-effects model, scanned all pairwise interactions, and then retested selected interactions while adjusting for all feature main effects.

The strongest adjusted main effect was `feature_041`: each one-unit increase was associated with +0.17584 months of `pfs_months` (p<1e-300). Several features had large adverse adjusted associations: `feature_025` was -1.17140 months per unit, `feature_036=1` was -1.36149 months, `feature_056` was -0.07606 months per unit, `feature_001=1` was -0.35437 months, `feature_015=1` was -0.28655 months, `feature_029` was -0.00501 months per unit, and `feature_066=1` was -0.35535 months. `feature_050` and `feature_043=1` were favorable, with adjusted effects of +0.46940 months per unit and +0.94295 months, respectively. `feature_028` had a statistically strong but very small negative coefficient.

Some marginal signals did not survive adjustment. `feature_042=1` had a positive marginal association, but in the full main-effects model its coefficient was only +0.02138 months with p=0.3503, so its main-effect hypothesis was not supported. The pairwise screen also suggested a `feature_064:feature_060` interaction, but after full adjustment it was not significant (interaction -0.03813 months, p=0.0624).

The most important heterogeneity centered on `feature_043`. Its favorable association was sharply reduced in several subgroups: the fully adjusted interaction with `feature_001` was -1.63231 months, with `feature_015` was -1.46275 months, with `feature_066` was -0.98816 months, and with continuous `feature_029` was -0.02580 months per unit. In simple subgroup means, `feature_043=1` was associated with much higher pfs_months when `feature_001=0` (5.773 vs 4.117 months), but little difference when `feature_001=1` (4.143 vs 4.116 months). A similar pattern appeared for `feature_015` and `feature_066`.

There were also positive interactions that refined the main-effect picture. `feature_042` was not an independent main-effect predictor, but `feature_042:feature_043` was strongly positive (+0.88849 months, p=1.296e-57), indicating that the `feature_042` signal was concentrated among `feature_043=1` patients. The adverse `feature_029` slope was attenuated among `feature_001=1` and `feature_015=1` patients, with interactions of +0.00802 and +0.00773 months per unit, respectively. `feature_015:feature_001` was also positive (+0.51254 months), meaning their combined adverse association was less negative than a simple additive model would predict.

Interactions involving the dominant continuous predictor `feature_041` were statistically robust but modest in magnitude. The positive `feature_041` slope was weaker at higher `feature_025` values (`feature_041:feature_025` -0.00732 months) and weaker among `feature_036=1` patients (`feature_041:feature_036` -0.00693 months). The `feature_025:feature_036` interaction was small and positive (+0.04419 months), again suggesting partial sub-additivity between two adverse predictors. A weak exploratory positive interaction was seen for `feature_023:feature_068` (+0.34324 months, p=0.0215), but it was much smaller and less secure than the dominant interaction structure.

Overall, `pfs_months` in this cohort is dominated by a strong favorable gradient with `feature_041`, strong adverse associations for `feature_025` and `feature_036`, and a favorable but highly subgroup-dependent association for `feature_043`. Because the features are de-identified, I did not assign clinical semantics to them, but the statistical pattern is consistent with substantial treatment-marker or biomarker-outcome heterogeneity concentrated around `feature_043`, `feature_001`, `feature_015`, `feature_029`, `feature_066`, and `feature_042`.
