Dataset ds001_breast contains 50,000 patients, 77 opaque feature columns, and the continuous outcome pfs_months. I evaluated univariable effects, a full all-main-effects OLS model, selected subgroup contrasts, pairwise interactions, and nonlinear quadratic terms. Effect estimates are signed in months of pfs_months.

The strongest main-effect signal was feature_044. In univariable standardized OLS, each one-SD increase in feature_044 was associated with +1.7509 months of pfs_months (p<1e-300), and the all-main-effects model retained essentially the same association (+1.7485 months per SD, p<1e-300). This strongly supported the hypothesis that higher feature_044 is associated with longer PFS. The ordinal feature_027 showed the opposite pattern: each one-SD increase was associated with -0.7997 months univariately and -0.7940 months after adjustment (both p<1e-300), supporting a robust adverse gradient.

Several binary or near-binary features had clear adjusted associations. feature_039=1 was adverse (-1.5363 months univariately; -0.7093 months per SD after full adjustment, p<1e-300). feature_046=1 was favorable (+1.0981 months univariately; +0.5247 months per SD adjusted, p<1e-300). Other independently supported adverse effects included feature_015 (-0.2971 months per SD), feature_037 (-0.2965), feature_031 (-0.2770), feature_062 (-0.2298), and feature_063 (-0.1813), all with very small p-values. Independently favorable effects included feature_069 (+0.2441) and feature_004 (+0.2383), also highly significant. A smaller adjusted adverse effect for feature_033 was statistically clear (-0.0419 months per SD, p=1.99e-16). Borderline adjusted effects for feature_005, feature_032, feature_017, and feature_059 were nominally significant but much weaker.

Some apparent univariable signals did not remain independent. feature_010 and feature_025 had favorable univariable associations, but their adjusted coefficients were small and non-significant in the full main-effects model. This refuted the interpretation that those columns had strong independent main effects; their univariable patterns were better explained by correlation with stronger features.

Interaction testing showed that heterogeneity was concentrated around feature_046. In all-main-effects-adjusted pairwise interaction screens, large feature_046 interactions appeared with feature_031 (-0.3778 months per SD-product), feature_069 (+0.3413), feature_062 (-0.3078), feature_063 (-0.2457), and feature_010 (+0.2354), all p<1e-300 in single-interaction models. In a joint model containing all main effects plus the top 20 interaction candidates, the feature_046 interactions with feature_031 (-0.3763), feature_069 (+0.3323), feature_062 (-0.3015), and feature_063 (-0.2455) remained strong, whereas the feature_010 by feature_046 term attenuated to +0.0092 months (p=0.0783). Thus, the feature_010 interaction was not independently supported after accounting for the dominant interaction structure.

Raw subgroup checks supported the same directions. The feature_046 favorable contrast was much smaller when feature_031=1, giving a 2x2 difference-in-differences of -1.623 months. The feature_046 contrast was amplified when feature_069=1, with a raw difference-in-differences of +1.5944 months. For feature_062, the adverse slope was much steeper among feature_046=1 patients (-0.6421 months per SD) than among feature_046=0 patients (-0.0049 months per SD), consistent with the negative feature_062 by feature_046 interaction. For feature_044, the positive slope was weaker when feature_039=1 (+1.6757 months per SD) than when feature_039=0 (+1.7867 months per SD), suggesting modest interaction between a strong favorable continuous feature and an adverse binary context.

Nonlinearity probes found statistically detectable but secondary curvature. feature_044 had a positive quadratic component (+0.0820 months for standardized z^2, p=5.94e-25), and feature_062 had a positive quadratic component (+0.1904 months, p=3.19e-32). These nonlinear terms were much smaller than the dominant linear and interaction effects, so they refine rather than replace the main conclusions.

Overall, the data show a strong multivariable outcome signal. The all-main-effects model explained substantial variation in pfs_months (R^2=0.792505), and adding the leading interaction terms increased R^2 to 0.878932. The most important pattern is not a single opaque feature alone but a feature_046-centered interaction structure: feature_046 is favorable overall, but that association is reduced in feature_031=1 and feature_063=1 contexts, amplified in feature_069=1 contexts, and coupled with a stronger adverse feature_062 slope. Because the feature names are de-identified, these results identify treatment-biomarker-outcome-like relationships statistically but cannot assign clinical labels to the opaque features without external metadata.
