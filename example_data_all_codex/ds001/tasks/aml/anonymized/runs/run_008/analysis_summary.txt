Dataset ds001_aml contained 50,000 patients and one binary outcome, objective_response, with an overall response rate of 18.75%. All analyses used only files in this task directory. Binary-feature effects are reported as absolute response-rate differences; continuous effects are one-standard-deviation risk differences; interaction effects are linear-probability interaction coefficients with HC1 robust standard errors. Very small p-values that underflowed numerically are recorded as 1e-300 in transcript.json.

The strongest main-effect signal was feature_027. Response fell monotonically from 23.10% at feature_027 level 0 to 17.43% at level 1 and 12.91% at level 2, a -5.22 percentage-point change per level (p<1e-300). Several binary features initially appeared favorable: feature_015 (+5.59 points), feature_025 (+6.74 points), and feature_023 (+5.35 points), all p<1e-300. Continuous feature_004 and feature_031 were adverse (-2.01 and -1.43 points per SD), while feature_059 was favorable (+0.90 points per SD). Smaller adverse effects were seen for feature_001, feature_032, feature_033, and feature_053.

Mutual adjustment preserved the main-effect directions for the leading variables: feature_027, feature_004, feature_031, feature_033, and feature_053 remained negative; feature_059 remained positive; and feature_001 and feature_032 remained adverse in the main-effect model. The largest refinement came from interaction analysis. Pairwise interactions among feature_015, feature_025, and feature_023 were highly positive, but counting the three features showed why: patients with 0, 1, or 2 of these features had response rates near 16-17%, while patients with all three had a 63.31% response rate. In a model including the three-way term, the pairwise terms were no longer significant and the three-way feature_015:feature_025:feature_023 interaction was very large (+46.24 to +46.47 points, p<1e-300). Thus the apparent favorable main effects of feature_015, feature_025, and feature_023 are best interpreted as markers of a specific all-three-positive high-response subgroup.

Additional heterogeneity appeared among adverse modifiers. feature_001 attenuated the positive-feature pattern, with negative interactions against feature_025 (-9.87 points), feature_015 (-6.31 points), and feature_023 (-5.19 points). feature_032 similarly attenuated feature_025 (-8.77 points) and feature_023 (-7.28 points). These interactions remained significant in the integrated model containing the three-way high-response subgroup.

Exploratory screens found smaller secondary interactions, including feature_040:feature_046 (+4.78 points), feature_071:feature_050 (-4.33 points), feature_034:feature_074 (-3.70 points), feature_073 by feature_034 (-1.28 points per SD), and feature_053 by feature_068 (-1.99 points per SD). These were nominally significant but much weaker than the feature_015/feature_025/feature_023 three-way pattern. Several plausible main effects were not supported: feature_047, feature_006, feature_005, feature_044, and feature_018 did not meet p<0.05.

Overall, the cohort contains a dominant multivariable response pattern: objective_response is much higher only in the joint feature_015=1, feature_025=1, feature_023=1 subgroup, while higher feature_027, feature_004, feature_031, feature_033, and feature_053 mark lower response. Because the feature labels are opaque, these results should be read as de-identified feature-outcome associations rather than named treatment or biomarker effects.
