Dataset ds001_aml contained 50,000 patients and no missing values. The outcome, objective_response, occurred in 9,375 patients (18.75%). Because feature names are opaque, I treated findings as dataset-specific associations rather than causal clinical claims. I tested binary features with response-rate contrasts, continuous or multi-level features with median and quartile contrasts, pairwise subgroup heterogeneity with difference-in-differences in response risk, and selected multivariable patterns with linear probability models so coefficients stayed on the natural response-probability scale.

The strongest main-effect signal was feature_027. Patients with feature_027 >= 1 had a response rate of 16.39% versus 23.10% for feature_027 = 0 (risk difference -6.71 percentage points, p < 1e-300). The relationship was ordered: response was 23.10% at level 0, 17.43% at level 1, and 12.91% at level 2.

Several other main effects were supported. feature_015 = 1, feature_025 = 1, and feature_023 = 1 each appeared beneficial in univariate tests, with response-rate differences of +5.59, +6.74, and +5.35 percentage points respectively. Higher feature_004, feature_031, feature_053, feature_033, and feature_032 = 1 were associated with lower response, while higher feature_059 was associated with higher response. In top-versus-bottom quartile checks, feature_004 and feature_031 showed larger negative contrasts (-4.74 and -4.01 percentage points), supporting monotonic or threshold-like lower-response patterns.

Some screened hypotheses were not supported. feature_005 = 1 had a positive but non-significant response difference (+1.56 points, p=0.056), feature_074 = 1 had a negative but non-significant difference (-1.15 points, p=0.069), and median-split feature_057 and feature_044 were also not significant.

The most important subgroup finding involved feature_015, feature_025, and feature_023. Pairwise interaction screens showed very large positive differences-in-differences: feature_015 by feature_025 (+19.26 points), feature_015 by feature_023 (+15.28 points), and feature_025 by feature_023 (+19.08 points), all with p < 1e-300. The eight-cell table clarified the pattern: patients with all three features equal to 1 had response 63.31% (1520/2401), while all seven other cells were tightly clustered around 15.7% to 17.2%. In an adjusted model containing all lower-order terms, the three-way feature_015:feature_025:feature_023 coefficient was +46.19 percentage points (p < 1e-300), and the pairwise terms were no longer significant. This indicates that the univariate positive effects of feature_015, feature_025, and feature_023 are largely concentrated in the triple-positive subgroup.

Two suppressive subgroup patterns also appeared for feature_025. Its benefit was strong when feature_001 = 0 (+8.19 points) but absent when feature_001 = 1 (-1.39 points), giving a -9.58 point interaction. Similarly, its benefit was present when feature_032 = 0 (+7.50 points) but absent when feature_032 = 1 (-0.05 points), giving a -7.55 point interaction.

Multivariable checks supported the independent negative associations of feature_027 >= 1, feature_004 >= 3, feature_031 >= 60, feature_001 = 1, feature_032 = 1, and feature_053 >= 12.2, plus an independent positive association for feature_059 >= 3.8. After adding the three-way interaction among feature_015, feature_025, and feature_023, isolated main effects for feature_015 and feature_023 were no longer significant, while the three-way term remained dominant.

Overall, the cohort appears to contain one very high-response interaction-defined subgroup, feature_015 = 1 / feature_025 = 1 / feature_023 = 1, on top of several lower-response risk markers led by feature_027, feature_004, feature_031, feature_001, feature_032, and feature_053. These findings should be interpreted as statistically strong associations in the anonymized feature space, not as causal treatment effects without additional design information.
