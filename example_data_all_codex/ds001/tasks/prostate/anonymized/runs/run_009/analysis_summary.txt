Dataset ds001_prostate contained 50000 patients and an overall objective_response rate of 24.0%. I treated objective_response as a binary outcome and used response-risk differences as the natural-scale effect estimate. Tests were two-proportion z tests for simple subgroups, delta-method risk-difference contrasts for interactions, and HC1 robust linear-probability models for adjusted analyses.

The strongest main-effect findings were binary. feature_005=1 was associated with much higher response (36.1% vs 15.9%, +20.2 percentage points, p=<1e-300). In contrast, feature_034=1 was associated with lower response (15.4% vs 34.6%, -19.2 percentage points, p=<1e-300). feature_009=1 and feature_065=1 each had about a -10.1 percentage point adverse difference, and the rarer feature_055=1 had a -6.6 percentage points difference. feature_067 and feature_025 were weak/null comparators and did not meet p<0.05.

Continuous and ordinal features added additional structure. Upper-quartile feature_003 (>=41.28) was strongly adverse (-11.7 percentage points, p=<1e-300), while lower-quartile feature_003 (<=6.46) was favorable (+10.4 percentage points, p=<1e-300). Upper-quartile feature_063 and feature_054 were also adverse, and higher feature_027 was modestly favorable. feature_023 showed a clear level gradient: level 2 had lower response than level 0 (18.2% vs 28.2%, -10.0 percentage points, p=<1e-300). In contrast, upper-quartile feature_039 and feature_040 level 10 versus 6 were not meaningfully associated with response.

The dominant interaction was feature_005 by feature_034. The feature_005 response advantage was +44.0 percentage points when feature_034=0 but only +0.8 percentage points when feature_034=1; the interaction contrast was -43.3 percentage points (p=<1e-300). This made feature_005=1 and feature_034=0 a high-response subgroup (61.0% vs 15.9% for all others, +45.1 percentage points).

Other binary features modified the favorable feature_005 pattern. The feature_005 association was smaller in feature_009=1 patients (interaction -24.3 percentage points), feature_065=1 patients (-21.8 percentage points), and feature_055=1 patients (-23.1 percentage points), all significant. The adverse effects of feature_009 and feature_065 were much smaller inside the feature_034=1 subgroup, suggesting overlapping low-response strata rather than simple additive penalties. The feature_065 by feature_009 interaction was positive (+12.6 percentage points), also consistent with nonadditive overlap.

feature_003 was the continuous feature most tied to heterogeneity. Upper-quartile feature_003 reduced response only modestly when feature_005=0 but sharply when feature_005=1; the interaction contrast was -22.4 percentage points (p=<1e-300). By contrast, the upper-quartile feature_003 by feature_034 interaction and the upper-quartile feature_063 by feature_005 interaction were not significant.

Composite strata sharpened the pattern. Any of feature_009, feature_065, or feature_055 identified a low-response group (-11.9 percentage points, p=<1e-300). Both feature_003>=41.28 and feature_063>=6.4 identified a very low-response group (11.9%, -12.9 percentage points), while feature_003<=6.46 with feature_063=0 identified a favorable group (35.2%, +12.0 percentage points). The highest-response subgroup found was feature_005=1, feature_034=0, and no feature_009/feature_065/feature_055 flags: 79.8% response versus 15.9% in all others (+63.8 percentage points, p=<1e-300).

Adjusted models supported the same conclusions. In a main-effects linear-probability model, feature_005 remained positive (+20.2 percentage points), feature_034 remained negative (-18.8 percentage points), feature_009, feature_065, and feature_055 remained negative, feature_003/feature_063/feature_054 remained adverse, and feature_027 remained favorable. feature_039 stayed null after adjustment. In the interaction model, feature_005:feature_034 (-43.1 percentage points) and feature_005:feature_009 (-24.2 percentage points) remained strongly negative, while standardized feature_003 remained independently adverse (-0.9 percentage points per SD).

Overall, the cohort appears dominated by a favorable feature_005 signal that is highly conditional on feature_034 and further weakened by feature_009, feature_065, feature_055, and high feature_003. Because all features are de-identified and this is observational EHR-derived data, these are association patterns rather than causal treatment effects.
