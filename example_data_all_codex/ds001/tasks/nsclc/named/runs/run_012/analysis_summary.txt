Analysis summary for ds001_nsclc

I analyzed 50,000 NSCLC records with progression-free survival in months (`pfs_months`) as the continuous outcome. Effect estimates are signed differences or regression coefficients in months of `pfs_months`. The main prognostic model adjusted for age, sex, smoking status, ECOG performance status, histology, stage IV, brain/liver/bone metastases, pleural effusion, albumin, LDH, and 6-month weight loss. Treatment-biomarker models added the relevant treatment, biomarker, and interaction term.

Baseline clinical factors showed the strongest and most consistent associations. Higher `age_years` was unexpectedly associated with longer PFS (+0.16871 months per year, p<1e-300). Poorer `ecog_ps` was strongly adverse (-1.10270 months per point, p<1e-300), as were `stage_iv=1` (-1.41821 months, p<1e-300), `has_brain_mets=1` (-0.91651 months, p<1e-300), squamous histology versus adenocarcinoma (-0.71890 months, p<1e-300), current smoking versus never smoking (-0.39023 months, p=7.80e-240), and `sex_female=1` (-0.20494 months, p=1.39e-178). Former smoking was associated with slightly longer PFS than never smoking (+0.09307 months, p=1.26e-18).

Laboratory and wasting markers behaved mostly as expected. Higher albumin was favorable (+0.45428 months per g/dL, p<1e-300), while higher LDH (-0.00041 months per U/L, p=1.29e-20) and greater 6-month weight loss (-0.07294 months per percentage point, p<1e-300) were adverse. In contrast, liver metastases, bone metastases, and pleural effusion did not add meaningful independent PFS signal once stage, brain metastases, and the other baseline factors were included.

The clearest treatment-biomarker result was for sotorasib and KRAS G12C. A treatment main-effects model showed longer adjusted PFS with `treatment_sotorasib=1` (+0.32405 months, p<1e-300), but the interaction model localized this to `kras_g12c=1`: the `treatment_sotorasib:kras_g12c` interaction was +2.53491 months (p<1e-300), while the sotorasib coefficient among `kras_g12c=0` patients was essentially zero (-0.00068 months, p=0.911). This supports a strong KRAS-specific sotorasib association rather than a broad treatment effect.

Other targeted-treatment hypotheses were not supported. The `treatment_osimertinib:egfr_mutation` interaction was -0.01664 months (p=0.472), and the `treatment_olaparib:brca2_mutation` interaction was -0.00449 months (p=0.923). `brca2_mutation` itself was independently adverse in the biomarker model (-0.11120 months, p=9.54e-9), but there was no evidence that olaparib offset this in BRCA2-mutated patients.

Immunotherapy-marker hypotheses were also refuted. Pembrolizumab benefit was not larger for high PD-L1 (`pdl1_tps>=0.5` interaction +0.00485 months, p=0.781), for `tmb_high=1` (interaction -0.01378 months, p=0.384), or for `stk11_mutation=0` versus `stk11_mutation=1` (interaction -0.01307 months, p=0.515). Continuous `pdl1_tps` was not independently associated with PFS (+0.01261 months per 1.0 increase, p=0.422), and `tmb_high` was not independently associated either (-0.00633 months, p=0.408).

Among residual oncogenic biomarkers, `stk11_mutation` had a small adverse independent association (-0.01998 months, p=0.0323). `alk_fusion` was directionally adverse but narrowly non-significant (-0.03011 months, p=0.0531), and `egfr_mutation` had no independent association (+0.00101 months, p=0.928) after adjustment.

Overall, the dominant signals in this cohort were baseline prognosis, an unexpectedly favorable age association, and a highly specific KRAS G12C-sotorasib interaction. I did not find evidence for pembrolizumab enrichment by PD-L1, TMB, or STK11 status, nor for osimertinib-EGFR or olaparib-BRCA2 interaction effects on `pfs_months` in these data. These are association analyses on the available EHR-derived cohort and should not be read as causal treatment-effect estimates without design assumptions about treatment assignment and censoring.
